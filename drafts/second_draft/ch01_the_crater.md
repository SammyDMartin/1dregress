## 1

'What a mess,' I said, to no-one in particular.

I looked down at the twisted piece of metal in my hands, letting the weight of it bend my wrist. I couldn't even tell what part of the base it had originally come from. Twenty-five years in stasis, only for the mission to fall apart within months, leaving more questions than it had answered.

'Is that the official verdict of your team, Colonel Hansun?' asked Aumonier wryly, using my acting rank.

The Admiral's holographic presence squinted at me.

'No sir, sorry, I just...' I glanced back towards the crater rim. My tremor was back, returning as it often did in moments of stress. I clamped my hand close to my leg, doing my best to still it. 'I've never seen anything like this. I don't think any of us have.'

Aumonier's projection stayed patiently silent while I tried to gather my thoughts.

The crater was all that remained of the base, a cluster of extruded concrete shelters and prefabricated structures reduced to their constituent molecules. Last manifest had put it at almost two hundred personnel. Accounting for the bodies would be impossible. Nothing recognisable remained.

The entire base had somehow been transmuted into a thin slurry at the base of the crater, membrane-thick and almost perfectly lenticular, as if the entire complex — silicon, metal and carbon — had suddenly turned to liquid and flowed together. It looked like a poorly prepared microscope slide. Scattered around the crater were warped and twisted fragments of the structures that had been ejected, as if the base had simultaneously imploded and exploded. I held one such fragment.

'I'm no engineer, but none of this makes sense to me,' I said. 'The implied energy of whatever did this...' I gestured helplessly.

'It makes perfect sense,' said Aumonier. 'If you accept the premise. Something opened up a region of very high gravity — hundreds of Gs at least, lasting perhaps a minute — directly on top of the base. Everything collapsed inward. When the field cut off, the compressed matter exploded outward.' He let that sit. 'Our sister ship in orbit suffered the same fate, just days before.'

The star's trying to kill us.

I looked up at the blue-black Kailash sky and felt exposed, as though caught in the gaze of a malevolent eye. The Resolute was just visible in geostationary, keeping watch — for all the good it would do against something capable of this.

Run. I was seized by the impulse to sprint for the dropship and flee. If the annihilation came again, it would be fast. I wouldn't even notice my death.

'We spotted strange emissions from the star immediately before both events,' Aumonier continued. 'Massive coronal disruptions, synchronised with each attack. Too much of a coincidence. Whatever did this, it draws power from the star itself.'

'Belligerence?' I asked. 'Or an accident? Someone messing with alien technology and breaking a few laws of physics?'

Aumonier's expression sharpened. 'This is not an accident, Hansun. There is agency behind this. And whatever it is, it's beyond anything we've encountered — the Mimics can barely work metal, let alone the fabric of spacetime.' He leaned toward the camera. 'Which means we have undisputed access to something genuinely new. Those godforsaken Arco goons can't fly rings around us if we discover an authentic gravitonic drive. Imagine if we'd had something like that at Threshold.'

There it was. Two hundred people turned to slurry, and the Admiral was already calculating the strategic advantage.

'Sir, I would recommend caution. Just because we can see it doesn't mean we can duplicate it.'

'A culture with superior technology, perhaps extant,' Aumonier said, his projection looking past the camera at something far away and indescribable. 'Every species we've encountered so far has been inferior. The Dyn, the Tanu. What could we learn, I wonder?'

This is a mistake, I decided.

'I understand, sir. We should still aim to minimise our time on the surface.'

'Yes, of course...'

I turned and noticed the Admiral wasn't listening, eyes darting elsewhere, consulting a display. I waited. A pair of silver drones spun in midair and darted upwards, away from the crater. Something had them agitated.

A priority alert flashed in my peripheral vision.

Aumonier broke contact with the invisible screen and turned to me, face suddenly solemn.

'I'm afraid your survey will have to be cut short, Hansun. I've called a flyer down. You will gather a legionnaire squad, maximum readiness, unsafe everything. Are my orders understood?'

'What's going on?'

When I saw the image I couldn't avoid yelling out of shock. There was a suited human figure in the frame, jumping up and down and waving its arms in the air in grainy enhanced colour. Then I saw the location data.

'They're almost fifteen hundred kilometres from—'

'No. I don't want to hear about it, not yet. Get over there and recover them, if at all possible. You have your orders.'

---

Hansun's career had taken a storied path since his near-death at the hands of Arco three decades ago. Seconded to fleet intelligence after the discovery, given the rank of Colonel, transferred to the Kailash expedition. He'd been submerged in coldsleep while the decades passed, thawed out and seen new worlds under alien skies and done his best to handle the complex logistics of establishing a forward base on an alien world. Then, in a moment, all their work had been wiped out, apparently by the boot of an angry god.

And somehow, I'm the point man once again.

Clearly, Aumonier had seen something in him. What it was, Hansun didn't quite understand. His staying power, perhaps. His talent for being the last man standing when everything went wrong.

Perhaps it's my fate to be brought in to salvage fuckups, he thought, as the flyer reached the top of its ballistic arc and began to descend. Perhaps the survivor would shed some light on what had happened.

But that just leads to further questions — how did they make it so far alone?

Somehow Hansun knew that this wasn't going to unravel neatly.

The legionnaires around him sat stiffly in their heavy armour as the air brakes deployed and the thrusters kicked in, the thin atmosphere of Kailash turning from blue-black to merely deep blue. There was a thud as the flyer made contact with the ground, another as the ramp descended, and the squad filed out after Hansun.

They were near the equator, on a relatively high plateau — piles of erratic boulders littered the landscape, relics of ancient glacial flows. The grey dirt crunched beneath Hansun's feet as he advanced, the legionnaires' targeting lasers skating over the low hills.

He could see the survivor just ahead, sitting cross-legged on the dirt.

It's small. A child?

'Point those somewhere else, I don't want anyone getting tagged,' Hansun snapped. The heavy ballistic rifles shifted, targeting beams aimed down.

The survivor was quite close now, facing away from them. Thin, maybe starved.

Hansun raised his hand and the squad halted.

'For god's sake, we're not here to arrest them. Hold position, linkup with my suit cam. I'll go on ahead.'

He turned on his suit speakers as he walked forward. 'We're from the expedition. Are you alright?'

The figure turned. Hansun realised with a start that she was just a girl, about eighteen, skinny, with a narrow face and unkempt dark hair, wearing one of the standard-issue exo-environment suits and a transparent mask. It was patched and stitched in places, covered in grime and dirt.

Facial recognition kicked in a couple of seconds later — Eszel, family of the former base's civilian crew, reported missing five weeks ago.

Five weeks?

Hansun tried to give a reassuring smile, aware of how intimidating he must appear, backed by the line of towering legionnaires. He pointed his gun at the ground.

'You're safe now,' he said.

The girl met his eyes. Her face was blank, but the blankness wasn't shock, not exactly — it was something more considered. As if she were looking at him from a very long way away and deciding what to do about what she saw. The moment stretched, and Hansun felt an unease he couldn't quite name.

'You're safe,' he repeated.

The girl burst into tears — a beat too late, though Hansun didn't let himself think about that until much later.

'I thought I was dead,' she sniffed, voice muffled by her suit respirator. 'I didn't think I'd ever...' she trailed off.

'It's alright,' said Hansun, motioning for the legionnaires to move forward. 'We're evacuating the planet, you need to come with us.'

Eszel hesitated for a moment, casting an apprehensive eye at Hansun. Then she smiled and reached out to take his hand.

'Can you walk to the lander?' he asked, trying to soften his voice. The girl nodded.

They didn't stay in orbit a second longer than necessary. In the end, a small surface camera was the only thing left to mark the fate of the base's wreckage. Perfectly controlled lines of plasma arrowed down from the sky, glowing even in the thin local air. Light bloomed and spread, shock waves shredding the wreckage of the base and the human detritus left behind by the recovery crews.

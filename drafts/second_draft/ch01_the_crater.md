There had been such hope at first.

The sense of anticipation that surrounded the discovery of the second interstice is hard to overstate. For someone used to the grinding, glacial bureaucracy of Union, the inevitable manned mission to the wormhole in that distant system came together with surprising speed — from prototyping and planning, to assembly, training and departure in just twenty-eight frantic months.

Aumonier had been right — the discovery was a coup for us, in more ways than one. Even if it was only for a brief few years, the rising tides of recrimination, factionalism and militarism fanned by the Threshold Crisis subsided as we rediscovered our common purpose.

I'm not naive. I knew that there were baser motives at play. You don't mobilise the vast resources required for interstellar travel purely out of a desire to seek out new frontiers or ennoble the human spirit.

Of course, I volunteered at the first opportunity — Aumonier had clearly expected it of me. But in my own way, I too came to believe in the mission.

So it was that I found myself on one of the twin vessels bound for the second interstice, almost twenty-six years ago.

We didn't wait to retrieve more information from the vanguard probes. Even with our bleeding-edge drives it was deemed there was no time to waste on that faltering conversation with its decade-long pauses — besides, we could catch up en route. Instead, we plunged into the void with only tantalising precedent and our collective imaginations to suggest what we might find at our destination. Perhaps first contact with an alien civilisation? Or the reliquary of one long since extinct?

What we found was far more ambiguous.

We emerged from the interstice to find ourselves, as promised, in the trailing Lagrange point of one of the system's handful of planets, a world we came to call Kailash. Older by far than Humanity's home, it had nevertheless narrowly failed to develop as an abundant cradle of life.

After billions of years as a cold, arid, Arean world, home only to prokaryotes, its sun's expansion had driven a surge in biocomplexity, the increased irradiance releasing life from its energetic constraints.

Kailash's thin atmosphere, radiation-scoured surface, sluggish geology and retreating seas meant that most complex organisms were confined to a maze of karst landscapes and sinkholes, like moss growing between the cracks of a badly-kept pavement. Life was forced to conserve, to cooperate, and in the end it learned to endure.

Yet somehow this only served to exaggerate the inescapable feeling of a world that was dying. Even the skies were a graveyard of red, a view from the fringes of a sterile elliptical galaxy. And while every interstice yet discovered led somewhere new and unknowably distant — galaxy cores, halo stars, the centres of globular clusters — to my mind no connection yet visited by human explorers was quite so frightening.

From almost the first instant of our arrival, I felt as if we were trespassing somewhere forbidden.

Still, we pressed on.

Now, in a geostationary orbit above Kailash, the Invincible hangs like a fixed star, watchful and wary. Of its sister vessel almost nothing remains but a thin cloud of wreckage. Dropships rise and dock with the Resolute at regular intervals, ferrying soldiers and material from the planet below, from the vicinity of a small and very recently formed crater north of the equator.

Our mission has failed.

I am on the last dropship off Kailash. We depart in a couple of hours and the Resolute will leave this world behind not long after that, to begin the long journey home.

Kailash is not a world that welcomes our presence.

## 1

'What a mess,' I said, to no-one in particular.

I looked down at the twisted piece of metal in my hands, letting the weight of it bend my wrist.

Twenty-five years in stasis, only for the mission to fall apart within months, leaving more questions than it had answered.

I couldn't even tell what part of the base the twisted metal had originally come from. In some ways I wanted to reject what I was seeing, but I knew it was useless. You have to deal with the reality in front of you.

'Is that the official verdict of your team, Colonel Hansun?' asked Aumonier wryly, using my acting rank.

The Admiral's holographic presence squinted at me.

'No sir, sorry, I just... I've never seen anything like this,' I finished lamely. 'I don't think any of us have.'

I glanced back towards the crater rim. My tremor was back, returning as it often did in moments of stress.

Is he testing me?

I clamped my hand close to my leg, doing my best to still it.

Aumonier's projection stayed patiently silent while I tried to gather my thoughts.

The crater was all that remained of the base, a cluster of extruded concrete shelters and prefabricated structures reduced to their constituent molecules.

I'd visited briefly whilst it was still being assembled, before returning to the Resolute, continuing our survey of the rest of the system.

Last manifest had put the base at almost two hundred personnel, though accounting for all the bodies would be impossible.

Nothing recognisable remained.

The entire base had somehow been transmuted into a thin slurry at the base of the crater, membrane-thick and almost perfectly lenticular, as if the entire complex, silicon, metal and carbon, had suddenly turned to liquid and flowed together. It looked like a poorly prepared microscope slide. Scattered around the crater were warped and twisted fragments of the structures that had been ejected, as if the base had simultaneously imploded and exploded. I held one such fragment.

'I'm no engineer, but none of this makes sense to me... the implied energy of whatever did this...' I gestured helplessly.

Another dropship came down behind us, settling onto the landing pad on pillars of flame, kicking up plumes of grey dust. Above, up in geostationary beyond the blue-black Kailash sky, the light of the Resolute was just visible, keeping watch — for all the good it would do against something capable of this. After all, its sister vessel had been dispatched seemingly in a moment.

Run. I was seized by the impulse to sprint for the dropship and flee. If the mysterious annihilation came again, it would be fast. I wouldn't even notice my death.

'So, your initial assessment, Hansun?' prompted Aumonier, once the roar from the dropship had subsided. I just shook my head.

Aumonier nodded sympathetically.

'Well, the lab's analysed the samples your team collected, and it looks like what we would expect, chemically; mostly carbon allotropes, silicates, traces of biological matter. This was the base, beyond any doubt.'

'Then what happened?'

'Best we've come up with so far is that a massive blunt object descended out of orbit and flattened it.'

'Is that a joke, sir?'

'I'm not known for my sense of humour.' Aumonier let that statement hang in the air for a moment before continuing.

'Some of the physicists ran simulations, and most of the effects we've seen could have resulted from the creation of a small region of very high gravity; hundreds of Gs at least, lasting perhaps a minute.'

'I see...' I turned back to the wreckage, trying to imagine the scene. The base must have explosively relaxed when the gravity returned to normal. That would explain the deep crater and scattered debris.

'Practical gravity manipulation,' I mused. 'Evidently not a natural disaster. So what's the culprit? A failed experiment, perhaps? Someone messing with alien technology broke a few laws of physics?'

'The Mimics can, perhaps, achieve the energy densities required to work metal,' chided Aumonier. 'Working the fabric of space-time could be considered a significant step up for them.'

'Of course, sir.'

Still, the question remained... if the technology wasn't the Mimics', then whose?

'That, and it seems the same fate befell the Resolute just days before,' continued Aumonier, as though anticipating my next question. 'We'd expect such a dramatic discovery to show up in their records. The base gave no indication of understanding what might have happened to the Resolute either. In fact, reports dried up in the days leading up to the disaster. But I suspect you're right about something. There was intent behind this. Agency. And if not the Mimics themselves, then certainly a party unknown to us, with technology we don't understand.'

'Belligerence? Do we have any theory as to how this might have been done?'

'We spotted strange emissions from the sun immediately before both events. Abnormally large coronal mass ejections. We didn't make the connection until the second event — too much of a coincidence.'

The star's trying to kill us.

I suddenly felt exposed, as though caught in the gaze of a malevolent eye, burning brightly overhead.

We're so far out of our depth. Why are we still here?

I held my tongue.

'We've thrown a few ideas around; they might just have something really dense down there, monopoles or collapsed-state matter riding something like a vactrain line under the planet. I wouldn't bet on it though; we're probably looking at post-desert physics.'

HDF legionnaires in bulky vacuum armour milled around the perimeter, shoulder-mounted burnpulsers unsafed and tracking for enemies that would never arrive, META rifles raised menacingly. Closer in, a wing of drones flew over the disaster site, low and fast, shimmering like a school of fish.

And there's the kicker. The logic of it all unfolded, and I glanced out at the ring of legionnaires and drones. There was one reason why they had risked a horrible death to come to this disaster site.

'And we have undisputed access to whatever produced this.'

Aumonier nodded approvingly.

'So you understand. We're looking at a genuinely new piece of tech, something totally alien and therefore not reproducible by anyone. And I do mean anyone. Those godforsaken Arco goons can't fly rings around us if we discover an authentic gravitonic drive. Imagine if we'd had something like that at Threshold.'

'Yes, sir.'

'You doubt that this is worth it?' his tone sharpened. 'I sent you down to bring a fresh perspective. I hope my trust wasn't misplaced.'

I looked at the collapsed pool of shredded matter, and tried to marshal a response.

'Sir, I would recommend caution. Just because we can see it doesn't mean we can duplicate it.'

'A culture with superior technology, perhaps extant,' Aumonier said, his projection looking behind the camera at something far away and indescribable. 'Humanity — our Union — has been fortunate in one way. Every species we've encountered so far has been inferior, unsophisticated — the Dyn, the Tanu. What could we learn, I wonder?'

This is a mistake, I decided.

'I understand, sir. We should still aim to minimise our time on the surface.'

I should minimise my time down here. You're safe in orbit. Safer, anyway.

'Yes, of course...' Aumonier trailed off.

I turned around and noticed the Admiral wasn't listening, eyes darting elsewhere, consulting a display. I waited, raising my gaze to the inky sky. A pair of silver drones spun in midair and darted upwards, away from the crater. Something had them agitated.

The Admiral broke contact with the invisible screen and turned to me, face suddenly solemn.

'I'm afraid your survey will have to be cut short, Hansun.'

A priority alert flashed up in my peripheral vision.

'I've called a flyer down to our location. You will need to board it and gather a legionnaire squad. Maximum readiness, unsafe everything. They'll explain on the way. Are my orders understood?'

'What's going on?'

When I saw the image I couldn't avoid yelling out of shock. There was a suited human figure in the frame, jumping up and down and waving its arms in the air in grainy enhanced colour. Then I saw the location data.

'They're almost fifteen hundred kilometres from—' I began.

'No. I don't want to hear about it, not just yet. Get over there and recover them, if at all possible. You have your orders.'

Hansun's career had taken a storied path since his near-death at the hands of Arco three decades ago. He'd been seconded to fleet intelligence after the discovery, given the rank of Colonel and transferred to the Kailash expedition.

He'd been submerged in coldsleep while the decades passed, thawed out and seen new worlds under alien skies and done his best to handle the complex logistics of establishing a forward base on an alien world. Then, in a moment, all their work had been wiped out, apparently by the boot of an angry god.

And somehow, I'm the point man once again.

Clearly, Aumonier had seen something in him. What it was, Hansun didn't quite understand. His staying power?

So here he was — trillions of kilometres from home as the distance could be conventionally measured, vastly further in reality, riding an ageing flyer on a suborbital hop across an alien world he barely understood, trying to salvage something from this disaster of a mission.

What could do something like that?

Hansun couldn't get the image of the liquefied base out of his mind — a view from a nightmare or a horror drama.

Perhaps it's my fate to be brought in to salvage fuckups, Hansun thought to himself as the flyer reached the top of its ballistic arc and began to descend. Perhaps the survivor would shed some light on what had happened.

But that just leads to further questions — how did they make it so far alone?

Somehow Hansun knew that this wasn't all going to unravel neatly.

And all the while, the invisible death waited. If Aumonier had been right, and the hidden machinery could play with the inner structure of space-time, manipulating gravity with ease and finesse, he wasn't safe even now. The Resolute in orbit wasn't safe either.

Would there be a sign? Would I feel my body being torn apart before the end?

The legionnaires around him sat stiffly in their heavy armour as their air brakes deployed and the thrusters kicked in, the thin atmosphere of Kailash turning from blue-black to merely deep blue — even at ground level the air was too thin to breathe without assistance. There was a thud as the flyer made contact with the ground, another as the ramp descended, and the squad filed out after Hansun.

They were near the equator, on a relatively high plateau — piles of erratic boulders littered the landscape, relics of ancient glacial flows. The grey dirt crunched beneath Hansun's feet as he advanced, the legionnaires' targeting lasers skating over the landscape of low hills.

Hansun clutched his own pistol, holding it low — just in case. There were enough unknowns here that he wouldn't go in without precautions.

He could see the survivor just ahead, sitting cross-legged on the dirt.

It's small. A child?

'Point those somewhere else, I don't want anyone getting tagged,' Hansun snapped. The heavy ballistic rifles shifted, targeting beams aimed down.

The survivor was quite close now, facing away from them. He could already tell they were thin, maybe starved.

Hansun raised his hand and the squad halted.

'For god's sake, we're not here to arrest them. Hold position, linkup with my suit cam. I'll go on ahead.'

Hansun turned on his suit speakers as he walked forward. 'We're from the expedition,' his voice echoed. 'Are you alright?'

The figure turned towards him. Hansun realized with a start that she was just a girl, about eighteen, skinny, with a narrow face and unkempt dark hair, wearing one of the standard-issue exo-environment suits and a transparent mask. It was patched and stitched up in places, covered in grime and dirt.

A couple of seconds later facial recognition kicked in — Eszel, family of the former base's civilian crew, reported missing five weeks ago.

Five weeks?

Hansun tried to give a reassuring smile, aware of how intimidating he must appear, backed by the line of towering legionnaires. He pointed his gun at the ground.

'You're safe now,' he said.

The girl met his eyes, face blank. The moment seemed to stretch, tension building in a way that Hansun couldn't quite identify. She didn't move, didn't say anything.

'You're safe,' he repeated.

The girl burst into tears.

'I thought I was dead,' she sniffed, voice muffled by her suit respirator. 'I didn't think I'd ever...' she trailed off.

'It's alright,' said Hansun, motioning for the legionnaires to move forward. 'We're evacuating the planet, you need to come with us.'

Eszel hesitated for a moment, casting an apprehensive eye at Hansun. Then she smiled and reached out to take Hansun's hand.

'Can you walk to the lander?' he asked, trying to soften his voice. The girl nodded.

They didn't stay in orbit a second longer than necessary. In the end, a small surface camera was the only thing left to mark the fate of the base's wreckage. Perfectly controlled lines of plasma arrowed down from the sky, glowing even in the thin local air. Light bloomed and spread, shock waves shredding the wreckage of the base and the human detritus left behind by the recovery crews.

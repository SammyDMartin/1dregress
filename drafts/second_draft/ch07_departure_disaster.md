## 4

'Tell me how you came to leave the base, Eszel. Do you remember that?'

'Yes, of course.'

Hansun waited, his affect conveying patient anticipation.

'It was three days after the storm.'

'Do you remember the purpose of your excursion?' he pressed.

'Yes. I went to retrieve a drone that dropped out of the network.'

'Alone?'

Something about his interrogative tone suggested I should respond otherwise.

'No, there were two of us, me and Lao,' I lied easily. Had I ever been alone?

'What time of day was it?'

That question followed quickly — did he suspect?

'It was getting dark. Dusk. We took a Mule out with us, to carry everything. Tools for if repairs were possible, a replacement drone if not. They provide recon, forecasts, network coverage… we spotted the outage — probably just some glitch caused by the storm—'

'You took supplies?' Hansun interrupted.

'Yes. Precaution. Provisions for a week.' Hansun looked briefly concerned, before that not-quite-perfect mask of dispassionate interest slid back down. 'What's this really about?'

Hansun spread his palms, leaning back in his chair in a gesture of open invitation.

'We just want to get to the truth. Just tell me what you can remember.'

---

But there was no point in telling him the truth, helping him establish facts, compile a timeline. What purpose would it serve? What did the truth matter when he could never understand it?

The truth is I could not have told him why I left the base on the third day after the storm. I didn't know myself. Why did I hang over that precipice when I was young? Why did I go to such lengths to stare out into that rushing void between the stars? Why did anyone do anything?

I left the base because I wanted to. Because I desired solitude, a respite from the constant crushing pressure of being hemmed in with all those other people. Because I was curious. Because there was nothing left for me here. Because something called me to do so and I chose to heed it.

I found myself suddenly awake after yet another bout of fitful sleep, of seamlessly drifting between waking dream and fevered nightmare. I lay there for a moment in the crushing darkness of the simulated diurnal cycle, inhaled deeply and found myself resolved. There was little more to it than that.

I thumbed the room's lights on, blinking as they came up to match the level in the corridor beyond, dimmed though it was in a concession to our circadian rhythm. I crossed to the locker and dressed quickly in my environment suit and a heavy cape designed to provide some degree of further protection from the elements and the ambient radiation. Then I slung the helmet from a strap over my shoulder and without further hesitation, using the handover to the next shift as cover, I made my way to the nearest airlock, pausing only to grab all the provisions I could carry — power packs, a bubble tent, an aquifier, enough rations for two weeks. I was careful to disable any comms. I projected my usual assurance as I slipped from the base — nobody challenged me, nobody asked where I was going with so many supplies.

I secured my helmet and the airlock began to cycle, muffled sounds only growing quieter as the pressure differential equalised. As the incessant background noise of the base dropped away, an almost meditative sense of calm settled over me. Then I was out under the open sky. The heavy door thudded shut behind me, the sound dampened by the thin atmosphere.

Outside, beyond the confines of the base's artificially illuminated rat run, the ghostly blue twilight of the long Kailash dusk was dwindling to darkness, and the bloody-red disk of the eclipsed moon had risen above the opposite horizon. Nobody else even bothered to look up from their work as I strapped my provisions to an idle Mule, too preoccupied with repairing the damaged base — testing the patched mesh, replacing burnt-out sensors, checking the radiation shielding was sound — hurriedly rushing to finish the last of the day's work before the protracted night. I set off with a brisk stride, the Mule, keyed to follow, loping behind. Though some semblance of order had been restored, the expedition was still reeling from the loss of the Resolute. It would be many hours before someone noted my absence and by the time they committed to a search I would be long gone.

No more than thirty minutes after I'd awoken, I'd crossed the outer perimeter.

As I crested one of the low hills overlooking the base, I turned back one last time. I sensed a finality in that moment, something that told me that it was not just the base that I was leaving behind, yet I felt no apprehension. I took it all in — the sprawl of scattered modules, sheltered in that shallow depression, now visible only by their pinprick lights, like some paltry imitation of the stars overhead. A fallen constellation lying tarnished in the dirt. Our mere presence had sullied this place, Kailash, this perfect and timeless wilderness. Our ancestors had never set foot on its namesake, in deference to its sacredness. But modernity, delusional in its faith in its own objectivity, showed no such restraint. Already we sought to bend this discovery to our designs, to find a place for it in our schemes, discarding anything that didn't fit.

Then as I descended the other flank of the hill the base dipped out of view and with that it was as though I was truly alone. I picked my way carefully down the hillside, using only the moon and that fading twilight to illuminate my way.

An hour or so into my journey I came to a small stream, not yet frozen over. I followed its course as far as I could, in an attempt to confuse any potential pursuers. The temperature plummeted as the night drew on, but my environmental suit kept me insulated. I stopped only when I could walk no farther, my feet sore, my legs aching. I unpacked the tent in as sheltered a spot as I could find, watching as the orange smart fabric inflated, lit from within by a quantum dot skin. I clambered inside the airtight blister, the membrane parting around me and once the air had warmed inside, at last removed my helmet. The ancillary machinery supporting this tiny microcosm hummed softly outside. I lay back, enwombed, and changed the display from its default warming light to a view of the sky above, a vista of watchful stars.

---

I felt it first in my teeth.

A vibration, subsonic, rising through the ground and into my bones. The Mule stopped walking. Its stabilisers whined, recalibrated, whined again. I had never heard it do that before.

I turned around.

The base was behind me, its lights a faint scatter on the horizon, barely distinguishable from stars. For a moment everything was ordinary. Then the lights went out. Not flickering, not failing — removed. As if someone had edited them from the landscape.

The air changed. I don't know how else to describe it. The pressure shifted, my ears popped, and there was a sound like the planet inhaling. A deep, tidal pull. I braced without knowing why.

The ground shuddered. Not an earthquake — something more deliberate. More considered. The tremor had a centre and the centre was the base.

I watched.

What happened next was not an explosion. I want to be precise about that. There was no detonation, no shockwave racing outward. It was the opposite. Everything went inward. The base and the ground beneath it and the air above it compressed. I couldn't see details at that distance, but I could see the geometry of it — the way the darkness thickened, the way the stars behind the base warped and smeared as if viewed through bad glass.

It lasted perhaps a minute. Maybe less. I stood there with my hand on the Mule's flank and I counted my heartbeats and I lost count somewhere around forty.

Then it released.

Light. Not fire. Something worse than fire — a pale, searing light that was not fire and was worse than fire. It flared across the plain and I shut my eyes and still saw it, a white burn printed on the inside of my eyelids. The sound reached me a moment later, a low concussive thud that I felt in my chest.

When I opened my eyes the plain where the base had been was glowing. A dim, sullen luminescence. No structures. No shadows. Just a flat, faintly radiant smear.

I looked up.

The Resolute was visible most nights, a bright point tracking steadily across the sky. I found it. I watched it. I think I already knew.

It flared — a brief, silent brightening — and then it was gone. Not dimming, not falling. Gone. I didn't know about the Invincible yet. That came later.

My mother. My father. Lao, who would have tried to talk me out of leaving, if I'd given her the chance.

I sat down. I didn't decide to sit. My legs simply stopped working and I was on the ground with my hands in the dust and the Mule standing over me making soft diagnostic sounds. I was shaking. I couldn't stop shaking.

I should tell you that I wept. I should tell you that grief tore through me like the gravity wave that had just crushed everything I'd known into a thin slurry. But I would be lying. What I felt was shock, and fear, and a strange ringing emptiness, and underneath all of it — underneath everything — the knowledge that I had known. Not the details. Not the timing. But I had known. I had walked away.

I had walked away and I was alive and they were not and some part of me had chosen this.

The Mule nudged my shoulder. Its cargo frame hummed. It was waiting for a direction.

There was nothing behind me. There was nothing behind me and there never would be again.

I got up. I wiped the dust from my hands. I turned north, toward the dry hills and the empty country beyond them, and I kept walking.

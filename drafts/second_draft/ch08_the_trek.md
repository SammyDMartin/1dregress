## 8

I could not say exactly how long I wandered in that arid expanse; I'm sure I could have kept track but for some reason it never occurred to me to do so. Three weeks? A month? What did it matter? My days settled into a steady rhythm, walking for as long as I could in the hours around dawn and dusk, resting during the icy nadir of the long night and the hottest hours of the day when the blinding sun was high overhead.

An epic trek lay ahead of me — over eight hundred klicks to the fringes of the karst landscapes of the Scab and the nearest cluster of pits — and yet I never held the entire journey in my mind. I never really considered that vast distance from A to B nor what it would take to cross it. My mind was already set. I just kept putting one foot in front of the other and moment by moment the distance between me and the world I was leaving behind grew.

The landscape was a dead man's palette — grey dirt, grey rock, grey sky fading to blue-black at the zenith. The only colour was the ruddy light of the red giant at dawn and dusk, when it painted the hills in shades of rust and dried blood. Occasionally I crossed gullies where water still flowed, thin trickles of mineral-laden liquid that my aquifier could process. Otherwise there was nothing. No life visible, no movement, no sound except the wind in the thin atmosphere and the Mule's steady mechanical footfalls behind me.

I lost the Mule at some point in this span. I had looked around one day to find it no longer there. Retracing my steps, I finally found it at the bottom of a narrow ravine, its form twisted and twitching and broken. It seemed more alive in death than it ever had in its stunted mockery of life — I almost felt sorry for it. Maybe some radiation damage to its circuits had caused it to miscalculate and lose its footing. Maybe it had just made a mistake. I salvaged what I could from the wreck, slinging it across my back. Progress after that was even more agonising.

My rations ran low, then dwindled to nothing, until I was subsisting only on the meagre water the aquifier could scrape from the thin air or recycle from the waste products of my body.

Each time I resumed my journey I covered less and less ground. Each time I slept I feared that I might never wake. The prospect of surrender to the dreamworld was tempting, but the same singularity of will that had compelled me this far did not abandon me.

The dreams came every night and each night grew more vivid. I only call them dreams for lack of a better word. Simulacra? Hallucinations? Visions? All these words convey a sense of unreality, a sense that does not fit the world I found within, there for my discovery, as real as the waking world.

As the nights wore on, I cannot even be sure that I slept. Time passed. I felt rested. I had not left my tent. But in a way that I cannot truly describe I felt as though I had simply crossed over some thinning threshold and continued my journey by a different route.

Like a stone skimming over water, except that I was both the stone and its reflection, the stream of my consciousness a sinusoidal wave. As the surface of those black waters stilled, no longer broken by the troubled wake of other people's passing thoughts, the reflected world grew clearer, mirror-like. And with every oscillation, with every cycle, it became less clear to me which world was the reflection. It became less clear to me whether such a distinction even made sense.

I could feel myself growing delirious with hypoxia, dehydration and hunger, my vision blurring, my heart pounding in my chest, drowned out only by the gasping shallow breaths I drew. My aching legs and blistered feet pleaded that I stop, but I paid them no heed, listening only to the little voice that whispered just a little further now. Just a little further, you're almost there.

'So, that's how you came to be outside the base,' said Hansun. 'What I don't understand is, why is your suit recorder blank?'

'I guess I must have switched it off,' said Eszel, arms folded.

'Look,' said Hansun. He leaned forward, and something in his posture had changed. Not the patient, slightly deferential officer of the earlier sessions. Sharper. 'You've got to start giving us something, Essie.'

She frowned. Why did people always call her that when they were about to patronise her?

'With respect, Colonel, I've given you quite a lot. You've hardly left me alone since we broke orbit and all you've done is ask me the same questions over and over again.'

'Tell me about the missing thirty-three days. You were stranded with only a short-duration oxygenator, which doesn't seem to have been used for more than five hours.'

'I replaced the unit about five hours before the rescue arrived.'

'What did you eat?'

'The environment suits have protein reprocessors. I fed in local plants.'

Hansun didn't blink. 'That is an interesting answer, because it almost makes sense. One of the projects the forward base was working on when the last report was received was reconfiguring a protein reprocessor to work with Kailash organics. At last report, it wasn't going well.'

'The team working on it made a breakthrough. My father is... was a very clever man.'

There it was — the slightest change in Hansun's expression. Not quite a flinch. An adjustment. She'd said something wrong and he knew exactly what it was.

'I'm sure he was, and I know this must be difficult for you, but your father was a plasma physicist. His last mission log described inconsistencies in the behaviour of the incandescent matter in the pit-light tubes, which suggested the circulating ions were having their inertial mass altered.' Hansun paused. 'He didn't have anything to do with the protein reprocessors.'

She'd made a mistake. A small one, but Hansun had been waiting for it. She could see it in the way he held himself — the tremor was gone, his hands steady on the table. He'd found purchase.

Eszel's face screwed up in anger as a tear ran down her face. She wiped her eyes furiously with a sleeve and when she looked up her face was red.

'I'm not a liar and I don't care what you think of me. My father died just before building the machine that saved my life. Do you want me to deny that for your record?'

'That's not what I meant at all, I'm just trying to ascertain—'

'My father was a wonderful, brilliant, clever man, and he saved my life. I'm not saying any different, not to you and not to anyone else.'

She had him off-balance now, just a final push, and it would all be fine. The anger was real enough — she'd let it serve its purpose.

Hansun tensed, but he seemingly decided to drop the issue. He stood up from the chair, turning briskly to leave.

'Once again — I'm so sorry for putting you through this. Someone will be here to bring you food in the next few minutes.'

'When are you going to let me out?' she said coldly.

'Soon, I promise.'

'When we get back home, I'll remember what you've said. All the lies you've told me.'

He flinched, which surprised her a little. She forced herself not to smile as he turned away.

She was still in shock, after all. There was no possibility that she was glad to have gotten one over on the young officer. She missed the dead, her parents, her friends, all very much.

She'd said so, hadn't she?

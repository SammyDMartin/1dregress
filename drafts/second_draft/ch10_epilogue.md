Behind the retreating colonel, an unreal young man lounged against the wall, beaming at her. The Listener nodded his approval and vanished like a bad special effect.

---

## Epilogue

It was over. Hansun walked through the corridors of the HDFS Invincible, feeling the deck vibrate beneath his feet. The corridors were nearly empty now, with all but a skeleton crew returned to their cryogenic berths. They were still on conventional fusion power, slowly running diagnostics on the massive, slow-to-rouse conversion drive that would soon propel them home at constant acceleration.

The monopole-boosted drive, courtesy of Arco and leased to the HDF, would take them back to network-connected space and civilisation. It would still take two years. Two years of being stuck on the same ship as that girl. No matter that she was currently frozen and inert, a hundred metres below Hansun's feet. It spooked him, and even now he couldn't quite say why.

He didn't want to admit it, even in the privacy of his own mind, but he didn't like Eszel.

She was traumatised, surely, even if she had an odd way of showing it. But he was also sure she was hiding something. It was just obvious that her story made no sense and yet no one — least of all the Captain — seemed to care enough about how she'd survived her ordeal to investigate further.

Why am I so suspicious?

It was like a huge part of him simply wanted to forget, carry on as normal, but he couldn't quite do it.

Whenever he'd asked her a question it felt as though she were x-raying him. She never gave straight answers to the big questions, and somehow he always walked away with nothing worth reporting. She seemed to lie about trivial things with utter sincerity, so much so that he often walked away trusting her word over the evidence.

Sometimes she glanced over his shoulder at nothing, or smiled at inappropriate moments. Although she'd cried plenty after being picked up and thanked them time and again for saving her, she'd never seemed close to truly losing control. The ship's psychologist said she was just processing the fear and survivor guilt, but that didn't quite fit with what he'd seen. It was like she just wasn't too bothered by what had happened, but hadn't quite realised it yet. He supposed there wasn't any other way to put it.

There was something wrong with Eszel.

---

It was evening. Hansun was in his quarters, far wall set to imitate the rugged landscape of one of Deliverance's outermost moons, a few scattered positive-pressure buildings underneath the blue of a thin nitrogen-oxygen atmosphere.

The big display portal was showing a rerun of a century-old Oskar Nozoka drama — the melodramatic speculative thriller had always helped him disconnect from reality, and had been a guilty pleasure of his ever since he was a child.

The final post-mission briefing and laser transmission to the interstice was tomorrow, and then, the day after so far as he'd perceive it, he'd be coughing up revival fluid and trying to remember how to walk, back home aboard the Deliverance naval anchorage.

It was over, and nothing had been resolved.

Would they get a hero's welcome? What for? Simply for surviving and learning damn near nothing in the process?

Hansun rubbed his temples, closing his eyes briefly. The programme continued, Oskar brandishing a pulsar rifle at a horde of vicious pre-sentient Dyn, shouting that they would pay for their crimes.

Then the programme cut off, mid-line. Hansun opened his eyes. There was a red priority message blinking on the display. It was from the Admiral.

Ten minutes later he was dressed in full uniform and standing in the CIC of the Invincible, which was darkened and empty aside from the Admiral. Most of the surviving senior crew were already in storage deep within the vessel's suspension chambers. The circular area around the volumetric display reminded him uncomfortably of the Isidore.

The Admiral turned his hawklike face away from a console, eyeing Hansun.

'Hope I didn't disturb you, Colonel.'

'No sir.'

'I would think you'd be glad to have some R&R after what I put you through.'

'The evac went fairly smoothly in the end, sir. Though I'm sorry we never got answers.'

'I meant the girl. I've heard she's a handful.'

Hansun laughed, unsure if the Admiral was joking or not.

'You're wrong, you know. The other thing you said.' The Admiral turned away from his console, staring at Hansun across the volumetric display. 'I found some answers.'

Hansun's heart quickened.

'And that's why you brought me here?'

The Admiral nodded, waving a hand and calling up a volumetric plot of the Kailash system.

'I had the ship's general system analysing those anomalous coronal events, the ones we determined were synchronised with the fist-of-god events.'

'Is that what we're calling it — the gravity weapon?'

The Admiral shrugged, leaning over the display.

'It's as good a name as any other. Anyway—' he waved his hand and the display shifted, showing filtered ultraviolet views of the local star, the swells of plasma bursting from its surface.

'There were two major events, one associated with the destruction of the base and the other our sister ship in orbit. Magnetic flux lines pinching as part of the surface deforms inward at extreme speed, as if it's being hollowed out from the inside. The system tells me it's consistent with a vacuum cavity opening up, somewhere within the convective zone, and matter falling in to fill the gap. Enormous starquakes, rapid shift in field lines. Final proof that this is not normal solar weather.'

'The star's being siphoned? With what — one of those Arcan plasma extractors? We would have detected a solarwell, those things are huge and damn near impossible to hide.'

The Admiral smiled.

'Ah, but there was no solarwell. Nothing physical. It's as if a thousand cubic kilometres of solar plasma simply vanished from the star's convective zone. Almost as if it was moved.'

'A wormhole,' Hansun breathed. 'They've got a wormhole inside the star?'

'Indeed. Hot convective-zone plasma is being removed — likely via a wormhole gate, likely to provide power.'

Hansun flicked the display around, glancing at a time-series plot showing the starquakes and coronal mass ejections, the radiation emission spectrum rising and falling after each gravitational event on the planet.

'Where did the plasma go? We'd have seen it bursting out of the ground on Kailash like a nuclear explosion.'

'It wasn't siphoned to Kailash. That was the second thing I found.'

The map shifted again, displaying tagged objects crowding the distant edges of the system — clusters of comets in the inner Oort cloud.

'It's taken weeks for the light to arrive, but I think I know where that plasma went. Watch.'

The Admiral advanced the display, and all of a sudden, right across the map, twelve of the comets flared with brilliant radiation. Hansun started. They'd flashed like Christmas lights, coordinated and instantaneous, and — he checked the light-delay — at exactly the same moment as the weapon had fired.

'This whole system is an engine,' said Aumonier. 'Silent-running and stealthy, but an engine nonetheless. Solar plasma is relayed by wormhole, converted to some more usable form of energy inside these distant structures, then relayed back to the inner planets by more wormholes. This entire system is probably criss-crossed with them, stitched up into one great machine.'

And this entire time, we've been blundering through it without the slightest idea of what we were facing, Hansun thought.

'So we at least know how the weapon was powered, sir,' said Hansun. 'I don't understand — why the secrecy?'

Aumonier didn't look up, too absorbed in his display. He magnified one of the distant flaring comets, expanding the image and adding in enhancement and visual inference filters. Slowly the structure built up, and Hansun choked off as he realised what he was seeing.

The Other Moon?

An almond-shaped, grey-brown structure, superficially like an asteroid in appearance, but too regular, too symmetrical, unmistakable as a prehistoric hand-axe. The indisputable product of intelligent engineering.

Aumonier flicked the display, showing another 'comet', which had an identical shape. And another. And another.

'A dozen Other Moons, Colonel. The greatest treasure trove of technology in all history. And the most powerful weapon of them all. With one of these, the ancient Dyn broke the Utilitaria. With ten…'

Hansun saw Aumonier's thought then, and suppressed a shudder.

'With ten, there would be no limits to our power.'

---

Deep in the suspension chambers of the Invincible, in a cryopod indistinguishable from the hundreds of others around it, Eszel lay in the cold and the dark.

She was not asleep. Not quite. The revival systems registered nominal function across all metrics — heart rate, neural activity, metabolic baseline. By every measurable standard, she was in deep cryogenic suspension, dreaming of nothing.

But she was not asleep.

Somewhere beneath the flat line of the brainwave monitor, in a space the instruments couldn't reach, she felt the hum. The same hum she'd felt on the balcony, in the spine cupola, in the karst beneath Kailash. The Listener's frequency, impossibly faint, impossibly far, and impossibly patient.

She would carry this for the rest of her life. She could feel the web — the burning, incomprehensible web of everything — folded up and stored in some new annex of her mind, a room she hadn't known existed until he'd opened the door. It would be there when she woke. It would be there for all the years after.

She wasn't afraid. She'd never been afraid.

In the dark, in the cold, in the space between one heartbeat and the next, Eszel waited.

## 2

I'd been left alone in the small room — a room that they still insisted was not a jail cell.

I lay slouched over the hard foammetal table, playing idly with a strand of my hair, not looking up. The metal felt cold on my skin, uncomfortably so, but I didn't sit up. Project an image, Essie — lie with every gesture and action.

If I was going to make it through this, I had to play everything just right.

There was nothing in the definitely-not-a-cell other than a table and two uncomfortable chairs, but I could still hear a chittering emanating from the walls, a vaguely electronic noise that indicated circulating currents and the ebbing and flowing of magnetic fields. I'd wracked my brains trying to think what it might be, maybe unshielded electrics, and concluded that it must be microM or QES tech, scanning my brain for truthfulness.

Still, I didn't look up. A few minutes passed.

Did they expect me to be more forthcoming if I'd been left here with my own thoughts for just a few minutes? If so, then they'd underestimated me — again.

Minutes more passed, and I wondered how long they were legally permitted to keep me waiting. I'd have to look that up after this was over.

There was no reason to be afraid. There was nothing to be afraid of. Remember that.

He wouldn't get a hint of fear or uncertainty from me. Not now, no matter what. They were all in the wrong, and what was worse was that they knew it.

I'd give them boredom and mild irritation, and they could conclude whatever they wanted.

The wait continued. I could feel the slight vibration of the drive through the desk, minute fluctuations in thrust that made the entire room hum subliminally. It gave everything a slightly fuzzy quality, as if I needed visual corrective surgery. Even that felt like it was a tactic, somehow, like this had all been arranged simply to make my life as uncomfortable as possible.

They aren't here to help you, remember that. They're here to catch you out.

The door opened, and the bulky shape of Colonel Hansun stepped inside, his fleet uniform immaculately pressed.

The colonel had clearly been an athletic man at one point, with a mixture of african and asiatic features that marked him out as a native of Deliverance, but he was showing signs of age — balding, with a distracted air and a tremor along one arm. As he sat, I noticed the way he positioned himself at a slight angle, attempting to de-emphasise that side of his body. Insecure.

Remember that — it might be important later. Anything might be important.

Hansun gave a slightly apologetic nod and a murmured word of greeting, which I didn't return.

I didn't meet his eyes, letting the moment stretch. Hansun coughed. I waited.

A few more seconds passed. Almost there.

I sat up suddenly, putting on a placid smile, which only widened upon seeing his reaction.

The colonel straightened his navy uniform and tilted his head up a little.

'I'm sorry to have kept you waiting, Essie.'

'Eszel. My friends called me Essie.'

I was not being interrogated; the navy troopers had been very clear about that, several times.

'Yes, of course,' the colonel nodded slowly. 'Let me say again just how sorry I am, Eszel. We understand what you're going through, and if there's anything we can—'

'No you don't understand,' I snapped, putting just the right amount of hurt into the words.

The colonel gave me a stony look, and I wondered if I'd pushed it too far.

I was a traumatised girl, the only survivor of a catastrophe, I needed rest and to have my story listened to.

It was hard to keep an even temper through it all, but somehow I'd managed so far. More or less. I'd certainly made a good try of it.

'Is there anything you'd like? Something to drink—'

'Let's just start, Colonel.'

'Hansun, please. I'm not here in any military capacity.'

So I'm not the only one lying today… too bad you're no good at it, Colonel.

'Very well, if you're sure. Why don't we start at the beginning?' Hansun made a subliminal twitch with one hand, and I knew he'd activated some type of recording device.

'Which beginning?'

'Whenever you'd like. I want to know how you ended up here, in your own words. Any details might be important.'

'From when I left the base or…'

'From your first involvement with the mission.'

Ah, so that's your game. Get me to talk as much as possible, in as much detail as possible, to try and catch me in a lie. Clever.

'You want to know everything? We'll be here for years.'

'I meant everything relevant, Eszel. Why don't you start with how you found out about the expedition?'

'Fine.'

Essie lay sideways on her bed with the broad picture window in front of the balcony de-opaqued, revealing a square of sky with only a few of the taller skyscrapers visible.

She liked to watch the ships rising in the night. They wafted up like little flecks of ash, shimmering as they caught the light of the launch beams. She focussed on one, and though it wasn't visible as anything more than a speck at this distance, the delicate geometry was clear by the way the exhaust jet billowed out behind it — the ice-sheathed parabolic reflector that bounced the laser light and the way the air flowed around it, dividing the smooth flow like a scissor slicing through fabric.

Her slate said the design was five hundred years old, based on technology approved by the Threshold Authority for zero-tariff sale everywhere in the Union, and so it had been ubiquitous for almost as long as the Union had existed. She could have stood on this same balcony and seen this same sight two hundred years ago. Maybe she'd come back in two hundred years and watch the same thing again.

'It's twenty years!' said an older female voice, audible even through her room's walls. 'We can't just uproot her like this!'

'Most people would give their lives for what I've given us — do you have any idea how long the lottery list was, how lucky we were to be selected!'

'Oh yes, the lottery. The lottery that you entered without even thinking about how it would affect me or Essie… without even asking us!'

'This was a chance I had to take. It'll be historic, a chance we can't ever—'

'You don't know what you're missing right here!' the female voice grew louder.

The firefly light winked out. Essie knew what that meant — the shuttle had completed its launch, flung out so fast that the world curved beneath it faster than it could fall.

On impulse, she said a few words to her bedroom's system and called up Newton's ancient diagram that explained orbits, throwing the image onto the wall. She looked at it, a thousand years old and still as true as the day it had been drawn.

She'd always found it funny — some things, the holos in her room, the clouds, would change from day to day or minute to minute. Some things lasted for lifetimes, like the design of those launchers. But other things couldn't change, not ever.

In the background, the argument continued, the female voice full of concern and worry, the male strident and indignant. The roles continue to play out, action and reaction.

Boring, predictable, simple.

She tried to blot out the sounds of their argument. It was easy, really. If you made them no longer a part of your personal universe, they couldn't bother you.

And it was predictable — she secretly wanted to say yes to the mission as much as he did, but felt she had to stage a guilt fit first because of how it would impact her family, he felt bad about not asking her first, and did his best to cover it up with bluster. Pretending, always pretending, everyone always pretending and lying and lying. It drove Essie mad.

Maybe things wouldn't be the same at the new star. Then again, they probably would.

The way people acted had always made her angry, in an oblique way that she'd never been good at expressing. She knew they couldn't help it, but somehow that just made it worse.

Essie stood up from her chair and threw the balcony window open, stepping out onto the marble and placing both her hands on the railings. She hoisted herself up on both arms, feeling her feet leave the ground and her elbows lock. She leant over the balcony, tipping forward almost to the point where she'd overbalance, and holding there, swaying gently. The breeze rustled her hair, and she tilted her head up a bit. If she could just forget about her arms holding herself up, it was almost like she was flying, like the time when she'd been very small and her class had taken a day trip to one of the sky-bubbles.

Essie looked down, past the lip of the balcony, down the dozens of stories of the skyscraper to ground level, and tipped forward a little further, feeling slightly giddy. She felt her heart race, but there wasn't anything else there, nothing like fear, just exhilaration at being so close to something new and exciting, even if that new thing was death.

She could keep tipping forwards, and if she kept it up maybe she'd lose her nerve and fall eventually. Maybe falling that far would scare her. Or maybe it wouldn't.

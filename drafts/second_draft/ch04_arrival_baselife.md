## 4

'Tell me about arriving at Kailash,' said Hansun. 'What do you remember?'

'I remember waking up,' said Eszel. 'That's what I remember most.'

---

I woke up dying, or at least that's what it felt like.

Revival from twelve years of cryosleep was not the gentle ascent from slumber the orientation videos had promised. It was violence — my body convulsing on the slab, lungs burning, the revival fluid draining from my throat in a long, rasping cough that left me gasping on my side in the recovery alcove, shivering, blind, unable to remember my own name for almost a minute.

The ceiling resolved above me. Grey nanotube weave. The Invincible.

I was eighteen now, give or take. I had been six when I went under. My body had grown around me in the dark, fed by the ship's systems, and what had emerged from the cryopod was a stranger — long-limbed, thin, with hands I didn't recognise as my own.

My father found me in the recovery ward, his face gaunt from his own revival. He was grinning. He always grinned at the wrong moments — funerals, arguments, moments of genuine danger. His capacity for selective delusion was, in its own way, a kind of genius.

'We made it, Essie. Come and see.'

The observation cupola — the same one I'd broken into years ago, now officially opened for the occasion — was crowded with crew and civilians, all of them staring at the main display. I pushed through to the front.

Kailash.

A world the colour of dried blood, rust-red plains and grey karst highlands under the bloated orange disk of an elderly red giant. The atmosphere was a thin blue haze at the terminator, and the surface was scored with dark lines — channels, canyons, the remnants of ancient rivers. Nothing moved at this resolution. Nothing that I could see.

But I could feel it. The same hum I'd felt in the spine cupola, years ago, the taut-membrane vibration just below hearing. Stronger now. Closer. Coming from down there.

The others saw a planet. I saw something waiting.

'Beautiful, isn't it?' my father said. He touched my shoulder. I let him.

'Yes,' I said, because that was what you were supposed to say.

We were trespassing. I couldn't have told you how I knew.

---

The days at Walker Base had a texture like recycled water — technically adequate, stripped of anything you might call flavour.

The base was a cluster of extruded concrete shelters and prefabricated modules, set into a shallow depression near the equator. From inside, every corridor looked the same — grey walls, grey floors, the perpetual hum of life support, the faint chemical tang of scrubbed air. I counted the ceiling panels on my walks between my quarters and the mess hall. There were forty-seven.

My father threw himself into his work with the manic energy of a man who had staked everything on a single bet. He was part of the physics team studying the pit-light tubes — channels of incandescent matter running deep beneath the karst, circulating between the alien structures the survey probes had identified from orbit. His lab was a converted storage module, cluttered with sensor rigs and display screens showing false-colour readouts of plasma flow patterns. He came home late and talked too fast.

'The circulating ions are having their inertial mass altered,' he told me once, over dinner, his hands moving as they always did when he was excited — quick, imprecise gestures, as if he were trying to catch something invisible. 'Not their gravitational mass. Their inertial mass. Do you understand what that means?'

I did. It meant the rules were different here.

'That's interesting,' I said, in a voice that made him look at me for a moment too long before going back to his food.

My mother worried. That was the substrate beneath everything else. She worried about the thin atmosphere and the radiation levels and the distance from the nearest medical facility and the fact that I didn't seem to have any friends. She was right about the last one, technically. Lao was something, but I wasn't sure what.

Lao had come out of cryo two days after me. We'd orbited each other on the ship, before, but the base compressed everyone into closer proximity. She was smart — smarter than she let on. She performed a version of herself that was slightly less intelligent than the real thing, the way some people slouch to appear shorter. I noticed because I did the same thing, although for different reasons.

'You're doing it again,' she said to me once, in the mess hall.

'Doing what?'

'Staring at people like you're dissecting them.'

I looked at her. She didn't flinch. Most people flinched.

'Sorry,' I said. 'Bad habit.'

'It's not a habit,' said Lao. 'Habits are things you do without thinking about it. You do it on purpose.'

I didn't have an answer for that. I filed it away and moved on.

Commander Vasik ran the base the way a nervous man runs a meeting — too many check-ins, too many status updates, too many reminders that they were all there to serve the mission. He was afraid of being irrelevant, and the fear made him micromanage. The science teams tolerated him.

Dr Kessler, head of xenolinguistics, was quietly furious most of the time. Her team had been trying to establish communication protocols with the Mimics — the small, inscrutable aliens that inhabited the karst — for months, and had achieved nothing. She broadcast mathematical primers, visual sequences, acoustic patterns. The Mimics ignored all of it. They went about their business in the canyons and around the pit openings like midgets in greatcoats, their organic exoskeletons rustling, their purposes opaque.

'Nothing,' Kessler would say at the weekly briefings, in a tone that made the word sound like an obscenity. 'Absolute silence.'

I watched the Mimics from the base perimeter whenever I could. One of the advantages of being the youngest person on a base full of distracted adults is that you become furniture. Nobody noticed me standing at the outer fence, watching the dark shapes move in the karst below.

They noticed me, though. I was sure of it. Sometimes one would stop, and its exoskeleton would shift in a way that caught the light differently, and I would feel — not see, feel — something directed at me. Attention. Recognition. The hum that I'd felt since the spine cupola, since before that, maybe since the balcony.

Come closer.

I didn't tell anyone. Who would believe me? And besides — it was mine. The first thing in my life that was entirely mine. Not my parents' ambitions reflected through me. Not the colony's investment in my potential. Not Lao's friendship, which I valued but which always felt like something I was performing rather than inhabiting.

This was mine, and the rest of the species could wait.

---

The storm came two weeks into our second month on the surface.

Not weather — not in the conventional sense. A radiation event, the red giant belching a coronal mass ejection that overwhelmed the base's shielding for almost an hour. The lights went red, the alarms howled, and everyone crowded into the inner modules while the outer structures took the brunt. When it was over, a third of the base's external sensors were fried, the perimeter fence had gaps, and two of the surface vehicles were dead.

My father was ecstatic. The storm had caused anomalous readings in his pit-light tubes — the plasma flow had accelerated during the event, as if responding to the influx of energy. He spent three days straight in his lab, sleeping on the floor.

My mother checked on me twice. I told her I was fine. She looked at me with that expression she had — not quite worry, more like bewilderment, as if I were a piece of furniture she'd bought years ago and only just noticed didn't match the room.

The fence gaps weren't repaired for several days. There was too much else to fix.

Three days. In three days I would go. I didn't know that yet. But the thing beneath the karst did.

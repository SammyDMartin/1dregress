## 6

I went back three days later. Same route, same canyon, same descent. My body remembered the geometry, and the bioluminescence brightened as I passed, the pulses quickening. I was expected.

Three Mimics waited by the pit, their exoskeletons catching the blue-green light in shifting patterns. The metallic sand on the floor had been rearranged — concentric rings, tightened, more complex than before, and I realised with a small shock that the new pattern incorporated something of me. My geometry. Whatever it was about my mind that the channel locked onto, translated into a physical arrangement of iron filings on stone.

They had been preparing.

The patterns began immediately, denser and more layered than the first time. If the first contact had been a greeting, this was a briefing.

The pits. I had thought of them as features of the landscape. Sinkholes. Geological curiosities. They were not. They were nodes. Thousands of them, spread across the planet's surface and extending deep — deeper than our instruments could reach, deeper than the karst, down into regions of compressed matter where the distinction between rock and machine ceased to exist. Each pit connected to every other pit through the matter-streams, and the matter-streams were not trade routes.

They were circulatory.

Kailash was not a dying world with primitive aliens scrabbling in its ruins. Kailash was a system. A single, integrated, incomprehensibly old system, and the Mimics were not its civilisation. They were its hands. Its maintenance crew. Its caretakers, tending the nodes, repairing the streams, keeping the ancient infrastructure functional while the thing they served did whatever it was that it did.

Slept, maybe. Or thought. Or waited.

The Whole. The sense of it arrived not as a word but as a totality — every pit, every stream, every filament of bioluminescence, every Mimic, every layer of compressed matter down to the planet's core. All of it one thing, one system, one vast and patient entity that had been here longer than the human species had existed.

I stood there and received this and did not move.

Then they showed me the danger.

It came as a sensation first — a discordance, a wrongness, like a sustained note played slightly flat. I felt it in my teeth before I understood it. The vacuum energy drives. The drilling. The electromagnetic noise of two hundred people and their machines, all of it radiating into the substrate, interfering with patterns that had been stable for aeons. The matter-streams near the base had begun to falter. Tiny disruptions. Imperceptible to human instruments.

The Whole had noticed us.

And the Whole had decided.

I saw the principle. Gravity. Regions of it, focused and immense, powered by something beneath us that drew on energies I could barely conceptualise — wormhole-siphoned stellar plasma, as the scientists might have theorised, had any survived. The Whole would close its fist around the irritant, and the irritant would cease.

This was not cruelty. The patterns carried no malice. It was maintenance. The same function the Mimics performed every day — tending the system, correcting errors, removing what did not belong. The humans were a blockage in a pipe. The Whole would clear it.

How long? The patterns were imprecise. Days. Perhaps less.

The Mimics waited. Their exoskeletons shifted with minute adjustments, and I understood that they were doing something they did not often do. They were offering a choice. Not to the humans. To me. Because I was the one who could hear, and therefore I was the one who could choose.

I could go back. Run to the base and find Aumonier or Kessler or any of them and try to explain. The Whole. The danger. The need to evacuate immediately.

I played it out. I am good at playing things out. Aumonier's face, sceptical and then hungry. The military officers reaching for threat assessments. The xenolinguists demanding to know how I had achieved communication and could it be replicated and documented. The committee meetings.

They would not leave. I knew this with the certainty the Mimics had given me their other truths. The humans would not leave because they could not — not physically but psychologically. Institutionally. Aumonier would want the technology. The military would want the weapon. The scientists would want the data. And all of them would want more time, just a little more time, and time was the one thing the Whole would not give them.

They would die in the middle of a committee meeting about evacuation procedures. That felt right. That felt like how it would go.

The Mimics showed me the alternative. A direction. Forward, deeper, further. They would show me what I was meant to see. The thing that had touched me since childhood — the voice, the pull, the Listener — had a purpose for me, and that purpose lay ahead, not behind.

Stay, and die with the others. Leave, and see.

I turned. I felt the patterns shift around me, rearranging, the geometry of the choice inscribing itself into the chamber's metallic sand. The bioluminescence pulsed once, deep and slow.

I walked toward the deeper tunnels, and the Mimics moved with me, and behind me the chamber sealed itself — not with a door, not with a mechanism, but with a rearrangement of matter that was as casual and total as breathing.

I did not look back. I want to say that it cost me something. Perhaps it did, somewhere beneath the calm surface of my thoughts, in some locked room where I kept the things I could not afford to feel.

But in that moment, walking deeper into whatever waited beneath the karst, I felt only the pull, and I followed it.

Behind me, above me, two hundred people continued their lives. Eating, sleeping, arguing, repairing storm damage, filing reports. My mother was probably looking for me. My father was probably working. Lao was probably wondering where I'd gone.

They had days. Maybe less.

I kept walking.

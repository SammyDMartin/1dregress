I went back three days later. Same route, same canyon, same descent. My body remembered the geometry, and the bioluminescence brightened as I passed, the pulses quickening, and I understood this was acknowledgment. I was expected.

The chamber was not empty this time. Three Mimics waited by the pit, their exoskeletons catching the blue-green light in shifting patterns. The metallic sand on the floor had been rearranged — the concentric rings tightened, more complex, and I realised with a small shock that the new pattern incorporated something of me. My geometry. The shape of my mind's resonance, translated into a physical arrangement of iron filings on stone.

They had been preparing.

The patterns began immediately, denser and more layered than before. If the first contact had been a greeting, this was a briefing. I stood at the chamber's edge and received it, and my knees almost buckled under the weight of what they were showing me.

The pits. I had thought of them as features of the landscape. Sinkholes. Geological curiosities. They were not. They were nodes. Thousands of them, spread across the planet's surface and extending deep — deeper than our instruments could reach, deeper than the karst, down into regions of compressed matter where the distinction between rock and machine ceased to exist. Each pit connected to every other pit through the matter-streams, and the matter-streams were not trade routes.

They were circulatory.

Kailash was not a dying world with primitive aliens scrabbling in its ruins. Kailash was a system. A single, integrated, incomprehensibly old system, and the Mimics were not its civilisation. They were its hands. Its maintenance crew. Its caretakers, tending the nodes, repairing the streams, keeping the ancient infrastructure functional while the thing they served — the Whole — did whatever it was that it did.

Slept, maybe. Or thought. Or waited.

The Whole. The word arrived in my mind not as a word but as a totality — the sense of every pit, every stream, every filament of bioluminescence, every Mimic, every layer of compressed matter down to the planet's core, all of it one thing, one system, one vast and patient entity that had been here longer than the human species had existed.

I understood this, and I was terrified, and I was exalted, and I did not move.

Then they showed me the danger.

It came as a sensation first — a discordance, a wrongness, like a sustained note played slightly flat. I felt it in my teeth before I understood it intellectually. The vacuum energy drives. The drilling. The electromagnetic noise of two hundred people and their machines, all of it radiating into the substrate, interfering with patterns that had been stable for aeons. The matter-streams near the base had begun to falter. Tiny disruptions. Imperceptible to human instruments. But the Whole perceived them the way you would perceive a splinter working its way into your skin.

The Whole had noticed us.

That was the phrase that formed in my mind, though the Mimics did not deal in phrases. The Whole had noticed us, and the Whole had considered, and the Whole had decided.

I saw what it would do. Not in detail — not the precise mechanism, not yet. But I saw the principle. Gravity. Regions of it, focused and immense, hundreds of times the force that held me to this ground. Powered by something beneath us that drew on energies I could barely conceptualise. Wormhole-siphoned stellar plasma, as the scientists might have theorised, had any survived. The Whole would simply close its fist around the irritant, and the irritant would cease.

This was not cruelty. The patterns carried no malice, no anger, no satisfaction. It was maintenance. The same function the Mimics performed every day — tending the system, correcting errors, removing what did not belong. The humans were a blockage in a pipe. The Whole would clear it.

I stood there. I breathed. The air in my suit tasted metallic.

How long did I have? The patterns were imprecise on this point. Days. Perhaps less.

The Mimics waited. Their exoskeletons shifted with minute adjustments, catching the light differently, and I understood that they were doing something they did not often do. They were offering a choice. Not to the humans. To me. Because I was the one who could hear, and therefore I was the one who could choose.

I could go back. I could run to the base and find Aumonier or Kessler or any of them and I could try to explain what I had learned. The Whole. The danger. The need to leave immediately, to pack everything and evacuate before the gravity weapon — I was already thinking of it as a weapon, though that was the wrong word, it was a function — before the function activated.

I played it out in my head. I am good at playing things out. Aumonier's face, sceptical and then hungry. The military officers reaching for threat assessments and defensive protocols. The xenolinguists demanding to know how I had achieved communication and could it be replicated and documented. The committee meetings. The debates about whether to trust the word of an eighteen-year-old girl who had broken perimeter regulations and claimed to talk to aliens through geometry.

They would not leave. I knew this with the same certainty the Mimics had given me their other truths. The humans would not leave because they could not. Not physically — the ships were capable. But psychologically. Institutionally. They had come too far, invested too much. Aumonier would want the technology. The military would want the weapon. The scientists would want the data. And all of them would want more time, just a little more time, to study and to understand, and time was the one thing the Whole would not give them.

And even if by some miracle I convinced them. Even if they listened. The evacuation would take days they did not have. The drives would need to spin up. The equipment would need to be secured. There would be arguments about what to take and what to leave. There would be meetings. There would be protocols.

They would die in the middle of a committee meeting about evacuation procedures. That felt right. That felt like how it would go.

I am making it sound like a rational calculation. It was not entirely rational. But it was not difficult, either, and that is the part I do not want to examine.

The Mimics showed me the alternative. A path. Not a physical path — or not only a physical path. A direction. Forward, deeper, further. They would show me what I was meant to see. The Listener — the thing that had touched me, shaped me, opened the channel in my mind — had a purpose for me, and that purpose lay ahead, not behind.

Stay, and die with the others. Leave, and see.

It was not a hard choice. That is the worst thing about it. It should have been agonising. It should have torn me apart. I should have wept and raged and bargained with the small, inscrutable beings who were offering me this impossible ultimatum. I should have demanded more time, more options, some third path that saved everyone.

I didn't.

I thought of Lao, and I felt something twist in my chest, and then the twist faded, and that frightened me more than the gravity weapon. The speed with which it faded. As though the connection I'd thought I had with her was thinner than I'd believed. As though all my connections were thin. As though I had always been preparing for exactly this moment — the moment when I turned away from everyone I had ever known and walked toward something I did not understand but could not refuse.

I did not say yes. The Mimics did not deal in words, and neither, in that moment, did I. I simply turned. I felt the patterns shift around me, rearranging, the geometry of the choice inscribing itself into the chamber's metallic sand. The bioluminescence pulsed once, deep and slow, an acknowledgment.

I walked toward the deeper tunnels, and the Mimics moved with me, and behind me the chamber sealed itself — not with a door, not with a mechanism, but with a rearrangement of matter that was as casual and total as breathing.

I did not look back. I want to say that I wanted to. I want to say that it cost me something, that I hesitated, that I carried the weight of what I was allowing to happen. And perhaps I did, somewhere beneath the calm surface of my thoughts, in some locked room of my mind where I kept the things I could not afford to feel.

But in that moment, walking deeper into whatever waited beneath the karst, I felt only the pull, and I followed it.

Behind me, above me, two hundred people continued their lives. Eating, sleeping, arguing, repairing storm damage, filing reports. My mother was probably looking for me. My father was probably working. Lao was probably wondering where I'd gone.

They had days. Maybe less.

I kept walking.

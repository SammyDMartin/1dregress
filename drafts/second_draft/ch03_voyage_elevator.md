## 3

The news of the anomaly had arrived in increments, trickling back across lightyears from a nameless system so overlooked it had apparently never merited more than a catalogue designation. Probes had been this way before, but never lingered, hurtling by at a mere order of mag below c, gathering only enough data to broadly confirm what we already knew. A DZ class white dwarf. A halo of debris and frozen planetesimals. Nothing more to see here.

It had been hundreds of years before somebody had bothered to send a follow up — one of the vast development combines seeking to stake a claim on the off chance that at some point, another few centuries hence, it might become economical to exploit the system.

The discovery was a fluke — as the surveying probe made its protracted deceleration, magsail unfurled, it caught a glimpse of something in its scopes. Something that appeared to warp the stars behind it, the emission spectra and mass profile utterly wrong for an object that could not be much more than a kilometre across. Driven by an ersatz mechanical curiosity, the probe altered its approach, swinging towards the star then out into a wide ellipse that would take it back to the anomaly's distant orbit.

I can only imagine the impatience those privy to the initial discovery must have felt as they waited long months for the probe to reacquire its newfound target, made all the more agonising given the knowledge that this was already old news, the data already a decade out of date. But as it drew closer they were able to ascertain that which they had already tentatively begun to suspect. A second interstice, right there in the heart of Union space. A stabilised, macro-scale wormhole leading to god-knows-where, barely a stone's throw away in interstellar terms.

The news leaked soon afterwards — not even a corp could keep a secret like that, not when they'd just blundered across it. Of course they extracted concessions and contracts as restitution but within hours it was out of their hands and under government aegis. Even with our bleeding-edge conversion drives we weren't risking being beaten to the punch.

It had all begun before Eszel had been born, when a routine sky survey from a world on the fringe of the Union-controlled segment of the great interstice network had identified a planet, close in conventional interstellar terms, with an oxygen atmosphere, which orbited an unstable elderly red-giant star. A new biosphere was a rarity, with only a few dozen examples yet directly sampled, and this deserved closer study.

The Byzantine structures in the Union bureaucracy came up with the name Kailash.

Funding was secured and an interstellar-capable Star Archer was constructed and deployed a dozen light-sail probes, accelerating each to a substantial fraction of lightspeed. It was when the first of these probes clipped by the world, Kailash, that attention truly exploded. There were thermal emissions, lights on the surface, signs of technological civilisation.

The Union senate saw their chance — the Xianists had the Tanu, Arco had the Dyn — this was their opportunity to gain access to an intelligent, and above all technological, alien culture.

After negotiation with Arco, made easier by their recent transgressions into Union space, an interstellar-capable propulsion unit was leased, in exchange for promises that it would be used for peaceful exploration only.

Humanity had taken centuries to bridge the energy desert — the vast gulf that separated the electroweak energies that even 21st century technology could probe, and the grand unified realm where matter and force flowed into new, stranger forms. Arco's barely-controlled Armstrong accelerator tests in deep space and later the more stable monopole condensers, had finally achieved that long sought after goal, opening up a new realm of fundamental physics. One result, the vacuum energy drive, was unstable and poorly understood, but capable of sucking energy from an apparently limitless well.

The hull of the HDFS Invincible was constructed around the Arco-built propulsion unit and augmented to transport a crew of several hundred sleepers at a substantial fraction of c. For the expected twenty-one-year round trip — ten years out, a year on site, ten years to return — limited allowance was made for civilian families.

---

Hansun leaned forward, his elbows resting on the cold metal table. The harsh lighting of the interrogation room cast deep shadows across his face, accentuating the lines of worry that had formed over the past few weeks.

'Your parents brought you along with them. Were you excited, scared?'

Eszel met his gaze, her expression carefully neutral. 'I was six years old, and I'd been told I was going to fly to a planet around another star. So yes, I was excited.'

She analysed his body language, the slight tilt of his head, the way his fingers tapped almost imperceptibly on the table. He's trying to establish a rapport.

Hansun smiled, a gesture that didn't quite reach his eyes. 'I'd have been excited by that too. I was, in fact, and I was already a seasoned officer when I first heard about Kailash.'

She nodded, and a moment passed.

Undeterred, Hansun pressed on. 'Alien life, what is this, only the sixth ever independent discovery of intelligence. Was it something you found particularly interesting as you got older?'

'Sure, I guess. Who wouldn't be interested in aliens?'

'Can you elaborate on that?' Hansun asked, leaning in slightly. 'What aspects of the Kailash fascinated you the most?'

Eszel paused, considering her response carefully. 'Their biology, I suppose,' she said finally. 'The way they adapted to such an inhospitable environment. It's... admirable.'

Hansun nodded, making a note on his datapad. 'And what about their technology? We found some truly remarkable things on Kailash.'

'Like what?' Eszel asked, allowing a hint of curiosity to colour her voice.

'Well,' Hansun began, 'their manipulation of gravity, for one. We barely understood the principles, but the applications were... astounding.'

Eszel felt a thrill run through her, remembering the glimpses she'd seen of the Kailash technology. 'Astounding,' she echoed, her voice distant. 'Yes, I suppose it was.'

Hansun's eyes narrowed slightly at her tone. 'You sound like you gave it a lot of attention,' he probed.

Oh whoops. Probably shouldn't have said that.

'I perused the reports we got at the base,' she said, forcing a shrug. 'Before... you know.'

'Right,' Hansun said, his voice heavy with unspoken questions. 'About the base. When you were there, did you notice anything unusual? Anything that might have hinted at what was to come?'

'Unusual how?' she asked.

'Strange phenomena, unexplained events, anything out of the ordinary,' Hansun pressed.

A flicker of fear crossed her face. 'There were... tremors sometimes. And lights in the sky that didn't match any known celestial bodies. But the scientists said it was just normal planetary activity.'

Hansun leaned back, his expression unreadable. 'And you believed them?'

'Why wouldn't I?' Eszel countered, an edge of defiance in her voice. 'I was just a kid.'

'A very intelligent kid,' Hansun observed. 'Your academic records are impressive. You had a particular aptitude for physics, I noticed.'

Eszel felt a surge of pride, quickly suppressed. 'So what? Lots of kids are good at science.'

'Not many understand quantum field theory at sixteen,' Hansun said mildly. 'And political philosophy: I heard you gave your teacher quite a headache about posthumanism. And cell biology, and… well, everything. You have a bright future ahead of you.'

Eszel remained silent, sensing a trap.

Hansun changed tack. 'Let's go back to the Kailash themselves. Did you ever interact with them directly?'

'No,' Eszel lied smoothly. 'They kept us kids away from the contact zones.'

'But you must have been curious,' Hansun pressed. 'A brilliant young mind like yours, faced with an advanced alien civilisation. Didn't you ever try to... bend the rules a little?'

Eszel allowed a smirk to play across her lips. 'Maybe,' she said. 'What would you have done?'

Hansun chuckled, but there was no humour in it. 'I'm not the one being questioned here, Eszel.'

'Aren't I?' Eszel shot back. 'Feels an awful lot like an interrogation to me.'

'It's not,' Hansun said firmly. 'We're just trying to understand what happened. You're the only survivor, Eszel. Your perspective is invaluable.'

Eszel felt a flicker of something — not quite guilt, but a shadow of what guilt might feel like in someone else. 'I've told you everything I know,' she said, her voice suddenly tired.

Hansun studied her for a long moment. 'Have you?' he asked softly.

She met Hansun's gaze and said, with perfect sincerity, 'Yes. I have.'

---

The common room was crowded when Eszel arrived, and she couldn't help but notice the instant dip in the volume of conversation. Lao and Noah turned towards her, Noah showing something colourful on his terminal display.

'Hey,' she said.

Eszel leaned in closer, glancing over the image, part of an art project he'd been working on for class.

'What do you think?' asked the older boy. Eszel frowned. It was colourful, like a collection of burnished metal wires strung together. 'Nice, right?'

'Pretty good… But then I don't know art.'

Noah smirked. Eszel frowned.

'Let me guess… the terminal did it for you.'

'Nobody's ever gonna know, so who cares?'

Eszel pursed her lips in mock condemnation. Noah laughed as they all headed out the door on the chime.

The rooms of the Invincible were burnished grey nanotube weave, as light and simple a design as possible.

They all filed into the tutorial room, Eszel bringing up the rear and taking a seat next to Noah.

The room's lights darkened as the presentation began.

There hadn't been room for educators on the flight, so the Union had — despite its usual aversion to such things — resorted to virtual personas. She suspected that he was designed for significantly younger children.

Eszel spread her slate out on the table and planted an expression on her face, feigning attention. She'd always been good at that.

Jonathan winked into existence in the volumetric display — he looked professionally put together, like a newsfeed anchor, but his voice was slightly off, with a patronising edge to it that Eszel always found grating.

'I'm so glad to see everyone here. It's time to continue with yesterday's lecture. But first, our weekly journey update!' A stylised map of the interstice network appeared, showing the multiple connections they'd hopped between to reach the outpost, and the huge real-space distance they had crossed since.

Eszel did her best to look like she was paying attention as the volumetric display zoomed out, showing the arrow-straight trajectory of the Invincible, just about a twentieth of the way to Kailash.

'We are moving at almost half of the speed of light — that's fast enough to cross from one end of Stellenar to the other more than three times in a single second. Just think about that!' Jonathan continued. 'We have three weeks left until we all go under for the long sleep to face the unknown. Who's excited for that?'

There was muttered assent. Not for the first time, Eszel wished she could actually see the space outside Invincible, instead of being shuffled around like cargo from one world to another.

'With that out of the way, it's time for the rest of today's lesson. Who remembers our topic?'

There was silence for a moment.

'Citizenship and history,' said Noah, dully.

'That's right!' Jonathan beamed. 'We'll cover an overview first, just to ensure everyone's caught up.'

The view shifted and Union's flag, the stylised Vitruvian man on a field of blue, appeared behind Jonathan.

'General background. The Unified Government of Humanity has been in existence for more than five hundred years. That's longer than almost all the governments of old Earth, before the Arco conquered and subjugated it. Although we can trace our founding back to the old United Nations of Earth, our true origin is generally agreed to be… when?'

'The settlement act?' said someone.

'Not quite — that's when our constitution was written, but it wasn't when we truly began. Anyone else?'

Nobody answered. Eszel knew this one — she'd always enjoyed historical stories, tales of struggle against desperate odds, and a part of her had always wished she'd lived in those times, as horrible as they'd been. She said nothing.

'The aftermath of the Dyn invasion, the exodus fleet,' said someone near the front.

'Correct,' Jonathan nodded. 'I'm glad to see that some of you have done your research. I know that this is a busy time for us all, but your schooling is very important. Like many of the other polities currently making up human space, we can trace our origin—'

It was quite incredible, Eszel thought, the way the desk jockeys who planned this mission had managed to make a bold adventure to find life around another star this boring.

The question-and-answer session continued for a time.

'—you know our official name,' Jonathan was saying. 'The Unified Government of Humanity. But you've met Arcosians and Xianists — they're human. So why are we the government of all humanity?'

Silence. Don't say anything, Essie. It's not worth it.

'It's a lie, that's why.'

A few people turned to look at her. Eszel kept a flat expression.

'Don't look for sense in a name like that, it's just propaganda.'

Jonathan flickered for a second. He wasn't quite Turing-level, let alone generally intelligent, and her question might have tested some parameter. She'd sometimes wondered if it was possible to push him way outside his training distribution with the right combination of questions, and make his entire program break down.

'Eszel has just asked a very good question,' said Jonathan. 'Who can tell me what propaganda is?'

Nobody answered.

Noah elbowed her in the ribs. She shook her head.

'It's when you say something to make people believe you, even if you're lying,' said Noah.

'Good. So who can answer Eszel's question? What's the real reason we are called the "Unified Government of humanity"?'

Silence.

'Very well. Our Union is not the only government containing humans, but it is a government of humanity, by humanity and for humanity — and that truly is unique, and worth protecting. It's why we have the threshold committee. Who can tell me what the threshold—'

Eszel's thoughts turned to the world outside. Every week, Jonathan showed their weekly progress review, and they watched that tiny icon inch further away from the outpost system. They never got a decent look outside, at the way their sheer velocity would distort the light around them, and the sheer gulf of space between them and the nearest world or star.

She pulled up a diagram of the ship on her slate. Jonathan was still speaking. 'The Threshold itself — it is the original interstice discovered by our founders, which led to our own, closely-connected portion of the network.'

A graphic appeared behind Jonathan, a map showing marked stars, and a half-dozen small icosahedrons, symbols that represented trans-spatial connections, scattered almost randomly in a region centred on a single star.

'Who can tell me the name of that star?' said Jonathan.

'It's Earth's star, isn't it? Sol,' said someone.

'Correct,' Jonathan beamed. 'Sadly, Earth has been under occupation by Arco for many hundreds of years now. Part of learning and growing up is coming to understand that the world is not always nice, and that there are people out there who are dangerous.'

Eszel rolled her eyes. This was about as subtle as a chisel. She had to get away from it.

'But sometimes fate is kind to us — if we had not found the Threshold, our exodus might never have survived in the first place, and then Arco would have ruled everyone. So you see, there is always something to be grateful for!'

Jonathan nodded enthusiastically, and the view on the volumetric display behind him zoomed out, beyond the tiny bubble of stars around Earth, switching between views of different stars.

'To this day, our scientists don't know how common network interstices really are, or how far apart the stars they link together are in the real universe. All we know is that we can find some around the stars near Sol, and that almost every star they link to have their own scattering of connections. Sometimes, we are lucky, and they are strung very close together, with short gaps between. Sometimes two orbit the same planet! Often the gaps are much bigger, and we need huge ships like our own to travel between them.'

She had to get away from it.

Eszel frowned. Where had that thought of escape come from? It was a weird feeling, as if the impulse had suddenly struck her like a lightning bolt. But at that moment the desire to escape from the monotony of ship life was so strong it was almost unbearable.

Jonathan continued to drone on, explaining things she already knew.

'We once thought those interstices might be wormholes, but now are less sure exactly what they are... the term "trans-spatial connection" is a sort of placeholder, really. One, long ago, led all the way to the Dyn's ancient empire. Another was our own Threshold star, and still another, through many links in the chain that begun at the Threshold, and led through our home, Stellenar, led to the outpost that our own expedition launched from—'

Eszel was engrossed in the ship's diagram. True to form, there weren't windows anywhere in the habitation section. If she wanted to stargaze, that wasn't an option.

Just go outside and look.

Eszel sighed to herself. The thought was somehow halfway between a voice and mere instinct, like it was just on the edge of forming true words. She knew it was pointless to argue with herself.

---

The three of them sat around a table in the common room.

'You want to climb your way into the outer hull?' said Lao.

'Shh, not so loud,' Eszel nodded. 'We're not going to go anywhere dangerous, nowhere near the engines or anything. There's a passage the service bots use, to reach the spine elevators. We don't need to go hardly anywhere.' She flicked her finger over the terminal, magnifying part of the schematic.

The Invincible was built with the habitat section in the middle of a long pylon, a plasma umbrella projected to the fore and the propulsor held to the aft. Eszel magnified one of the access elevators.

'We just need to screw up the sensors for five minutes, move the elevator just far enough to be out from underneath the hab module, and then we'll see it — nothing but open space for ten billion light years to either side!'

'Essie, we can't. It's too dangerous.'

'It's not,' she snapped. 'And don't call me that. They just don't want us down there because it's off limits. They probably didn't think any of it through, just the usual — don't go there because we said so.'

'But, what's the point? Just to say we've done it?'

Eszel rolled her eyes. The next words came easily.

'You're going to go through life like that, aren't you? If the only reason you don't do something is that it doesn't pass some kind of complicated cost benefit test about whether it's worth it… then what's the point in living?'

Lao still looked uncomfortable.

'So the point is that there is no point?'

Oh, you did not just say that.

'You want to be a fucking slave to the fleet, to your parents? Think for yourself for once, Lao.'

She just needed a little nudge, and then it would be a done deal.

Eszel glanced over at Noah.

'I like the idea,' he said, in a tone that indicated he didn't. Eszel beamed, and the two of them looked back at Lao.

'We can take a terminal and record the whole thing, and keep hold of it for the rest of the trip. It'll be our secret, and then when we're back home, and they can't come after us to enforce their rules, release it on the streams…' she said, eyes darting from Lao to Noah.

Lao nodded slowly, as if she couldn't believe the words coming out of her own mouth.

'Okay. I'll get one of those disposable terminals, so they don't find the file.'

'Great, it's settled. Meet me at this junction at 2000 hours. I'll work up a hack for the internal sensors.'

Eszel raced through the rest of the day — mandatory exercise in the gym, another round of lessons, a quick dinner with her parents in their cabin, an ancient rerun of an Oskar Nozoka neo-thriller on their display portal, half an hour for them both to fall asleep, then up and out as silently as possible.

The sensor hack on her terminal worked perfectly, and none of the second shift workers spared her a glance as she made her way spinward to the elevator terminal. Project confidence, and you can get away with a lot — she'd lived by that maxim for much of her life, and it had rarely failed.

Eszel stepped into the access passage and walked along the rarely-trodden path, stooping a little as she moved. Lao and Noah were already waiting for her, facing away.

'Boo.'

The two of them whipped around, a shadow of a scowl crossing Lao's face. Eszel smiled innocently and stepped towards the door.

'All ready to go?'

'Sure.'

'I guess.'

She touched her terminal to the elevator, activating the hack she'd worked up. There was a brief interval while the override went to work, and then the door slid open. The two of them followed her inside reluctantly, and then the door slid shut, isolating them from the ship. There were a pair of wide cupola windows to either side, currently showing the inside of the elevator shaft.

'Just five minutes, right?' said Lao. 'We're just going to look around, take a video, then—'

'That's what we said, right?' said Noah.

Eszel ignored them both, working at her terminal, negotiating past the simple security system. There was a slight lurch beneath them as the elevator dipped, and they began to descend. Lao and Noah exchanged a few words, but Eszel stayed silent.

You're close now.

The intruding thought was more word-like, this time, almost as if someone behind her had spoken. She felt an impulse and turned, but it was just Lao and Noah.

Lao looked worried, but she was still holding up the terminal, panning the device around to capture the entire view. Noah was worried as well, but he was doing a better job of hiding it. She smiled at them both, and then, quite suddenly, the light coming through the windows shifted.

Eszel and the others turned to the windows, and they saw the space between stars.

'Woah,' said someone behind her. Eszel wasn't sure who. The two of them moved around the other window, somehow knowing not to bother her.

None of the stars were familiar, of course. This was not even the same galaxy she'd been born within. Nobody had ever mapped the links between two nodes in the network. Her mind kept trying to find and match constellations, patterns she knew, but there was genuinely nothing familiar, just brilliant cold light and endless space.

At this velocity, the starlight was bent — only subtly, but there was a tendency for stars to collect above and below, as if they were headed towards a great cluster of bluish stars, fleeing from a cluster of old, red stars. They weren't — it was just a trick of aberration.

And framing it all, the central pylon of the Invincible — to the front, a globe of dull metal, illuminated by running lights, and to the aft, an obscured pillar of luminous blue, surrounded by the wide disc that was the drive unit viewed from directly. She felt giddy, and her gaze was drawn back to the stars. There was something captivating and strange about the view, something beyond the obvious.

The empty space between the stars seemed to shimmer, like the darkness behind closed eyelids, colours that weren't quite colours drifting in and out, just on the edge of perception.

She felt another wave of dizziness, more obviously perceptible, and a flash of light from below. Eszel flinched back, but something compelled her to keep looking into the shadow between the stars, and she leant forwards again. The giddiness returned, stronger this time, and something else — a taste like metal.

Why did I call it a shadow?

What am I calling 'it'?

There was nothing fearful there, nothing dangerous about the blackness, just something illusive, fascinating. She felt like she could have stood there for hours.

'Why did I never see it before…' she wondered.

There was a thud from behind Eszel, snapping her out of her reverie. She whipped around, and saw Noah, holding Lao, doubled over, retching.

She felt sick too, and threw out an arm to steady herself.

'The drive,' Noah moaned. Eszel nodded, doubling over again, a retch rising to her own mouth. She suppressed it. Lao swayed.

You know what this is.

'Lie down, both of you, now.' They obeyed. At least now they won't fall and hurt themselves.

Don't think about it, just get away now.

Eszel grabbed her terminal, and punched in the instructions, and they accelerated upwards, the stars vanishing from view.

'Give me the terminal,' she snapped, but Lao didn't respond, curled up on the floor. She took a step towards her, feeling her head spin, and leant down.

The recording was still running as she picked it up and flung it at the opposite wall, smashing it. Then, just as the elevator drew to a halt, she grabbed her own terminal, dropped it on the floor, and ground it to fragments beneath her shoe.

They'd still know who'd been down there, of course, but without the recording and the hack, they might not figure out who'd been the ringleader.

Noah shuddered on the floor, and she looked down at him. He looked pale, and had vomited weakly onto his shirt. She should probably be doing something — vague memories of first aid flitted in and out of her mind, but she couldn't remember details.

'Just hold on,' she said, her voice dull. 'It's going to be fine.'

You're getting better at lying, I see.

Eszel's strength failed her just as she punched the physical yellow button on the elevator console.

A shrill alarm screamed, and she collapsed to her knees, doubling over, bile rising in her throat.

It didn't matter. She wasn't afraid.

---

'You didn't know about GUT field excursions, I take it?' said Hansun, voice calm and measured. 'Vacuum energy drives are metastable — you know what that means, I assume. Among other things, it means that there are random fluctuations in power, and therefore radiation output. You were all reckless.'

'Yes, thank you,' Eszel scowled, lip trembling. 'It was dumb of us to listen to Noah, but I think I'd figured that one out already. I still miss him.'

Act like he's hurt your feelings. She flinched, looking down as if abashed.

'Yes… you and Lao were fortunate to survive, but I suppose neither of you deserved to be blamed simply for following his lead.'

'Colonel, was it really necessary to bring this up? That tragedy is long behind us, and I'm sure your bosses are aware of it.'

'I'm sorry, but I was asked to go over anything that might be significant.'

'Well if you're done, can we move on? I don't enjoy talking about it, and I don't see how it's relevant to anything that happened on Kailash.'

'Yes, yes of course…' Hansun trailed off. 'The disaster itself. You left the base immediately prior to the tragedy. Why don't you tell me about that.'

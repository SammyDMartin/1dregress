## 3

The news of the anomaly had arrived in increments, trickling back across lightyears from a nameless system so overlooked it had apparently never merited more than a catalogue designation. Probes had been this way before, but never lingered, hurtling by at a mere order of magnitude below c, gathering only enough data to broadly confirm what we already knew. A DZ class white dwarf. A halo of debris and frozen planetesimals. Nothing more to see here.

It had been hundreds of years before somebody had bothered to send a follow up — one of the vast development combines seeking to stake a claim on the off chance that some centuries hence it might become economical to exploit the system.

The discovery was a fluke — as the surveying probe made its protracted deceleration, magsail unfurled, it caught a glimpse of something in its scopes. Something that appeared to warp the stars behind it, the emission spectra and mass profile utterly wrong for an object that could not be much more than a kilometre across. Driven by an ersatz mechanical curiosity, the probe altered its approach, swinging toward the star then out into a wide ellipse that would take it back to the anomaly's distant orbit.

I can only imagine the impatience those privy to the initial discovery must have felt as they waited long months for the probe to reacquire its newfound target, made all the more agonising by the knowledge that this was already old news, the data already a decade out of date. But as it drew closer they were able to ascertain that which they had already tentatively begun to suspect. A second interstice, right there in the heart of Union space. A stabilised, macro-scale wormhole leading to god-knows-where, barely a stone's throw away in interstellar terms.

The news leaked soon afterwards — not even a corp could keep a secret like that. Of course they extracted concessions and contracts as restitution, but within hours it was out of their hands and under government aegis.

It had all begun before Eszel had been born. A routine sky survey from a world on the fringe of Union-controlled space had identified a planet, close in conventional terms, with an oxygen atmosphere, orbiting an unstable red-giant star. A new biosphere was a rarity, with only a few dozen examples yet directly sampled, and this one deserved closer study. Sail probes were deployed. And when the first of them clipped by the world — designated Kailash by the Byzantine Union bureaucracy — attention exploded. There were thermal emissions, lights on the surface, signs of technological civilisation.

The Union senate saw their chance. The Xianists had the Tanu, Arco had the Dyn — this was their opportunity to gain access to an intelligent, and above all technological, alien culture.

After negotiation with Arco, made easier by their recent transgressions into Union space, an interstellar-capable propulsion unit was leased, in exchange for promises that it would be used for peaceful exploration only.

The hull of the HDFS Invincible was constructed around the Arco-built drive and augmented to transport a crew of several hundred sleepers at a substantial fraction of c. For the expected twenty-one-year round trip — ten years out, a year on site, ten years to return — limited allowance was made for civilian families.

---

'Your parents brought you along with them. Were you excited, scared?'

Hansun leaned forward, his elbows resting on the cold metal table. The harsh lighting cast deep shadows across his face.

'I was six years old, and I'd been told I was going to fly to a planet around another star. So yes, I was excited.'

She analysed his body language, the slight tilt of his head, the way his fingers tapped almost imperceptibly on the table. He's trying to establish a rapport.

Hansun smiled, a gesture that didn't quite reach his eyes. 'I'd have been excited by that too. I was, in fact, and I was already a seasoned officer when I first heard about Kailash.'

She nodded, and a moment passed.

'Alien life,' Hansun pressed. 'Only the sixth ever independent discovery of intelligence. Something you found interesting as you got older?'

'Sure, I guess. Who wouldn't be interested in aliens?'

Hansun's eyes narrowed slightly. 'You sound like you gave it a lot of attention.'

Oh. Probably shouldn't have said that.

'I read the reports we got at the base,' she said, forcing a shrug. 'Before... you know.'

'Right.' Hansun's voice was heavy with unspoken questions. 'About the base. When you were there, did you notice anything unusual? Anything that might have hinted at what was to come?'

'Unusual how?'

'Strange phenomena, unexplained events.'

A flicker crossed her face. 'There were tremors sometimes. And lights in the sky that didn't match any known celestial bodies. But the scientists said it was just normal planetary activity.'

'And you believed them?'

'Why wouldn't I? I was just a kid.'

'A very intelligent kid,' Hansun observed. 'Your academic records are impressive. Physics at sixteen. Political philosophy that gave your teacher quite a headache about posthumanism. And cell biology, and... well, everything.'

Eszel remained silent, sensing a trap.

'Let's go back to the Kailash themselves. Did you ever interact with them directly?'

'No,' Eszel lied smoothly. 'They kept us kids away from the contact zones.'

'But you must have been curious. A mind like yours, faced with an advanced alien civilisation.'

She allowed a smirk to play across her lips. 'Maybe. What would you have done?'

Hansun chuckled, but there was no humour in it. 'I'm not the one being questioned here, Eszel.'

'Aren't I? Feels an awful lot like an interrogation to me.'

'It's not,' Hansun said. 'We're just trying to understand what happened. You're the only survivor. Your perspective is invaluable.'

Eszel felt a flicker of something — not quite guilt, but a shadow of what guilt might feel like in someone else. 'I've told you everything I know,' she said, her voice suddenly tired.

Hansun studied her for a long moment. 'Have you?' he asked softly.

She met his gaze and said, with perfect sincerity, 'Yes. I have.'

---

The common room was crowded when Eszel arrived, and she couldn't help but notice the instant dip in the volume of conversation. Noah turned toward her, showing something colourful on his terminal display.

'What do you think?' asked the older boy. Eszel leaned in closer. It was colourful, like a collection of burnished metal wires strung together. 'Nice, right?'

'Pretty good... but then I don't know art.'

Noah smirked. Eszel frowned.

'Let me guess... the terminal did it for you.'

'Nobody's ever gonna know, so who cares?'

They all filed into the tutorial room on the chime, Eszel bringing up the rear and taking a seat next to Noah.

The room's lights darkened as the presentation began. There hadn't been room for educators on the flight, so the Union had — despite its usual aversion to such things — resorted to virtual personas.

Jonathan winked into existence in the volumetric display. He looked professionally put together, like a newsfeed anchor, but his voice was patronising in a way that Eszel found grating. He wasn't quite Turing-level, let alone generally intelligent, and she'd sometimes wondered if it was possible to push him way outside his training distribution with the right combination of questions and make his entire program break down.

'We are moving at almost half of the speed of light,' Jonathan continued after the weekly journey update, the little icon of the Invincible inching across the volumetric star map. 'We have three weeks left until we all go under for the long sleep. Who's excited for that?'

Muttered assent.

'With that out of the way — today's topic. Citizenship and history.' The Union flag appeared behind Jonathan, the stylised Vitruvian man on a field of blue. 'The Unified Government of Humanity has been in existence for more than five hundred years—'

'—you know our official name,' Jonathan was saying, some time later. 'The Unified Government of Humanity. But you've met Arcosians and Xianists — they're human. So why are we the government of all humanity?'

Silence. Don't say anything, Essie. It's not worth it.

'It's a lie, that's why.'

A few people turned to look at her. Eszel kept a flat expression.

'Don't look for sense in a name like that, it's just propaganda.'

Jonathan flickered for a second. Her question might have tested some parameter.

'Eszel has just asked a very good question,' said Jonathan. 'Who can tell me what propaganda is?'

'It's when you say something to make people believe you, even if you're lying,' said Noah.

'Good. So who can answer Eszel's question?'

Silence.

'Very well. Our Union is not the only government containing humans, but it is a government of humanity, by humanity and for humanity — and that truly is unique, and worth protecting.'

Eszel's thoughts turned to the world outside. Every week, Jonathan showed their weekly progress review, and they watched that tiny icon inch further away from the outpost system. They never got a decent look outside.

She had to get away from it.

Eszel frowned. Where had that thought of escape come from? It was a weird feeling, as if the impulse had suddenly struck her like a lightning bolt. But at that moment the desire to escape from the monotony of ship life was so strong it was almost unbearable.

She pulled up a diagram of the ship on her slate. The Invincible was built with the habitat section in the middle of a long pylon, a plasma umbrella projected to the fore and the propulsor held to the aft.

Just go outside and look.

The thought was halfway between a voice and mere instinct, like it was just on the edge of forming true words.

---

'You want to climb your way into the outer hull?' said Lao.

'Shh, not so loud.' Eszel magnified part of the schematic. 'There's a passage the service bots use, to reach the spine elevators. We don't need to go hardly anywhere.'

The Invincible's spine was a structural member that ran the full length of the ship between the habitat and the engines, exposed to the vacuum and the particle flux from the plasma umbrella. The spine elevators were access shafts, normally sealed, running from the habitat to various external hardpoints.

'It'll be fine,' said Eszel. 'We'll be in the spine for thirty seconds, max. The elevator capsule is shielded.'

'And then what?' said Noah, who was already grinning.

'Then we ride it to the mid-spine observation hardpoint. There's a cupola there. Transparent hull section, meant for inspections. We'll see everything.'

Lao said nothing. She was doing the calculations. Eszel could see it.

'The radiation—' Lao began.

'Is within tolerances for a short exposure. I checked.'

Lao looked at her. It was the look she sometimes gave — measuring, careful, as though she could see something Eszel didn't want her to see. Then she shrugged. 'Fine. But if we get caught, this was all Noah's idea.'

'What?' said Noah.

'You're the expendable one,' said Lao, and Eszel laughed — a real laugh, which surprised her.

---

The elevator was cramped, the four of them pressed together as it hummed upward through the spine. Eszel felt the gravity shift — lighter, then lighter still, as they moved away from the habitat's spin section. Her stomach lurched.

Then the elevator stopped, and the cupola opened above them.

Space.

Not a screen. Not a viewport filtered through seventeen layers of smart glass. Raw space, separated from her by nothing but a few centimetres of transparent hull material.

The stars didn't twinkle. They burned, steady and absolute, and there were more of them than she had ever imagined. The plasma umbrella was a faint shimmer to the fore, the stars beyond it subtly distorted, blue-shifted. Aft, the propulsor was a distant point of actinic light.

And between the stars — the void. Not empty. She could feel it, or thought she could. A vast, humming presence, as if the darkness between the stars were a taut membrane vibrating at a frequency just below hearing. She pressed her hands flat against the cupola and felt the ship's vibration and something else beneath it, fainter, deeper.

Just a little further. You're close now.

Noah was pointing at something, his voice excited and distant. Lao was breathing fast, her face lit by starlight. Eszel didn't look at either of them. She looked into the void between the stars, and the void looked back, and she felt, for the first time in her life, that she was exactly where she was supposed to be.

A proximity alarm shrieked.

'Out,' said Eszel, already moving. 'Now. Don't run — walk. Back the way we came.'

She was the last one into the elevator. As the capsule descended, she pulled up the diagnostics log on her slate and wiped their access records. It took her forty seconds. She'd practised.

Noah threw up in the corridor outside the habitat airlock. Lao was pale, her hands shaking. Eszel checked them both — elevated heart rate, slight nausea, the early signs of radiation exposure from the GUT field excursions of the vacuum energy drive. Nothing lethal. Probably.

She deleted the medical flags before anyone could see them. Noah would be sick for a few days, and Lao for a week, and neither would ever know the real reason.

It didn't matter. She wasn't afraid.

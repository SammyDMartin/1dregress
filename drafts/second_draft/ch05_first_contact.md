## 5

I went through the gap in the fence on the second night after the storm. No plan, really. Just the pull, and the gap, and the knowledge that nobody was watching the perimeter cameras because half of them were still dead.

The karst began about a kilometre from the base — a landscape of low ridges and narrow canyons, grey rock eroded into strange formations by wind and ancient water. In the canyons the bioluminescence started, faint blue-green traceries in the rock walls, following the veins of whatever mineral or organism produced them. The light pulsed in slow rhythms that I'd been watching from the fence for weeks.

The air was too thin to breathe without my suit. The gravity was lighter than standard — maybe 0.7 G. It made my steps longer than they should have been, gave everything a dreamy, slightly unreal quality.

I followed the canyon downward. The bioluminescence brightened as I descended, the pulses quickening. I'd studied the base's survey data enough to know that the canyons led toward the pits — the deep shafts that the geologists had identified as geological formations and the xenobiologists had identified as termite mounds and nobody had identified correctly, because nobody except me was paying attention to the patterns.

The Mimic was waiting at the bottom of a scree slope, standing by one of the smaller pit openings. I say waiting — I have no evidence that it was waiting for me specifically, except that I knew it was, the same way I'd known the fence would be unmonitored.

It was small. Barely a metre tall, wrapped in its organic exoskeleton — layers of chitinous material that folded and overlapped, giving it a bundled, hunched appearance. Its surface shifted constantly, micro-adjustments that caught the bioluminescent light in changing patterns. No face that I could identify. No eyes. No mouth. The xenolinguists had spent months pointing signal arrays at these things and getting nothing back.

I stopped about three metres away. We regarded each other.

'Hello,' I said, because I wasn't sure what else to do, and then felt stupid about it because the sound barely carried in the thin atmosphere and even if it did I was talking to something that had ignored every communication attempt humanity had ever made.

The Mimic didn't move. The bioluminescence in the canyon walls around us shifted, the pulse pattern changing — slower, more complex, as if the canyon itself were adjusting its breathing. I felt the shift before I saw it.

Then it happened.

Not words. Not images exactly. A perception — as if someone had taken a sheet of glass I'd been looking through my entire life and tilted it, just slightly, letting me see the refraction pattern for the first time. The pit below the Mimic was not a hole. It was a node. The bioluminescent lines were not decoration. They were readouts. The Mimic was not standing by the pit. It was monitoring it, the way an engineer monitors a gauge.

The canyon was infrastructure. I was standing inside a machine.

My knees went weak. I grabbed the canyon wall, my gloved hand slipping on the bioluminescent surface, and the surface pulsed where I touched it, a single bright flare that ran down the wall and into the pit. As if I'd just pressed a button. As if it had registered me.

The Mimic's exoskeleton shifted, and the pattern changed — more complex now, layered, and I understood that it was responding. Not to my words. To me. To whatever it was that made me different from every other human who'd tried to talk to it.

I stood there, breathing hard, my suit's air recycler humming.

So the inscrutable aliens want to talk to the teenager. Naturally.

The communication — if that's what it was — lasted maybe ten minutes. It wasn't a conversation. It was more like being tuned, the way you'd tune an instrument. Adjustments, calibrations, the Mimic (or whatever was working through the Mimic) figuring out the parameters of a channel it hadn't used before.

By the end of it I could feel the pit network. Not see it — feel it. Like a diagram laid over my proprioception, a sense of geometry extending beneath my feet, downward and outward, hundreds of nodes connected by the matter-streams the survey probes had mapped from orbit. Each node pulsed. Each connection carried something. The whole system hummed.

I looked at the Mimic. The Mimic had not moved. Its exoskeleton shifted one more time, a settling motion, like someone closing a book they've finished reading.

I climbed back up the scree and walked back to the base. The gap in the fence was still there. Nobody had noticed my absence. I sealed my suit and cycled through the airlock and went to my quarters and lay on my bunk and stared at the ceiling — forty-seven panels — and tried to understand what had just happened.

The xenolinguists had algorithms, signal arrays, mathematical primers. They had PhDs and decades of experience and the full institutional weight of the Union's academic establishment. They had achieved nothing.

I had walked into a canyon and been recognised.

Why me? I didn't know. I didn't care. That was the honest answer, and I was aware of how it sounded. The correct response was probably to report this immediately, to find Kessler and say actually, I made contact, here's how, let me show you. The correct response was to be a responsible citizen of the expedition and share what I'd learned for the collective good.

I didn't even consider it. Not for a moment. The thought simply didn't arise.

I lay in the dark and felt the hum of the pit network beneath me, a ghost-sense that was already fading but hadn't quite gone, and I thought: I'll go back.

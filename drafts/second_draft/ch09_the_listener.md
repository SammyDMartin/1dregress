## 9

The last few metres of the tunnel were a hard scramble. I pulled myself up with some difficulty, arms flailing for a moment as I tried to gain purchase on the scree. No way I could have managed it under a standard gravity.

Suddenly, there was another hand in mine, pulling me up to safety. I caught a glimpse of a silhouetted figure standing over me, and then I was being hauled abruptly to the surface by someone strong.

Why wasn't I surprised?

I should have been shocked but somehow, for some strange reason, it felt very natural that something must lie at the end of my journey, and now here he was.

With a final jolt, I came to rest on the bare earth, in the open air, and looked up at my rescuer.

The man stood before me, floating — resting — about half a metre off the ground. Pale-skinned and blandly handsome, maybe only a few years older than me, and as anonymous as anyone I'd ever seen. He wore a grey suit of an old-fashioned cut. No helmet, no mask, no protection of any kind against the thin atmosphere and the radiation that would have killed an unshielded human in hours.

I opened my mouth, but something stopped me. This wasn't a time for flippant remarks.

I studied the newcomer while reaching to wipe dust from my suit visor, searching for any hint of strangeness in his face. There was none, aside from the fact of his existence — standing untouched on the twilit, radiation-blitzed surface of Kailash, where no other human could be.

Had I finally cracked? Had the relief ships arrived weeks early? I'd always thought hallucinations weren't supposed to be this consistent and well-defined, yet he was casting a shadow. Perhaps he wasn't a hallucination at all, but some effect of the inertial fields the Kailash channelled beneath the ground, the same fields that had doomed the outpost.

I looked into his eyes, and despite their absolute ordinariness, found I had trouble meeting them. We stood a few paces apart, quiet and still.

I glanced down at the softscreen on my arm and flicked through the displays. The med readout noted high cortisol, elevated heart rate, and nothing else out of the ordinary. The low-res EF-scan registered nothing abnormal in my brain — unless that was also a hallucination. I looked up. The man was still there.

'Who are you?' I said. The air was too thin to transmit sound, so the suit speakers didn't kick in. He heard me anyway — that inert face broke into a surprisingly warm smile, and he reached out his arms towards me.

'I think you already know,' he said, sounding as though he stood right next to me. I checked the suit recorder's flat trace. His voice sounded real enough.

Hesitantly, I reached up and took his outstretched hand. The hand felt solid. Then sense returned, and I snatched it away.

'Tell me who you are,' I demanded.

'Words are strange things,' the man mused, and something about him compelled me to listen — the infinite patience, as though he had all the time in the world and then some. 'Folding a concept down, flattening it to fit inside bursts of compressed air. Detail is always lost. I am no one; what you should be asking is, what am I?'

'Okay. What are you?'

The man shrugged.

'Everything. I am the space between the stars and the ground beneath your feet.'

He said it the way someone might say I'm an accountant. No grandeur. No drama. Just a fact about himself that happened to be incomprehensible.

'So, what, I'll call you mister everything? Why don't you give me a real answer?'

'Call me the Listener, if it helps,' his lips quirked up.

I'd had enough of being played with, and reached forward, poking him in the centre of his chest.

The Listener didn't pitch backwards, even by the tiniest degree, as if he were anchored to the world's mass. Or the world was anchored to him.

'Is that proof enough?' he said. 'It's as much evidence as you'll get, so why not work with me on this? What is real but what you judge to be real? It's not like there's anyone here who'll disagree with you.'

'You're trying to persuade me to trust you?'

'No, of course not,' the man laughed. 'I don't need to.'

You're confident. I'll give you that.

I thought for a moment before I replied. He clearly wanted me to realise something.

'You said I already know you. I suppose that makes you that impulse — the one in my head?'

He nodded.

'A little extra push here and there — lean over a balcony, go see a window. Things to make you think, to realise who you are.'

'You've been steering me,' I said. 'Since I was a child.'

'Steering is a strong word. I opened doors. You walked through them.'

'The balcony. The spine elevator. The Mimics. All of that was you.'

'The impulse was mine. The action was always yours. You could have ignored every one.'

'But you knew I wouldn't.'

The Listener smiled, and for the first time it wasn't warm. It was the expression of someone who has watched a very long experiment arrive at its predicted conclusion.

'Yes,' he said. 'I knew you wouldn't.'

Something about that honesty — the complete absence of apology — made me angrier than the manipulation itself.

'Two hundred people are dead. Did you know about that too?'

'I know about everything, Eszel. I watched your species learn to split the atom. I watched the Dyn discover their first orrery. I have watched stars form and stars die and I have never once intervened to prevent a death, because death is not a thing I prevent. It is a thing I observe.'

The words should have sounded grandiose. They didn't. They sounded like a weather report.

'Then what's the point of you?'

'I listen,' he said, as if the answer were obvious. 'To everything. Always. And very occasionally, when the patterns warrant it, I find someone worth talking to.'

'And that's me? The teenager who let everyone die?'

'You didn't let them die. You chose to live. Those are different things, even if they feel the same from the inside.'

'They don't feel different.'

'No,' he conceded. 'They wouldn't. Not yet.'

I stared at him. The wind picked up, thin and cold, and my suit rustled. The Listener's grey suit didn't move. Nothing touched him.

'So where do we go from here?'

The Listener gave an ambivalent shrug, and looked at me, expectantly. I met his gaze, with some effort. He was going to make me say it.

'Does that mean you won't help? Me, I mean,' I threw my hands out theatrically. 'I'm in a bit of a bind here.'

'What do you expect me to do?'

'I don't know. Do you have a comm? Calling for help might be a good start.'

'Suppose I did. Suppose they burned hard to get here. What then?'

'I'd wait.'

'Your reprocessors wouldn't hold out, Essie. You'd be dead within fifteen days. Maybe sooner, considering your... state.'

'So that's it? I die out here, stuck with you? You'd let that happen?'

'No.'

I narrowed my eyes. The Listener looked down at me, expression passive.

Does he want me to beg?

'Help me,' I said.

'Trust me, you mean.'

'Fine. I trust you. Happy?'

'No matter what I decide to do?'

The Listener was suddenly serious, any trace of levity gone. His eyes, which had been merely ordinary, were now something else — depthless, old, the eyes of something that had watched from the dark between stars for longer than I could conceive.

'Yes,' I said.

'There will be a cost,' said the Listener. 'Not pain. Something harder than pain. You will see what I see. You will understand what I understand. And once you do, you will not be able to un-know it. You will carry it for the rest of your life, and it will make you strange, even to yourself.'

'I'm already strange.'

'Not like this.'

'Do it anyway.'

Everything went black. A thick, heavy darkness bloomed around me, shot through with sudden bursts of gleaming white, traceries that completed themselves, forming web-like patterns, structures that shifted and interpenetrated, burning with luminance.

It was overwhelming, terrible and beautiful, too complex to understand, too bright to see, too loud to hear. It overwhelmed me, yet I couldn't look away. I tried to scream, and heard nothing but that eternal raw light.

The pattern bloomed like a flower, growing more complex, more incomprehensible, with each new second. And I knew that this was the way the Listener saw the world, that within this web was everything — every star, every particle, every thought, every death. The Kailash system was a single bright node in a web that stretched to the edges of the visible universe and beyond. And within that node, a tiny flare — two hundred lives, ending. Not a tragedy. Not a crime. A fluctuation. A note in a chord so vast that the individual frequency was meaningless.

That was the cost. Seeing the base's destruction from above, from outside, from the perspective of something to which it was less than a rounding error.

I wanted to be horrified. I was not horrified.

Time passed, or didn't. The patterns held me, and I held them, and somewhere in the space between I stopped being Eszel and became something temporarily larger, a receiver tuned to frequencies that human neurology was never built for. I felt my mind stretch, and heard something tear at the edges, and understood that this was the part I would not recover from.

Then the Listener's hand was on my shoulder, and the light dimmed, and the world reassembled itself around me — sky, ground, gravity, the Mule's wreckage somewhere behind me, the thin atmosphere biting at my suit seals.

I staggered backwards, a flood of static bombarding my suit radio and turning into screeching feedback.

I shouted, tripping and falling to the ground.

A second later, the suit triangulated on a signal — one of the emergency beacons in the collapsed base had warmed itself up and screeched out its report. My legs ached beneath me, as if I'd just run a marathon, and something slid over the sky, casting a sharp shadow. The local sun had jumped clear across the sky, from twilight to noonday.

I reeled, searching for the Listener, eyes dashing out towards distant ditches and rock formations. He was gone. Then I looked up and saw a HDF navy landing craft descending through the blue-black sky on spikes of thermal thrust.

It took a few moments before I noticed the time displayed on my HUD. In the moment the Listener vanished, more than three weeks had passed.

The last few metres of the tunnel were a hard scramble. I pulled myself up with some difficulty, arms flailing for a moment as I tried to gain purchase on the scree. No way I could have managed it under a standard gravity.

Suddenly, there was another hand in mine, pulling me up to safety. I caught a glimpse of a silhouetted figure standing over me, and then I was being hauled abruptly to the surface by someone strong.

Why wasn't I surprised?

I should have been shocked but somehow, for some strange reason, it felt very natural that something must lie at the end of my journey, and now here he was.

With a final jolt, I came to rest on the bare earth, in the open air, and looked up at my rescuer.

The man stood before me, floating — resting — about half a metre off the ground. Pale-skinned and blandly handsome, maybe only a few years older than me, and as anonymous as anyone I'd ever seen.

I opened my mouth, but something stopped me. This wasn't a time for flippant remarks.

I studied the newcomer while reaching to wipe dust away from my suit visor, searching for any hint of strangeness in his face. There was none, aside from the fact of his existence — standing untouched on the twilit, radiation-blitzed surface of Kailash, where no other human could be.

Had I finally cracked? Had the relief ships arrived weeks early? I'd always thought hallucinations weren't supposed to be this consistent and well-defined, yet he was casting a shadow. Perhaps he wasn't a normal hallucination at all, but some effect of the inertial fields the Kailash channelled beneath the ground, the same fields that had doomed the outpost I'd just barely escaped from.

I looked into his eyes, and despite their absolute ordinariness, found I had trouble meeting them. Still, the apparition waited. We stood, him and I, a few paces apart, quiet and still.

Slowly, I glanced down at the softscreen on my arm and flicked through the displays. The med readout noted high cortisol, elevated heart rate and nothing else out of the ordinary. The low-res EF-scan registered nothing abnormal in my brain — unless that was also a hallucination. I looked up. The man was still there.

'Who are you?' I said. It was hardly a question, because how could he answer? The air up here was too thin to transmit sound, so the suit speakers didn't kick in. He heard me anyway — that inert face broke into a surprisingly warm smile, and he reached out his arms towards me.

'I think you already know,' he said, sounding as though he stood right next to me. I checked, saw the suit recorder's flat trace. His voice sounded real enough.

Hesitantly, I reached up and took his outstretched hand. The hand felt solid. Then, it was as if sense finally returned, and I snatched it away.

'Tell me who you are,' I demanded.

'Words are strange things,' the man mused, and something about him compelled me to listen — the infinite patience, as though he had all the time in the world and then some. 'Folding a concept down, flattening it to fit inside bursts of compressed air. Detail is always lost. I am no one; what you should be asking is, what am I?'

'Okay,' I said, narrowing my eyes. 'What are you?'

The man shrugged.

'Everything. I am the space between the stars and the ground beneath your feet.'

He didn't say it like a boast. He said it like it was ordinary, like that was his job description.

'So, what, I'll call you mister everything? Why don't you give me a real answer?'

'Call me the Listener, if it helps,' his lips quirked up.

I'd had enough of being played with, and reached forward, poking him in the centre of his chest.

The Listener wore a grey suit of an old-fashioned cut. He didn't pitch backwards, even by the tiniest degree, as if he were anchored to the world's mass. Or the world was anchored to him.

'Is that proof enough?' he said. 'It's as much evidence as you'll get, so why not work with me on this? What is real but what you judge to be real? It's not like there's anyone here who'll disagree with you.'

'You're trying to persuade me to trust you?'

'No, of course not,' the man laughed. 'I don't need to.'

You're confident. I'll give you that.

I thought for a moment before I replied. He clearly wanted me to realise something.

'You said I already know you. I suppose that makes you that impulse — the one in my head?'

He nodded.

'A little extra push here and there — lean over a balcony, go see a window. Things to make you think, to realise who you are.'

So I suppose I must be going mad.

'You're, what, my hidden conscience?'

He laughed.

'I don't think that quite fits, Essie. Perhaps it's close enough.'

'Then tell me why I should trust you.'

'You already trust me, Essie. Have I ever led you astray?'

I thought briefly about the other times in my life where, with hindsight, it seemed like I had been driven on by something inexplicable.

'Well, I'm about to die. Does that count?'

'Some people believe that death is salvation.'

'Yeah, I'm sure they do. But I don't.'

'You're not afraid of death, Eszel — you never have been. It just took you a little while to realise that.'

'You're wrong,' I said it, but I didn't believe it.

'Look at what you've done, Eszel. Step back and just look, imagine someone is watching you and consider how you might appear from the outside. Be honest.'

What had I done? I'd gone out on this pilgrimage alone, caring nothing for the danger, not even thinking about it. How had I done that? What was I?

I tried to imagine the steps that had taken me here, tried to figure out what had driven me onward, and something hollow opened up in me. He was right, truly and entirely.

This whole time, from the very beginning, I'd never been afraid. What did that mean?

'That you aren't ordinary. That you're capable of things that would make most people collapse in on themselves and surrender,' the Listener interjected.

I didn't bother asking if he could read my mind. Dumb question.

'So where do we go from here?'

The Listener gave an ambivalent shrug, and looked at me, expectantly. I met his gaze, with some effort. He was going to make me say it.

'Does that mean you won't help? Me, I mean,' I threw my hands out theatrically. 'I'm in a bit of a bind here.'

'What do you expect me to do?'

'I don't know. Do you have a comm? Calling for help might be a good start.'

'Suppose I did. Suppose they burned hard to get here. What then?'

'I'd wait.'

'Your reprocessors wouldn't hold out, Essie. You'd be dead within fifteen days. Maybe sooner, considering your… state.'

'So that's it? I die out here, stuck with you? You'd let that happen?'

'No.'

I narrowed my eyes. The Listener looked down at me, expression passive.

Does he want me to beg?

'Help me,' I said. 'I trust you.'

'No matter what I decide to do?'

The Listener was suddenly serious, any trace of levity gone.

'Yes.'

Everything went black. A thick, heavy darkness bloomed around me, shot through with sudden bursts of gleaming white, traceries that completed themselves, forming web-like patterns, structures that shifted and interpenetrated, burning with luminance.

It was overwhelming, terrible and beautiful, too complex to understand, too bright to see, too loud to hear. It overwhelmed me, yet I couldn't look away. I tried to scream, and heard nothing but that eternal raw light.

The pattern bloomed like a flower, growing more complex, more incomprehensible, with each new second. And somehow I knew that this was the way the Listener saw the world, that within this web was everything, that I had just begun to understand something true and fundamental, to see things the way that he did. Time passed.

I saw an unseen hand, a tweak, and the burning light-webs shifted, strings snapping, unravelling and changing, configurations shifting. Physicality returned and I was flung somewhere, the roaring in my ears subsiding, ground and sky and gravity returning. I was back on Kailash.

I staggered backwards, a flood of static bombarding my suit radio and turning into screeching feedback.

I shouted, tripping and falling to the ground.

A second later, the suit triangulated on a signal — one of the emergency beacons in the collapsed base had warmed itself up and screeched out its report. My legs ached beneath me, as if I'd just run a marathon, and something slid over the sky, casting a sharp shadow. The local sun had jumped clear across the sky, from twilight to noonday.

I reeled, some part of me still searching for the Listener, eyes dashing out towards distant ditches and rock formations as if he might appear again. Then I looked up and saw a HDF navy landing craft descending through the blue-black sky on spikes of thermal thrust. I was hallucinating, then. No relief ships arriving for months; that had been the defining fact of my life ever since the outpost's destruction.

It took a few moments before I noticed the time displayed on my HUD. In the moment the Listener vanished, more than three weeks had passed.

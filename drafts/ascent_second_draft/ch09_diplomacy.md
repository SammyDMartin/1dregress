Chapter 9: Diplomacy
Vash woke feeling groggy and disorientated, although only a few hours had passed. His bones felt as though they had been stress-tested to breaking point, every vertebra a separate complaint. He strained in the webbing, shifting uncomfortably as he became aware of the sensation of free-fall building in his stomach — that awful weightless hollow that no amount of experience could render tolerable. He groaned as every joint in his body protested the movement. Vash had never enjoyed spaceflight even in its most refined state; his lapse into unconsciousness had been a small mercy, the only kindness the universe had seen fit to offer him today.

In the centre of the screens that surrounded him, an orbital habitat was drawing closer, its scale impossible to judge against the featureless black. The structure was cylindrical, spinning around a non-rotating axial hub, catching occasional flares of reflected sunlight along its flanks. For a moment Vash almost convinced himself that nothing had changed, that this was the world as it had been — but the hab lights were dark and as the lander drew closer still he could see the unmistakable signs of disrepair. Ragged holes pitted the hull where debris had blown through, the edges curled outward like peeled skin. Heavy dark shapes, like fluted columns tipped with knives, floated at various angles around the wheel, silhouetted against the blue curve of the Earth. Dyn spacecraft; the product of a technology and a mode of thought utterly different to that of humanity. Less advanced, with their nuclear reactors and steam-jet nozzles, but more refined — elegant, even, in a way that made human engineering look brutish by comparison.

Stabs of thrust adjusted the course, flashes of hydrazine aligning the craft with the axial docking spine. There was a jarring thud as the docking clamps engaged, a vibration that ran through the hull and settled into Vash's teeth. The silence that followed was broken only by creaking as the lander settled against the hab's superstructure, metal adjusting to metal. Vash had been unsure of what to expect, but it wasn't this — this derelict stillness, this abandonment. He disentangled himself from the webbing and kicked off awkwardly in the direction of the airlock. He saw now that it had been repurposed from a human craft and crudely welded into place, the seams rough and irregular, a patchwork of incompatible technologies forced into reluctant cooperation. The air that cycled into the lock was thick with moisture, a potent mix of exotic hydrocarbons and too much oxygen, all of which only worsened the nausea he felt in microgravity. It tasted green, somehow. Organic and unsettling.

The corridor beyond was deserted and dark, the hub beyond that the same, lit only by the faint glow of what appeared to be a bioluminescent algae-analogue smeared over the walls in uneven streaks. It pulsed with a slow rhythm that might have been deliberate or might have been the metabolic cycle of something alive. The algae had been painted in a rough trail leading deeper into the hab, guiding him to an elevator — a breadcrumb path laid for a species that needed light to navigate. He didn't call out; something about the quiet watchfulness of the place cautioned against it, some animal instinct whispering that he was being observed. He felt the nausea of microgravity fade; it was replaced by the peculiar twisting sensation of coriolis as the elevator began its descent along the hab's spinning interior wall.

As he descended the gravity increased smoothly up to a gee, then further still. One and a half gees, he guessed, but there was no way to tell for certain. His coat grew heavier on his shoulders and his knees began to ache with the extra burden. He could hear condensed moisture begin to trickle down the elevator's walls, rivulets catching what little light there was. Vash came abruptly to a halt before the doors opened and was knocked back by the vista that confronted him.

The habitat interior was almost entirely open, broken only by the remains of hastily torn out bulkheads, arcing up over Vash's head where it disappeared in a mass of storm clouds the colour of old bruises. Rain lashed down, warm and chemical, stinging where it hit his skin. The air smelled of wet earth and something sharper — ozone, perhaps, or the alien equivalent. He squinted into the gloom and in the distance he saw someone; a human figure sat cross-legged, alone in the vast open expanse, impossibly small against the curving landscape. Apprehensively, he made his way forwards, each step careful and deliberate. The convoluted root networks of low Dynic flora threatened to trip him with every step, their tendrils curling instinctively toward the warmth of his ankles. The figure was a woman of perhaps twenty.

'Hello?' he called, feeling faintly ridiculous. His voice echoed strangely in the cavernous space, bouncing off unseen surfaces before dying away. She remained motionless. Vash walked closer, his sense of unease worsening with every metre of ground he closed.

Her head was bowed, features obscured by long dark hair that hung dripping over thin shoulders. She wore a crudely cut smock, which was similarly soaked, the fabric clinging to narrow ribs; the flimsy parasol she held was completely inadequate in the face of the torrential rain, its canopy already sagging under the weight of accumulated water. As Vash approached, the girl looked up, flashing an imitation of a smile — the mechanics correct, the expression hollow.

'Welcome Ambassador Vash, it is good to see you,' said the girl, her voice sweet, but flat, somehow off. The cadence was wrong, the emphasis falling on the wrong syllables, as though the words were being read from a script by someone unfamiliar with the language. The voice from the phone.

'K'txl?' he managed, stumbling over the transliteration.

'That is my name, but I believe Liar to Animals would be the closest translation into your own tongue,' the girl replied without the slightest trace of irony. 'Please, sit.' She smiled again, gesturing at the sodden imitation of a picnic spread — a checked blanket, plates, cups, all arranged with painstaking care, all drenched and ruined.

He did as he was asked, drawing his coat around him against the damp. She passed him a teacup, full to the brim with tepid rainwater. A teabag sat at the bottom, swollen and colourless, long since spent. In spite of himself Vash accepted it, his mind still racing to catch up and struggling to process the entirely unexpected reality he was confronted with. The rain eased off to a steady drizzle, as if on cue.

'It is such wonderful weather we are having today,' the girl remarked, making a show of looking around at the artificial storm with apparent satisfaction. 'Would you like some more tea?' She handed him another teacup before he could answer, water sloshing over the rim.

'— I'm fine for drink,' he replied, accepting the teacup and placing it to one side of him. She didn't seem to even notice the refusal.

'How about food? Are you hungry? Are you tired? Or would you like to have sex with me? Would that not be in the spirit of a mutually beneficial cooperative relationship?' She grasped his hands in hers. Her fingers were cold and slightly damp, the grip too firm, lacking any sense of the natural hesitation of human touch.

'No, no, I'm good.' Vash shivered, pulling away. The girl, Liar to Animals, was watching him, her head tilted to one side at an angle that was just slightly too far, her smile fading by degrees like a dimmer being turned down.

'I realise you must have questions.'

'You're human,' he said abruptly, but without conviction. The words felt inadequate even as he spoke them.

In response the girl delicately gathered her hair to one side, bowing her head in a gesture that was almost demure. Vash leant forwards, his breathing shallow. Scar tissue showed beneath stubble at the base of her skull, ragged and inflamed. Thick cables sprouted from crudely implanted ports behind one ear and at the nape of her neck, the skin around them red and weeping where flesh met metal. They snaked away across the plains towards a dense thicket of foliage, disappearing into the undergrowth like roots seeking water. Vash withdrew unsteadily, feeling the hairs on the back of his neck rise as he scoured the shadows beyond the thicket, his pulse quickening.

'I am Liar to Animals. I am Dyn. This body is little more than a puppet.' She — it — looked at him, meeting his gaze with an unsettling directness. And behind the empty eyes Vash swore he could see something alien looking out at him, peering through borrowed windows with an intelligence that did not belong in a human face.

'That tech is —'

'Repurposed. Much of the functioning of old human technology remains opaque to us, but we have made significant progress through reverse engineering. The field of brain/computer interfaces has been of particular personal interest. We examine the human and Dynic brain, map how they represent concepts and sensations, and construct a bridge between the two. The barriers between our minds become porous, and the more that I experience the more I understand the perversities of human nature.'

'What has happened to her?'

'She lives. When I use this body she sleeps. And when she wakes she will not remember this meeting. You cannot help her.' The words were delivered without cruelty, without compassion — simply stated, as one might describe the workings of any mechanism.

The girl sat completely motionless, hands folded in her lap, the rain beading on her skin and running in thin rivulets down her arms. A silence stretched between them as she watched Vash with that unblinking stare. And then suddenly, without prompting, she asked the last question he would have expected.

'Tell me Vash, do you consider me to be evil?'

He hesitated. The question demanded a careful answer, and Vash was acutely aware that his response was being dissected by an intelligence he could barely comprehend. 'I think that it would be a mistake to moralise.'

'You hate me for my part in what has been done to the people of Earth,' K'txl said flatly.

'I don't hate you, no, though many do. I don't consider you to be evil. But your goals are not my own.'

'What are your goals, Vash?' she asked, cocking her head to one side. He didn't respond to that. K'txl merely smiled lifelessly and took a sip from her watery cup, the gesture eerily precise — a facsimile of an action observed and reproduced without understanding its purpose.

'You fascinate me, Vash — in many ways you're more human than any of them. When you talk of your goals, your motives, you don't speak of self-interest do you? You appeal to something more fundamental. Do you remember what you said to me, the first time we spoke? You said you wanted to do what was right.'

K'txl reached out a thin arm, tracing the outline of Vash's unshaven jaw with a cold finger as if he were a specimen of some fascinating, but dangerous animal — something to be catalogued and studied from behind glass, if only a suitable glass existed. He sat calmly as the rain soaked through the coat, willing himself not to cringe away, though his every instinct screamed at him to recoil.

'Right. Another story you tell yourselves, another thread of the mass hallucination that is human existence; like law or currency or justice,' she continued, speaking as though the very words were an affront, each concept a stone in her mouth that she turned over with distaste. 'You don't live in the real world. You would kill each other for the sake of things which do not exist. War for the sake of ideas. When we learnt of your nature we were… baffled is too insubstantial. We found it impossible. It was insane. We were afraid.'

'Is that why you attacked Earth without warning?'

'No,' she said, expression hardening, the muscles of her borrowed face pulling tight. 'We attacked you in self-defence.'

'How can you claim that?' Vash said, voice rising despite himself. He recoiled from her touch, pausing for a moment before he said something regrettable, forcing his breathing to steady. 'Were you truly so paranoid?'

'Not paranoia, prediction. Humanity had a great knowledge of the rules that govern reality and with that came power. But you used that knowledge to contort reality so that it reflected your waking dreams. We knew that you would not be able to tolerate our existence, that our nature would have been another wrong that had to be righted. So we acted first.'

'I don't understand.'

'I don't understand you, Vash,' she leant forward again, eyes focussing on his with an intensity that was almost physical. 'And that is the problem.' Rain dripped from the tips of the parasol and ran down her face and hair. Vash watched a drop run over her forehead and drip onto her eyeball. She didn't blink. The drop slid across the surface of the eye and disappeared, and still she stared.

'Was that why you brought me here, to study me in the flesh?'

'Wars are fought over conflicts of interest, over differences. As my line flourished they competed with other lines for territory and resources. The victorious lineage would take what is desired. But that is not so with wars of ideas, not when it is our very nature that is the difference. It would be a war of extermination. That is why I must understand you. I must get inside your minds.'

'That's why you've connected yourself to the girl,' Vash said. A horrible realisation was floating just beyond his awareness, like a forgotten word that defied memory, a shape moving beneath dark water.

'Conflict between my domain and others is coming to a head. There are many who wish to end your world and the threat it represents. I must have a solution to the difference between our species. I must understand the decisions you make.'

'The decisions I make,' Vash breathed. The realisation surfaced, and he felt something cold settle in his chest. 'You really weren't going to kill anyone in Conurbation One, were you? That was another experiment you performed on me?'

The girl clapped her hands together and let out a bark of delighted laughter, the sound harsh and mechanical, more like a recording of laughter than the genuine article.

'Yes, now you understand! This thing you call right is the key — I observed and recorded your words as you deliberated my ultimatum. I saw inside your mind, just as I am now inside the mind of this human girl. And so we make progress.'

Behind Vash's back, where his arms rested on the picnic mat, his hands curled together, knuckles whitening. It lasted only a moment, before the fingers relaxed. He was another conduit for the still-unseen K'txl, just like this girl; another experiment, another specimen pinned to a board.

'Hundreds of people died.'

'Yes,' K'txl said. She took another sip from the cup, unhurried, indifferent. 'For us, there is no fear in individual death.'

'The Dyn don't fear death?' Vash said incredulously. 'All of evolution is against that —'

'A line and domain may persist, even when a single Dyn is dead. If one dies for the sake of one's line then all that matters continues after death. What is there to fear but the end of the line? And I do fear that Vash, make no mistake. I have been attacked.'

'You've been attacked? When did this happen?'

'My other, the next of my line,' K'txl corrected, and for the first time Vash thought he detected something that might have been genuine urgency beneath the flat affect. 'The night after I summoned you here he was abducted from my domain by a group of rebels.'

'This other, he is your son?'

'The first of my sons, the one who will become Liar to Animals when this self dies. I have invested much in the shaping of his mind, and would not wish to see his life go to waste. You will recover him for me.'

'I'm not your soldier.'

'You will lead a party... I come to you directly because those that would challenge me cannot be allowed to know. They are a threat to us both. Consider this the next experiment, if you like.' Her voice carried no menace, no entreaty — merely the calm expectation of compliance, as one might instruct a tool to perform its function.

'What happens if I fail?'

The girl's lips twisted up again in that pale imitation of a smile, the expression sitting on her face like a mask that didn't quite fit.

'I will be forced to call for a more immediate response to our differences.'

The image on the memory machine's screen was gray and blurred by compression artefacts, the resolution barely adequate for the purpose. Vash watched the drone footage for a second time, almost unable to believe what he was seeing.

A heavily modified crawler, the kind isolated conurbations used to move essential supplies and lay roads, was framed in the centre of the screen. It burst from a warehouse in a cloud of dust and shattered masonry, a blurry but unmistakably alien figure lashed to the top of it like Prometheus chained to the rocks, before ploughing into the jungle as it travelled north-east, leaving behind a trail of destruction — a scar of flattened vegetation and churned earth that would take months to heal. Corbin's finger tapped on the screen, the nail clicking against the glass.

'That'll be our missing Dyn. The first reports arrived while you were off-world; a breach in one of the largest reservations, not far from Conurbation Fifteen. We tracked their vehicle back to the warehouse but by the time Enforcers arrived it was too risky to engage — it's not as though we can use restraint and precision against a mobile fortress. I checked the crawler's registration — it was pulled out of circulation for maintenance just over a fortnight back...'

'Do we still have eyes on the crawler?'

'Yes, they didn't seem to have any problem with the drone. They're not trying to hide, but I've ordered the Enforcers in pursuit to hang back.'

'Good,' said Vash, taking a deep breath. 'Good work.' Corbin nodded, accepting the praise with characteristic understatement. Grey afternoon light filtered into his office through the broad window, casting long shadows across the cluttered desk and the maps pinned to the far wall.

'No matter what weapons those revolutionaries have improvised or stolen from us, they can't expect to hold out for long... Just how important is this Dyn? As diplomatic incidents go, it can't get much worse than a bunch of rebels driving around with the equivalent of a giant middle finger pointed at the sky. It would be much simpler if we could just—'

'Don't even consider it,' Vash interrupted. 'The Dyn have demanded we recover it — him — alive and unharmed. She implied if we didn't there would be dire consequences.'

'She?'

'The Dyn leaders are female.'

'You actually met it, face to face; what was that like?'

'I'd rather not say.' The words came out more clipped than he'd intended. Corbin studied him for a moment, then had the grace to let it go.

Vash typed more commands into the keyboard, freezing the feed with the bulk of the vehicle in the middle of the screen. A sigil like a jagged mountain was visible, daubed across the entire top deck in crude, defiant strokes. It was hauntingly familiar.

Corbin broke the silence.

'Sir... this entire situation doesn't make sense. Hijacking a crawler, kidnapping a Dyn and daubing that sign on the deck — it's insane. The whole thing is so overt that it's as if they want us to notice.'

Vash stared, transfixed at that symbol. It didn't belong in this time or place. He'd last seen that symbol on the hand of a man long-dead, who'd spoken of 'warseeds' and 'dragon's teeth'. What was it doing here, painted on the hull of a stolen crawler in a world that had forgotten what it meant?

'Are you alright?'

'Does that symbol mean anything to you?' Vash asked, deflecting the concern.

'I spoke to the local Director. The enforcers have been scraping that sign off the walls of conurbations in the region for a couple of months. This new rebel group has clearly emerged recently. Why, is it important?'

'Perhaps it is just coincidence. If not, it may be more important than anything else.'

'You've lost me, sir.'

Vash reached beneath to the keyboard, shifting the image to frame the symbol in the centre. He stared at it — that jagged, angular shape — and felt the weight of centuries pressing down on him.

'Is that a mountain or a jagged tooth?' Vash asked, innocently. 'Could it be, say, a dragon's tooth?'

Corbin sighed, squinting down at the image with the careful attention of a man who suspected he was being led somewhere.

'Now that you've put the suggestion in my mind I can't be sure what I'm seeing. I suppose it could be the canine of some predator. What's the significance of that?'

Vash let out a hollow laugh then abruptly cut himself off when he saw Corbin's expression — the sharp, searching look of a man whose curiosity had been weaponised.

'I suppose the Enforcer academies skipped the classical education. It's an old myth. The goddess of wisdom, Athena, devised a plan to sow dragon's teeth into the ground and the teeth grew into fully-fledged warriors.'

Corbin didn't say anything for a moment, weighing his words with the precision of a man who understood that careless speech could be as dangerous as a loaded weapon.

'Sir, with all due respect, the Dyn ships are already descending into lower orbits — look at the sky and you can see their drives flaring. We don't have time for pondering the origins and implications of the symbols of various insurrectionist groups. Just tell me what your point is in plain Americano and I'll lead the party — I'm sure you could do with a rest.'

Vash considered for a moment whether to share his suspicions, but something stopped him. A reluctance he couldn't quite name — not distrust, not exactly, but something more cautious than that. A sense that this particular knowledge was dangerous in proportion to how widely it was shared.

'Get gunships prepared for departure,' he said, switching off the memory machine with a dull whirr. 'We're going to meet the revolution.'

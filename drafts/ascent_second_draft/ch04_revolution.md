His head was throbbing and Jan could taste blood in his mouth. The pain came in slow, nauseating waves, each one peaking with his heartbeat. He probed his split lip with his tongue and tried to work out how long he'd been unconscious.
'We should have just shot the bastard in the first place,' a male voice said from somewhere to his left. 'Easier all round. The wastelander's only gonna be trouble.'
Jan opened his eyes, squinting in the harsh sodium light that stabbed at him from a fixture overhead. He tried to raise his hands to block the glare, but he couldn't; they were tied behind his back, the bindings cutting into his wrists with each involuntary movement.
'How do you know he's a wastelander?' said another voice, a woman's. It was coming from almost directly behind him, and Jan could hear the creak of a chair as she shifted her weight.
'Oh, come on Tuva. Some random looking like he's just crawled out the jungle wanders into town, pisses off a squad of Enforcers and leads them right to our door. Who else is it gonna be, a bloody accountant? He's either a liability or it was a set up and he's working for Arco. Either way we should just get rid of him.'
Slowly his eyes adjusted to the glare. It wasn't possible to make out much of the room — he was sat facing one wall, the concrete rough and stained with what might have been oil or something worse — but it seemed big given the way their voices carried, each syllable bouncing off hard surfaces and returning a half-second later. That same mountain, stencilled on the wall in front of him in white paint. Except that the more he looked at it the less it looked like a mountain. There was something too purposeful about the slight curvature, its serrated edges too regular, like a vast tooth protruding from the earth.
'You're not thinking straight. Why would Arco bother with the whole chase and a shootout shtick? If they knew where we were we'd have been infiltrated already. And whoever he is the guy took out two of them with a dotbow. He could be useful.'
'Please,' the first voice sneered. The man walked into the corner of Jan's vision — heavy boots, stained cargo trousers, the glint of something metal at his hip — and shook him roughly by the shoulder. Jan felt the cold metal of a knife against his neck, the edge resting just above his collarbone.
'You a wastelander? Just nod; say one word and I'll cut you.'
Jan nodded, sweat prickling his forehead. He could smell the man now — unwashed skin and gun oil, the sour tang of cheap alcohol.
'You're not working for Arco?' his captor hissed. He was a big man, well-muscled by a lifetime of hard labour, with a face that had been rearranged more than once. Scar tissue ridged the bridge of his nose.
Jan shook his head, though he didn't expect it to make much of a difference. He'd been in similar situations before — waking up bound in some stranger's territory, one wrong word away from a slit throat — and the only thing to do was to watch for opportunities and stay capable of taking them.
'Oh, leave it Pao,' said the woman's voice. 'What kind of interrogation is that?'
'You hot for him or something Tuva? Like a bit of rough?' goaded Pao, but the knife didn't move from Jan's throat.
She laughed — a short, derisive bark.
'Seriously though, think about it. We keep him around we're gonna have to spend every minute of every day watching him. Do you want that? We do it careful and the boss doesn't even have to know — we use one of the Arco standard rifles we can make it look like he died from an earlier injury.'
'I suppose...' Tuva trailed off, as though she was genuinely considering the proposition. Jan tensed, his bound hands curling into fists behind his back. He measured the distance to Pao's chin and wondered if he might be able to headbutt the man hard enough to buy himself a few seconds.
'What do you suppose?' came a new voice and the sound of a heavy door swinging open on rusted hinges. The sodium light from the corridor beyond threw a long shadow across the floor. 'Come now, this isn't any way to treat a guest. Untie him.'
'He's not a bloody pet. We should find out what he knows and—'
'Untie him, Pao,' the third voice repeated, quiet but with an authority that made the bigger man's hand drop away from the knife. Jan felt the ties cut as Pao stepped behind him, the sudden rush of blood back into his fingers almost painful. 'Help him up, you two. Gently!'
The guards gripped Jan under each arm and lifted him into a standing position, sending shooting pains through his aching muscles. They turned him to face the source of the new voice, and Jan blinked, trying to focus.
The man was tall and dressed in mismatched camo only slightly less patched and shabby than that of the others. A large bandolier, empty as far as Jan could make out, was slung over his shoulder, and a beret set at a jaunty angle sat on a thick shock of curly black hair. His eyes were bright and restless, taking in everything at once. Jan was surprised by how young they all looked. Barely older than Eva. The thought hit him like a fist in the chest and he pushed it away before it could take hold.
'I am Christo and this is the Revolution,' the man said, gesturing to the two others with a grandiose sweep of his arm. His grin was infectious, absurd given the circumstances — the kind of grin that belonged on a showman, not a revolutionary in a concrete warehouse that smelt of diesel and fear. Jan suppressed a mad urge to laugh.
'You're going to bring down Arco; all three of you?'
'Ha, no,' Christo laughed, and the sound was genuine, ringing off the warehouse walls. 'What would the point of that be?'
Jan stared at him in utter confusion, rubbing the raw skin of his wrists. 'So you're just bandits?' Christo's lips twisted down faintly, the first crack in his showman's composure.
'I suppose you think Arco is the source of all your woes?'
'My what?'
'You blame Arco for all this,' Christo swept his arm again, as if to indicate the world in general — the warehouse, the wasteland beyond it, the entire rotting edifice of human civilisation on occupied Earth. Jan nodded, because of course he did. Everyone did.
'Do you not wonder why Arco exists? Why our ambassador merely delivers the demands of those above, never negotiating? Why the false life spreads, never retreating? The answers are obvious if you think to ask the right questions.'
'Christo, what the hell are you playing at?' said Tuva, stepping into view for the first time. She was small and wiry, with close-cropped hair and the careful eyes of someone who'd learned young that attention to detail was the difference between breathing and not. 'We shouldn't be telling him anything.'
'This man will know his way around the jungle,' Christo replied, turning back to Jan. There was a calculating edge beneath the warmth now, a quick intelligence assessing Jan's potential. 'You've seen the reservations before right?'
Jan nodded, trying to ignore a growing sense of unease, just keeping his options open. He'd learned to read people in the wasteland — learned to tell the dreamers from the dangerous — and Christo was somehow both at once.
'Of course he has,' Tuva replied patiently, as if she was explaining something to a child. 'Which is why we make him tell us the route, then leave him here. If the information's good then maybe we'll have a reason to trust him.'
'And how would you make him Tuva?' Christo asked, a hard edge creeping into his voice. The grin vanished. 'Hurt him until he cooperates? I don't think so.'
'Christo, you're making a—' Tuva began, but Christo made a cutting gesture and she trailed off sullenly, folding her arms. He grinned suddenly, the tension dissipating as quickly as it had appeared, like a storm front that had changed its mind.
'I'm not interested in coercion. Jan is not going to join us because he's made to. Jan is going to join us because he will want to. And besides,' he spread his arms wide, palms up, the gesture almost priestly, 'That's not what the heroes do!'
Christo watched Jan expectantly, meeting his gaze with those bright, restless eyes. The warehouse was quiet except for the distant hum of machinery and the soft drip of water somewhere in the walls. Jan became aware that his heart was beating very fast.
'So if you don't want to bring down Arco, then who?' asked Jan hesitantly. He thought of Eva, of his frantic, hopeless fight against the Enforcers on that terrible day — the way they'd knocked him aside without breaking stride, as though he weighed nothing, as though he was nothing. 'Why not Arco?'
'Now those, those are the right questions. And isn't the answer obvious? Who cares who rules the world when they own it?' asked Christo. He pointed an accusing finger at the sky — or rather, at the warehouse ceiling, as though he could see through the concrete and the corrugated iron and the grey overcast to what lay beyond. 'We're bringing them down.'
Like a fool, Jan actually glanced up at the warehouse ceiling. Rust stains wept from the rivets and a pigeon shifted nervously on a crossbeam. Then he realised what Christo meant and could no longer help himself. He folded up, laughing hysterically — great whooping gasps that hurt his bruised ribs and made Pao twitch for his knife.
'You might as well try and bring down the sky,' Jan said, trying to control himself, wiping his eyes with the back of his hand. After everything he'd been through — the hunger, the chase, the Enforcers, waking up tied to a chair with a knife at his throat — it was almost a relief to realise that they were all just crazy. 'And I suppose you want me to help?'
'You've got it,' Christo winked, making a gun shape with his index finger and thumb, pointing it up at the roof and miming pulling a trigger. But something about his affect was deadly serious. The grin was still there, but behind it, in the set of his jaw and the stillness of his eyes, there was a conviction that Jan recognised because he'd seen it before — in the faces of men and women who had decided to die for something. 'We're going to tear them from the sky and end this nightmare.'
Jan's laughter died in his throat. The warehouse felt suddenly smaller, the air thicker. He searched for something to say, aware that Tuva and Pao were watching him intently.
'What do you need me for?'
'I need you to lead us back into the jungle, to a reservation. We're going to meet the Dyn.'

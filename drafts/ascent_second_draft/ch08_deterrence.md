Chapter 8: Deterrence
Jan lost track of the violent turns and twists Aurelie executed as they attempted to evade their hypothetical pursuers. The car skidded over a rocky outcrop and spun wildly, the chassis groaning in protest, but with a sharp turn of the wheel Aurelie brought it back under control. Its headlights were dipped, making it almost impossible to see more than a few metres ahead, yet somehow they never collided with anything truly dangerous. Aurelie drove as though she could see in the dark — or as though the road itself was accommodating her.
Christo stripped off his jacket, revealing a long open wound on his shoulder, the blood dark against his skin and already beginning to clot.
'Don't look so worried, it's just a graze. The guards were half asleep, but with the volume of fire they were pumping out, one of them had to get lucky.'
'How many were on you?' Jan said, dumbfounded. The car swerved again and they were nearly thrown out of their seats, Jan bracing himself against the dashboard with one hand.
'Not more than twenty,' Christo replied nonchalantly, as though discussing the weather. 'They were much more cautious once we had our hostage.'
Reaching underneath the seat with his good arm, Christo opened a first aid kit and handed a bottle of antiseptic and dressing to Jan. Without a word, Jan raised the bottle and tipped the strong-smelling substance onto the revolutionary's shoulder, watching Christo's jaw clench as the liquid hit raw flesh. He reached up to apply the dressing. His first attempt was interrupted as the transport skipped over a low stream, the impact jarring through his spine, but on his second attempt the dressing stuck. Christo gritted his teeth and nodded his thanks.
'What's the plan here?' Jan demanded, raising his voice over the engine noise. 'Because you'll be lucky if those above just kill you after they discover there's one of them in the flatbed of this truck.'
'The Dyn are paranoid and protective of their own; observe how carefully they wall themselves off from the outside world,' said Aurelie, her eyes never leaving the dark track ahead. Her voice was calm, measured, utterly at odds with the violence of her driving. 'They will be cautious because we have a valuable hostage. Arco will receive orders to stand off and observe.'
'I know how a hostage works!' Jan exploded. 'But sooner or later they're going to attempt to retrieve it. You've just made yourselves Arco's priority!'
'It won't matter, we just need Arco to hesitate. Just a few days. Until—' Christo cut himself off at a brief glance from Aurelie, a flash of communication between them that Jan couldn't read. Jan felt a jolt of worry; what counted as a step up from raiding an alien reservation and taking one of the Dyn hostage? He slumped back into the seat, trying to calm jangling nerves, the camera-gun heavy and warm in his lap.
Without warning, Aurelie swerved off the rough track they'd been following and ploughed deep into the undergrowth. Fronds of false life lashed against the windscreen, leaving sticky smears where they hit, and the car bucked and jolted as it crushed smaller growths beneath its wheels.
'What's wrong?' Christo shouted, bracing against the roof.
'Aircraft, sounds like a drone,' answered Aurelie, slamming on the brakes as the car buried itself in a dense thicket. The false-life closed around them like a fist. She killed the lights and engine. 'Help me with the camo.'
Jan complied without question, taking his cue from Christo. Together they hastily fumbled the camo sheet over the vehicle, the material surprisingly light in his hands, its surface rough and mottled with patterns that seemed to shift as he moved it. They ignored the shrill clicking emanating from the thing lashed down in the flatbed — louder now, more agitated, as though it could sense their fear. They clambered back into the darkened cab, pulling the sheet down behind them.
For a moment Jan couldn't hear anything beyond the prisoner's alien clicking and their own heavy breathing, amplified in the enclosed space. But then he heard the aircraft, an almost inaudible low droning, growing closer. The sound rose and fell as it circled above, making one pass after another, each circuit tightening. Jan imagined the drone's cameras sweeping the canopy below, sensors probing through the layers of false vegetation.
'Are you sure it can't see us?' hissed Jan, his hand tightening on the camera-gun.
'The camo will hide us, it'll even keep our heat signature hidden for a bit, but if the drone operators can hear over the sound of their own engines then we're screwed,' cursed Christo, his injured shoulder pressed against the seat. 'Any way to shut up our passenger, Aurelie?'
Sat in the pitch black confines of the cab the Dyn sounded uncomfortably close, still singing its alien song like some demented cicada. The clicking had taken on a rhythmic quality, almost like speech — though whether it was calling for help or simply expressing distress, Jan had no way of knowing.
'Unfortunately Dynic physiology makes that challenging,' she replied, her voice betraying nothing. 'But it's unlikely that the drone's even equipped with mics. Just wait, it will pass.'
Christo fidgeted impatiently in the seat besides her. The minutes oozed by, the cab growing ever more stuffy, the air thick with the smell of sweat and antiseptic and the faint chemical tang of the false-life pressing against the windows. Jan counted his own heartbeats. Finally the drone moved on and at last the thing in the flatbed quietened. Whether it had simply exhausted itself or realised the futility of its actions, Jan neither knew nor cared. Aurelie insisted they wait another couple of minutes before revealing themselves.

They finally reemerged from the jungle a couple of hours later, the dark canopy replaced by the half-light of the predawn sky. The eastern horizon was beginning to pale, a thin line of grey separating earth from cloud. Jan scanned the sky nervously. The sun, still below the horizon, glinted off the wings of a distant drone. It circled like some vast carrion bird, patient and watchful.
'Not to worry,' Christo said, following Jan's gaze as Aurelie pulled into a makeshift depot. 'We'll be moving on soon enough.' Guards displaying the revolutionary insignia — that same serrated mountain stencilled in white — waved them in through a gap in the perimeter fence.
'I thought we were returning to Fifteen,' Jan said, looking to Christo for an answer.
'Ha, no, way too much heat! Besides, that part of the journey is over,' the man smiled, and there was something in his expression that made Jan's skin prickle.
The car wound its way through piled construction materials and industrial machinery — pallets of riveted steel plate, coils of heavy cable, drums of fuel stencilled with hazard markings — until Aurelie stopped alongside a vast warehouse that dwarfed everything around it.
Pao emerged from behind a sheet of camouflage netting, dressed in cutoff fatigues. A long bayoneted rifle hung from a shoulder strap. He flicked on a torch and waved it in through the cab windows, dazzling Jan.
'You took your time,' he said with a scowl. Then his eyes shifted to the still-twitching shape in the flatbed and his face bleached, the colour draining from it like water from a cracked jug. 'First that wastelander and now this... You just taking in anything out the jungle these days?'
'Oh, do shut up,' Christo grinned as he climbed out of the car, wincing as his injured shoulder caught on the doorframe. He jabbed a hand in the direction of the covered alien. 'Get some of the others out here and tie the Dyn down up top.'
'Hell, I wondered what had Arco's spotters so riled up, but you've cracked if you think we're taking that onboard—'
'Just get it done, Pao. Lash it down securely where anyone who might be watching from, say, five hundred kilometers up, should be able to spot it. Comprende?'
'There's no way we'll get away with this,' Pao said, his hands shifting instinctively in the direction of his rifle.
'This is the only way we'll get away with this,' Christo snapped, voice hardening. The two men stared at each other for a long moment before Pao looked away.
Pao grunted something unintelligible, lifting the camouflage netting to allow Jan, Aurelie and Christo to pass into the warehouse. It reminded Jan of a factory — a vast, clanging space of heat and noise and purposeful activity. A column of rebels passed in the opposite direction, some pausing to nod or greet Christo with obvious reverence, while Aurelie somehow contrived to remain invisible even in the centre of the crush. It was bewildering; there was more heavy machinery than Jan had ever seen in one place before. Hot steam gushed from loose joints and sparks cascaded from welding torches being applied to a structure that filled the warehouse from wall to wall, its true shape impossible to discern beneath the scaffolding. There was a sense of anticipation that was almost tangible — the kind of energy that gathers before a storm. Whatever it was they were working on was almost complete.
Jan didn't bother to ask where they were going next or how they were going to get there; he knew Christo well enough to realise he wouldn't give a straight answer to such questions. Instead, without instruction or inclination to the contrary, he followed Christo and Aurelie up a set of metal stairs that wound upwards for what had to be more than a dozen metres, their boots ringing on each step. They emerged into a raised control room bristling with instruments and screens, many of them dark or displaying static.
Whilst Aurelie and Christo examined the banks of controls Jan looked down at the wide deck beneath, covered with netting. Small lights on the deck provided the only illumination, making the shape of the huge structure hard to discern. Men and women rushed to drag the netting away, and as the material fell, sections of the thing beneath were revealed in pieces — heavy armour plating, angular turrets, the dark mouths of gun barrels.
'It's a fort,' Jan said, incredulous.
'You could say that,' Christo said, a note of pride creeping into his voice. 'It's certainly built to keep the outside world... outside. But we'll need to be more proactive about that from now on. Thick walls won't be enough.'
A klaxon sounded, its two-tone wail echoing through the warehouse. Aurelie turned to Christo.
'They've found us,' she said. He merely nodded, unsurprised.
Jan heard shouts filtering up from the deck as the remaining netting was thrown from the flanks, revealing slats of grey armour beneath. He thought he recognised Pao's voice from somewhere behind, yelling orders to tie the prisoner down.
A cascade of bright spotlights ignited out of nowhere, illuminating the deck fully. Jan squinted against the sudden glare and saw a huge white sawtoothed shape stretching across the entire deck — the ubiquitous insignia of the revolution, painted across armour plating that was thick enough to stop anything short of an orbital strike.
'There's no way anyone could ignore what we're about to do, you see,' Christo said with a nonchalant shrug, watching the activity below with those bright, restless eyes. 'So there's no more point in being subtle.'
Jan gaped as more lights came on, banks of floods that revealed the full size of the base. He could now see that the deck stretched far ahead of him, narrowing to a blunt point. It tapered like the prow of some enormous vessel. The deck started to rumble as if all the heavy piping beneath them was breaking apart, a vibration that climbed through the soles of his boots and into his bones.
'There'll be here in a couple of minutes — helicopters, at least fifty soldiers. Drones are already overhead,' Aurelie reported, scanning a screen.
The rumbling grew in intensity until it was a roar, drowning out the klaxon, drowning out everything.
'Aurelie, everything's ready. Let's get going!' Christo shouted, grinning wide enough to split his face. 'Launch!' he cried into a mic, to cheers from the occupied decks below.
The world-ending roar reached a climax and Jan stared ahead as the entire structure lurched forward, the warehouse wall ahead of them tearing like paper, corrugated steel peeling away on both sides. The vast vehicle — no, not a fort, a landship — departed from its hiding place with ponderous acceleration, annihilating a grove of false-life trees and trailing discarded netting like a great beast shaking off its sleep. Jan's mind flashed back to the days before, to the trail of uprooted trees he'd spotted just before his first encounter with the revolution. He was beginning to understand how, but not why.
'Do you want the drones shot down?' asked Aurelie, a wry smile playing across her lips as the control room shuddered around them.
'Let them watch!' Christo called back, laughing over the thunder of the engines.
'You're mad,' Jan shouted, shaking his head, but laughing all the same — because what else could you do, when a man you'd met three days ago was driving a mobile fortress through the jungle towards an alien occupation force?
'My friend, it is the world around us that is mad,' Christo winked, steadying himself against the console. 'What would be crazy is reacting to this insanity in an ordinary manner.'

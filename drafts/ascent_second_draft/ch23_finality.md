Chapter 23: Finality
Jan's eyes opened onto blackness and for a few disoriented moments he wondered whether they really were open at all. He blinked, or thought he blinked — in the absolute dark there was no way to tell — and sat still, waiting for his eyes to adjust. But the suffocating darkness never lifted. There was no glimmer, no variation in the void, nothing for his retinas to latch onto. Just black, total and complete, as though someone had drawn a curtain across the world.
He felt around blindly, his hands brushing over something sticky on the floor, as he noticed for the first time the metallic taste in his mouth, the deep ache in his chest that sharpened with each breath into something white-hot. With a growing sense of dread he touched the source of the pain, probing gingerly with trembling fingers. It felt wet and wrong — torn fabric, torn flesh beneath, the heat of exposed tissue against the cold air. He wasn't going to be able to move far.
'Aurelie,' he croaked. His voice sounded small and hollow, like Eva's after a visit from her Shadows.
There was no reply. The silence pressed in around him, dense and absolute, broken only by the sound of his own breathing and the distant, irregular dripping of water somewhere in the walls. Where was Aurelie? Had she made it out alive, leaving him trapped here in this tiny bubble of air beneath a mountain of water, only so he could die a little slower? Could he blame her after what he'd done?
What had he done?
He had given them the seconds they needed. He had launched himself at the Dyn, knowing what it would cost — knowing that the weapon would fire, that the sky would empty, that Eva would die. He had chosen the world over his daughter, and the enormity of that choice sat on his chest heavier than any wound.
Had he redeemed himself? Would he ever forgive himself?
Jan felt lightheaded, his thoughts losing their focus and beginning to loop, circling back to the same images — Eva's face, the Dyn's claws, Vash squeezing his eyes shut as the Emissary materialised. He needed to staunch the bleeding. Tearing his shirt into strips with clumsy, blood-slicked fingers, he did his best to bandage the wounds, hissing through his teeth as each movement sent new jolts of agony through his torso.
'Aurelie!' he called again, as loud as he dared. Only the echo of his own voice came back, flat and dead against the strange walls of this underwater tomb.
Maybe she was lying somewhere else in the labyrinth of tunnels, bleeding out like him. Maybe she was already dead. The unsettling image of the Dyn stalking through the darkened rooms, hunting down the survivors of its bloody rampage, came to him unbidden. He fumbled for his knife, even as he pushed the thought aside. The handle was slippery with blood — his own, or the Dyn's, or some mixture of both.
Jan crawled along the floor, every movement agony, his hands feeling blindly ahead of him until he came up against a wall. The surface was smooth and slightly warm to the touch, alive in some way he couldn't identify. His breathing ragged, he propped himself up against it, knife in hand, gripping it tight. The posture was familiar — how many nights had he spent exactly like this in the wasteland, back against a tree or a rock, weapon in hand, watching the dark for threats? But those nights he had always been watching over Eva.
He wouldn't stand a chance if the Dyn came for him but that didn't matter anymore — it was all over, one way or another. That was what mattered. Christo's revolution had triumphed against all odds. They'd won, and this was what came after. All he had to do now was survive for a little longer. Just hold on.
Jan thought about how it had come to this, trying to piece together the events that had led him here, but, although he tried, it made little sense. It was as though he had been a spectator, a bystander, watching his own body blunder from one crisis to another, without any real autonomy. He had been swept up in other people's plans ever since he had left the jungle — Christo's grand vision, Aurelie's calculated strategy, Vash's agonised diplomacy. He'd been a pawn in a game played by minds vaster and older than his own, and the cruellest part was that he'd gone willingly, hoping that somewhere in the machinery of their schemes there might be room for one small, personal miracle.
But now the others were gone and whilst he lived for now the only end he had ever pursued was a lost cause. They had killed Eva. They had killed her, and he had helped them. He had been the one to give them the time they needed. Maybe it was the right thing to do. Maybe he had spared her a worse fate — a lifetime imprisoned in an alien mind, her identity slowly dissolving into something that was neither human nor Dyn. It didn't make the pain any easier to bear. Nothing could.
He thought of the day by the stream, Eva holding up the Dyn lens, the light filtering through it. The way she had giggled when she wiped her hands on him. He thought of the night terrors and the stars above the palisade, and the promise he had made. I'll protect you. He had broken that promise, and the breaking of it had broken something in him too, something that all the bandages in the world couldn't repair.
Jan didn't know how long he sat there, slumped against the wall in the dark, but after a while sheer exhaustion and sensory deprivation began to set in, his eyes unseeing, his ears unhearing. Even the pain had become a background throb, a dull pulse that kept time with his heartbeat, growing fainter. He had to keep shifting his position and straining his eyes open but try as he might, he inevitably drifted into unconsciousness…
He dreamt the same dreams he always did, but they never descended into nightmares. Just memories — the settlement, the stream, Eva's investigations, the false plants swaying in the wind. He could happily let himself slip away into the past, let it enfold him, let it carry him somewhere warm and bright where none of this had happened…
He awoke.
He could see; there was light. It was soft and warm and sourceless, like the light in the warseed chamber but gentler, kinder. Eva knelt beside him. She appeared older, as she would be now, her face in clearer focus than the hands he reached out to her with. She looked so like her mother. He gasped, the sound catching in his ruined throat.
Was he hallucinating? Was this some ghost emanating from the dying machinery that entombed him? How could he ever know? And did it matter?
'You're alive?'
She didn't answer, a playful smile on her lips, her hands on his. The warmth of her touch felt real — impossibly, achingly real. Jan knew none of it was real, but he decided to just go ahead and believe in it anyway. He had earned that much.
It's real, she assured him, proving nothing. You'll see me again. I promise.
Then she was gone, the apparition dissolving into light.
The light stabbed at him, then suddenly it was drenching everything, flooding into the room from a direction that hadn't existed moments before. Armoured figures, indistinct against the brilliant electric light, emerged through the wall. Flashes of colour that Jan's starved mind was unable to process filled his vision. Voices, shouting over one another, the words blurring into noise. Torch beams skittered around madly, piercing the shadows that still clung in the recesses of the chamber, resting on a body, smears of blood across the floor. Jan's mind ran around inside his dying brain, desperately reaching for something he could understand, something he could cling on to.
One of the figures loomed larger in Jan's vision. The figure, a mask covering his face, raised a blocky object — a medical scanner, its display casting a faint blue glow.
'This one's still alive,' he murmured to a colleague, disbelief evident in his voice. 'Haemorrhaging almost everywhere, collapsed lung, five broken ribs, irregular heartbeat and neural activity in decline. Horrible wounds in his abdomen. Kidneys aren't going to be much good…'
'No way he will make it,' replied the colleague as he examined the other body. 'Just shoot him full of morphine; nice and humane.'
'We have to try,' exclaimed the man standing over Jan, reaching for something Jan couldn't see. 'He may be our only chance of finding out what happened here. The Ambassador was very clear that he wanted all survivors —'
'Not Ambassador anymore, remember?'
The man produced a cuff, like a neck brace lined with needles and pads, fitting it around Jan's neck with careful, practised hands. The voices of the figures blurred together and sensation ebbed away, the pain retreating like a tide going out. Jan found himself slipping out of consciousness, perhaps for the last time. He smiled. He was going to see his daughter again.

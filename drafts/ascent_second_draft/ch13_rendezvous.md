Chapter 13: Rendezvous
Vash studied the latest image of their target, the printout still warm from the machine. Cliffs divided land from sea in a jagged line, men and materiel clustered on the beach below like insects swarming a wound. The crawler lay at the cliff edge, the symbol painted on its top deck still clearly visible, its meaning apparent only to him — a secret message broadcast to the sky, hidden in plain sight.

'When was this taken?' he asked, glancing up.

'Not two minutes ago,' replied the Enforcer nearest to him; the head of the pack, as far as Vash could discern. Dias, according to the name stitched on the shoulder of his fatigues, the lettering faded from years of wear. 'From orbit?' Vash asked, momentarily perplexed. Dias shook his head.

'High altitude drone, solar powered. They shouldn't be able to see it. I thought you knew, sir — Corbin had it requisitioned for us. Better to not have to rely on the Dyn for recon.'

Beneath them equatorial jungle the colour of a bruise stretched away to the horizon in every direction, an undulating carpet of false life, with Dynic flora already recolonising the swathe of destruction cut by the crawler. The alien vegetation moved with an urgency that bordered on eagerness, tendrils stretching across the torn earth, sealing the wound. Within a couple of days there would be no indication it had passed this way.

'When do we get there?'

'Not long now, only another ten minutes of flight time, though we'll be landing further out. Flying low I reckon we can get within three kilometres of the coast before the risk of one of their scouts spotting us —'

'We'll have to take that chance. Get us within two.'

'Sir, we'd risk losing the element of surprise. They're not going anywhere. They've lowered all their equipment down onto that beach and they're digging in,' Dias said slowly, his jaw tight, stopping just short of disrespect.

Vash could imagine how he felt, to have responsibility for some clueless civilian foisted on him, worse still, ostensibly the most powerful man on the planet — a man who'd never held a weapon in combat, never slept in a ditch, never watched a comrade bleed out in the dirt. To their credit, the Enforcers had taken his intrusion in their stride, but these men were used to operating with considerable autonomy.

'That's what I'm concerned about,' said Vash. 'Tell me, if you were going to make a last stand, would you choose an exposed beach hemmed in by cliffs on one side and the sea on the other?'

'They're not going to think like us, we're professionals. Way I see it these rebs stole a crawler, took a Dyn hostage then panicked when they realised they were in way over their heads. They've been running ever since,' Dias offered, his tone carrying the certainty of a man who'd built his career on understanding how desperate people behaved.

'Strange to run straight for the sea though, don't you think? What does that look like to you?' Vash asked, pointing to an object down near the shoreline, half obscured by tarpaulin. Dias shrugged.

'Some kind of vehicle?'

'It looks like a submarine to me. Someone's not intending to stay on that beach.' The Enforcer looked closer, squinting at the grainy image, then spoke into his radio.

'Rocha, take us down, close as you can.'

'Copy that,' came the pilot's response.

Moments later they angled away from the path cut by the crawler. The hills rose up around them as they shed altitude, the jungle canopy rushing past close enough to touch, close enough to smell — that heavy, organic reek of Dynic vegetation. The Enforcers began their final checks, securing helmets and body armour, unloading and reloading weapons with quick, practised motions. Vash massaged his temples and tried to focus.

They had departed the ruins of the linear arcology in the early morning, after only a few broken hours of sleep snatched on the cold floor of an abandoned control room. Whilst the Enforcers had quickly lapsed back into unconsciousness once they were underway, with an ease that spoke of years of experience, Vash had been unable to do so. Of course, it helped that they were ignorant of what was truly at stake — ignorance could be a remarkable sedative.

The world balanced on a knife-edge, the consequences of a single misstep more dire, more readily apparent than Vash could ever remember. The least risky course of action would be to follow Liar to Animals' diktat, to save her heir, pin the rebels on the beach and let the column of Enforcers still in pursuit mop them up before they could melt back into the jungle.

Vash had quickly learnt it was better not to take risks — a coward's rationalisation, as some would have it — but one didn't casually gamble with the lives of billions of people. Yet if he took the safest course of action, the obvious course of action, he might be denying the people of Earth their best hope of ending the Dynic occupation. That symbol, the jagged tooth, so familiar now that he could sketch it from memory, called to him across the centuries like a voice from a world he'd almost forgotten.

Roughly two hundred million people had died last year. It seemed an incongruous, almost trivial fact — a number too large to feel, too familiar to shock. Yet it hadn't always been so. Vash remembered a world without death, yet now it took everything they had to merely forestall that toll's inexorable rise. It was even worse in the years when the pandemics were bad, when the overcrowded conurbations became incubators for diseases that medicine could no longer keep pace with. There were only so many people you could support on a single planet at a given level of development, and Arco was beginning to run up against hard limits — the mathematics of population against resources drawing an ever-tightening noose.

If Vash again placated the Dyn, within less than a century he would condemn more people to short, miserable lives than he would save. Faced with the chance to end the occupation, he had for the first time in decades considered this world as an outsider and found it unacceptable. They were in the grip of a slow-burning disaster, a grinding Malthusian attrition of living standards; that it had continued for nearly two centuries didn't change anything. Preserving things the way they were was not a free action. Vash knew he was talking himself into the decision, marshalling arguments to justify a conclusion he'd already reached, yet that didn't matter. The time had come to take a risk.

Rocha brought them down in the centre of a deserted wastelander settlement, the forms of the surrounding buildings still discernible beneath the false life — angular shapes softened by layers of alien growth until they resembled ancient ruins claimed by a forest, which in a sense they were. The second helicopter landed further out, its rotors throwing up clouds of spores and pollen from the Dynic vegetation.

'We'll be in constant radio contact,' said Dias as he stepped out, boots crunching on the overgrown ground. 'Shouldn't be more than a couple of hours but Rocha here will keep you company. You can do… whatever it is that you do once we've got the area clear of hostiles.'

'I'll be coming with you,' said Vash firmly.

'With all due respect sir, that won't be necessary. We have our orders, we know what we're doing. We'll bring you in when we have the Dyn secured.'

'I don't doubt it. But that wasn't a request.' Vash perfunctorily selected a handgun from the rack and followed after Dias, checking the safety with a familiarity that surprised even himself — the muscle memory of a life lived centuries ago. 'I have my own orders to follow, after all.'

Dias forced a smile and protested no further, but Vash knew what he was thinking: you don't bring your commander-in-chief on a reconnaissance mission. The thought hung between them, unspoken, as they moved into the jungle.

The Enforcers fanned out, scanning the buildings surrounding them with instinctive, fluid movements — each soldier covering the other's blind spot, communicating through gesture and glance. There was no sign of any rebel scouts, at least none that would be revealed by such a cursory search. Vash followed a few meters behind, acutely aware of how loud his footsteps sounded compared to theirs.

He'd only seen the false jungle from the outside before, in satellite imagery or through the windows of a vehicle. It was different when you were immersed in it, swallowed by it. Fetid and heady scents hung in the humid air and plants like cancerous growths crowded him on every side, pressing close enough to brush his sleeves. Some pulsed softly with the beat of muscular nutrient pumps, like rudimentary hearts; a queasy blurring of boundaries between plant and animal. False life could not be so easily categorised. But what Vash noticed most of all was the sense of quiet watchfulness, of things moving just beneath the threshold of casual perception — a rustling that was always behind him, never quite visible when he turned. It would have been easy to convince himself that this was an alien world, if it wasn't for the details.

Scattered amongst the knotted tendrils and filaments of false life lay the debris of humanity, abandoned or forgotten the day the settlement had been cleared — relatively recently, by the looks of things. A broken pair of glasses, one lens cracked in a starburst pattern. A lighter, its chrome finish dulled by moisture. The rusted frame of a bike, half consumed by creeping tendrils. A stuffed toy, its synthetic fur matted and rotting, one button eye still staring up at the canopy. Vash could picture the people they had once belonged to, driven from their homes at gunpoint, herded like cattle. The collateral damage of another claim made by those above. Enforcers had come this way before, riot shields locked together, corralling terrified wastelanders ahead of them. Then on to buses; families arbitrarily divided between conurbations. Those that resisted would be sent to internment camps. Others simply ended up as corpses, and still lay there, mercifully obscured by the dense groundcover, slowly becoming part of the alien landscape that had claimed their homes. For better or worse, what happened today could end it all.

A body lay across the path, its outline softened by the encroaching plants, empty eye sockets staring blankly at the sky through a veil of creeping tendrils. One by one the Enforcers stepped over it without breaking stride — an obstacle, nothing more, a commonplace of their profession. Without conscious thought Vash found himself kneeling beside the body, his hand reaching out before his mind had formed the intention.

'Sir?' The Enforcers up ahead had realised he'd stopped, their formation losing its shape as they turned.

The corpse was small, although beyond that it had decomposed far enough that he could discern nothing else — age, sex, identity all stripped away by time and the relentless hunger of the false jungle. All he knew was that he should cover it. It seemed important, suddenly, though he couldn't have said why.

'Vash!'

Vash tore a curtain from one of the nearby doors, the fabric stiff and mildewed. A pair of wide, green eyes stared back at him from the darkness beyond the threshold. One of the rebels. He couldn't be more than sixteen — thin, hollow-cheeked, with the pinched look of someone who'd been underfed for most of his life. A gun shook in his white-knuckled grip, the barrel wavering between Vash and some point over his shoulder.

'Is everything okay, sir?' The Enforcers were turning back, their formation tightening.

Run, Vash pleaded silently. Just go. Get as far from here as you can.

'Everything's fine, I just wanted to cover the body,' he called, turning to the approaching Enforcers with what he hoped was a convincing expression of mild embarrassment.

Get as far away from here as you can.

'We need to keep moving. We can't hang back for you.'

Vash laid the sheet over the corpse, smoothing it down with hands that trembled slightly. The Enforcers took a few more steps then stopped as he stood and made to follow them. When he glanced back to the doorway, the boy had disappeared — swallowed by the shadows, by the false jungle, by whatever desperate chance at survival still remained to him.

Rejoining the other Enforcers, they continued to make their way slowly towards the coast, the foliage around them growing increasingly stunted and anemic as the salt air began to assert itself, the Dynic plants withering at the edges. After another fifteen minutes of cautious progress, Dias raised his fist, signalling for the others to stop.

'The forward squad has eyes on the crawler and they've confirmed the hostage is down on the beach,' said Dias from Vash's side, one hand pressed to his earpiece. 'We're gonna call in an airstrike on the vehicle before advancing further —'

'I want you to hold position here. Take no further action until I give the order.'

'Sir, the longer we wait, the more chance they have to discover us,' Dias replied, as though explaining to a child, the patience in his voice thin enough to see through.

'I understand, but I have orders from the Dyn to attempt negotiation first,' Vash lied, the falsehood coming more easily than he would have liked. 'If I don't report back in an hour, then execute whatever plans you've prepared. Hand me a radio.' Dias' expression hardened, something shuttering behind his eyes, and Vash realised immediately he'd overplayed his hand.

'I can't let you do that sir,' the Enforcer said coldly, all pretence of deference gone.

'Really? On whose orders?'

'The success of the mission takes priority,' Dias said, ignoring the question. It didn't matter — Vash already knew the answer. Corbin. Of course it was Corbin. Could he just keep walking towards the coast? Could he use his gun to alert the revolutionaries if the Enforcers stopped him?

Then, without warning, the Enforcer nearest to them collapsed, a dotbow bolt protruding from his neck like a dark thorn, and Vash's immediate concerns evaporated. Before he had even registered what was happening, another Enforcer went down and the others dived for cover, Dias pulling Vash down with him, one strong arm around his shoulders forcing him flat against the damp earth.

The Enforcers returned fire in the direction of their unseen assailants and within seconds the entire jungle erupted with the staccato cacophony of gunfire and barked orders. Stray shots and fragments of shattered trunks thwacked into the undergrowth around Vash, showering him with splinters and pulpy Dynic matter. At length he realised Dias was shouting something, barely discernible over the ringing in his ears, and fumbling a roll of bandages into his hands.

'Put pressure on the wound!' he yelled, pointing to a fallen Enforcer. 'Stay there! Keep down!'

Vash did as instructed, pressing the wadded bandages against the ragged wound, doing his best to staunch the bleeding. Even as he secured the bandages with shaking hands, he knew it was too late. The Enforcer gasped desperately at the air, choking, legs kicking, hands clutching at his own with a grip that was already weakening. Vash watched the life ebb from the man's face — the colour draining, the eyes glazing, the features slackening into an expression that was less than sleep and more than stillness — and after a long moment he fell still.

Vash fought down the rising nausea and scanned his surroundings. Dias had his back to him and was some way off, focussed on the assailants that had flanked them, shouting orders into his radio. This could be his only chance. Vash took a deep breath, tasting cordite and the iron tang of blood, then started running. Dias turned and shouted something — a command, a warning, a curse — but within moments the Enforcer was once again pinned down and forced to take cover. Vash heard Dias calling, but nobody emerged from the firefight to accost him.

A stray shot grazed Vash's shoulder, sending him sprawling into the undergrowth. He gasped at the pain — a line of white-hot fire across his deltoid — but it seemed somehow irrelevant, overwhelmed by the imperative to keep moving. He picked himself up and kept running, the treacherous ground-cover threatening to trip him again at every step, roots and tendrils clutching at his ankles. Up ahead, the hulking mass of the crawler was visible through the thinning jungle, its armoured bulk silhouetted against the grey sky. Then Vash was out in the open under the steel-grey sky, gulping at the clear, salty air. He reached inside his coat and drew the handgun, removing the magazine and discarding both with a careless throw — the gun tumbling end over end and disappearing into the scrub. The muffled gunfire was growing more sporadic, petering out behind him. If Vash was wrong, he might have just jeopardised the tenuous balance he'd spent the last century trying to maintain. If he was right, none of that would matter.

Sentries from the crawler were already waiting for him at the cliff edge, anxiously scanning the jungle at his back with weapons levelled. A couple came running towards him, guns drawn, their faces taut with alarm.

'Stay where you are! Hands on your head!' one shouted, in heavily accented Americano. He started as he drew close enough to recognise Vash's face — recognition, then confusion, then something approaching disbelief passing over his features in rapid succession.

He did as instructed, grimacing as he raised his left arm and felt the pain in his shoulder anew, now that the adrenaline rush had subsided, the wound throbbing in time with his pulse.

'I need to talk to your leader.'

If they heard they seemed to pay him no heed. The rebels searched him roughly then, silently, they walked him at gunpoint to the cliff edge where a makeshift crane awaited and lowered him down, the platform swaying in the wind, the beach rising up to meet him.

Not far from the foot of the cliff a crowd had gathered. They seemed focussed on a man with a shock of dark hair, a shotgun holstered on his left hip, but as Vash descended one by one their heads turned to him, drawn by the groan of the crane. When they saw who he was, one yelled in shock. The others simply gaped, their mouths open, weapons forgotten in slackened grips.

For all Vash knew they'd lynch him the moment he touched the ground — tear him apart on this grey beach and leave his body for the tide. Yet as the platform settled on the shingle a sense of unreal calm washed over him and the rushing in his ears subsided. There was none of the doubt or uncertainty he'd felt on the flight over — only a crystalline clarity, a sensation he hadn't experienced in so long he'd almost forgotten what it felt like. He stepped off the platform and walked slowly towards them, his boots crunching in the stones.

'I surrender,' he said.

He kept walking towards the one that had held the mob's focus until moments ago, assuming he must be the leader. The man, dark-skinned, dark-haired and garbed in a makeshift uniform and bandolier slung across one shoulder, had that look about him — that quality of someone around whom events arranged themselves, the centre of gravity in any room he entered. The rebels parted around Vash, letting him pass, too stunned to do otherwise.

'My my, Vash, the emperor of the world,' mused the revolutionary with a twinkle in his eye. He exchanged a brief, amused look with the woman stood behind him — a look that carried the weight of a shared secret. 'If I'd known we were going to have such esteemed company I might have smartened myself up a bit. But it's been a busy day. I'm sure you know how it is.' There wasn't even a hint of malice in his voice — only warmth, and a wry amusement that seemed genuine rather than performed.

'You can put your hands down now, friend. We can get that fixed up for you too,' the man continued, nodding at his shoulder. Vash looked down. Blood had seeped through the thin fabric of his overcoat, which now glistened darkly, the stain spreading wider than he'd realised.

'You're in charge here?' Vash asked.

'Where are my manners? We know your name, you should know ours. I'm Christo,' he smiled, then indicated the woman. 'This is Aurelie, my second in command, I guess, but I prefer to think of it as more of a collaborative effort.' Aurelie stepped forwards, her expression guarded but not hostile.

'What's he doing here?' The challenge came from a tall, wiry man near the front of the crowd. His hands were bound behind his back and two other revolutionaries flanked him, gripping his arms.

'Quiet,' snapped Aurelie, her voice cold enough to freeze the salt spray. 'Get him out of here.'

'We'll talk later Jan, okay?' said Christo, adopting a more conciliatory tone. 'The same goes for the rest of you.'

'But he's —' someone in the crowd started, pointing at Vash.

'I know who he is,' Christo laughed. 'Consider this; who could claim a greater reason to hate the Dyn than a man turned against his own species, forced to serve as a human face for our callous overlords? Vash is no collaborator. He's here because he shares in our dream.' He raised his shotgun theatrically to sporadic applause that was more relief than celebration. Christo's control over the crowd was admirable — effortless, instinctive, the kind of charisma that Vash could only aspire to, for all his centuries of political manoeuvring.

'Now come, we still have work to do. Back to your posts,' the rebel leader said, in a voice that didn't offer any alternative. Reluctantly, the revolutionaries dispersed. Some exchanged baffled glances, but none looked back.

'I was beginning to think you weren't coming,' Aurelie said, turning back to Vash. Her cold expression seemed to flicker, the mask slipping for just a moment, then she smiled warmly — a smile that transformed her face entirely. A wave of vindication surged through him, so powerful it made his eyes sting.

'You really did summon me here,' he said.

'Yes, we really did,' Christo said. He held out a hand and Vash found himself shaking it. The rebel leader had a vice-like grip that spoke of years of manual labour.

'You couldn't have picked a less ambiguous sign?' Vash actually found himself laughing — a strange, almost giddy sound in the salt air.

'You're not the easiest person to make covert contact with,' said Aurelie ruefully. 'It was as clear as we could risk. We need your help.'

'But why risk it at all; what do you need me for?'

'Tell me Vash, what does dragon's teeth mean to you?' asked Christo.

'Is this a test; I assumed you knew?'

'Tell us — for Christo's edification,' Aurelie said with a shrug. Christo regarded him with rapt focus, leaning in slightly, his dark eyes bright with an intensity that was almost feverish.

'Well there's the ancient myth. It's a metaphor, you see. A kind of hidden message that became a symbol for a dangerous technology. You don't have a word for it anymore, language has changed so much. In English, we called it a warseed.'

'Warseed? Aurelie's told me so little,' interrupted Christo, betraying a childlike fascination. Vash searched for the words that would explain, to a world that had long forgotten the context, concepts that had once been as commonplace as electricity.

'Before the Dynic invasion, machines were advanced enough to self-replicate, to create copies of themselves out of nothing but the materials that existed in the environment. Neummanetics, like the printers that Arco distributes, but fully general.' Christo signalled that he followed, though his eyes were wide.

'General replicators could create any machine, given the right materials and sufficient time. Pump enough energy into them, give them a way to dissipate the waste heat and they could replicate faster than you'd believe was possible. Imagine something that bears the same relation to our own cell's division as steel does to flesh and bone.'

Christo nodded, while Aurelie remained expressionless, her gaze fixed on some point beyond them both. Vash wondered how much of this she knew already — suspected she knew most of it, perhaps all of it, perhaps more.

'The world was peaceful, but simultaneously weapons were powerful beyond imagination. At the time, they thought the greater risk was to keep huge stockpiles of legacy weapons sitting around, so actual war machines were increasingly replaced by stockpiles of warseeds. The world retained the capacity to fight, but at one further remove from actual use. It brought lasting peace.'

'And yet still the Dyn invaded,' said Christo, a trace of bitterness creeping into his voice, his jaw clenching.

'All factions in-system followed the same logic, so it was assumed that any potential invasion would have to cross interstellar space, giving them years of warning. The possibility that an enemy fleet might just appear in Earth orbit, using means we don't understand even after the fact, seemed so absurdly remote that it didn't even merit consideration. The appearance of the Other Moon and the Dyn fleet was a black swan; an unknown unknown. Conventional forces were quickly overwhelmed by brute force and within minutes Earth was held hostage.'

'You say they,' Christo interjected, taking a step back from Vash, his expression shifting. 'But you're speaking from memory, aren't you?'

Vash nodded sadly; there was no point withholding that fact. The weight of those memories — of a world lost, of friends lost, of everything lost — settled on him like a physical burden.

'Things unravelled so fast that they… that we didn't have a chance to respond in real time. But what I don't understand,' said Vash turning back to Aurelie, 'is exactly how this is relevant. Do you have a warseed in that sub?'

'Better than that,' Christo grinned, the bitterness vanishing as quickly as it had appeared, replaced by something almost boyish in its enthusiasm.

'The Utilitaria had a contingency plan,' said Aurelie. 'And now that you're here, the final piece is in place.'

Dias found the kid not far from where the other rebels had abandoned him, pulling himself forward through the dirt with both hands, leaving a dark smear behind him. His legs trailed uselessly behind, shattered from where a frag had caught him, the bones splintered beneath the torn fabric of his trousers.

'Is Vash working with you?' Dias asked.

The kid ignored him and just kept putting one hand in front of the other with the single-minded determination of someone who recognised they were already doomed but refused to concede the point.

'Hey kid, I asked you a question,' said Dias. He rolled the kid over and pinned him down with one boot resting gently on his chest. The rebel looked back up at him, holding his gaze with bright, green eyes — the same green eyes that Vash had seen in the doorway, though Dias would never know that.

'Finish it or lemme be on my way,' the rebel managed through gritted teeth, his voice thin and reedy, the words costing him visible effort. 'Either way I ain't tellin' you shit.' He tried to spit, but the bloody spittle made it no further than his chin, where it hung in a trembling strand.

'I'm not asking twice,' Dias cautioned. Weak hands pushed ineffectually at his foot, the fingers slipping on the leather. After a while the rebel gave in, exhausted, his body going limp, his chest rising and falling in shallow, ragged breaths.

'We're gonna free you,' the kid said, his voice barely more than a strained whisper now, the words coming from somewhere deeper than his ruined body. 'We're gonna free you all.'

'Maybe you will,' Dias sighed, something passing across his weathered face that might have been regret. He drew his sidearm and leveled it at the point between the boy's closed eyes. No sense in wasting good morphine; he only had minutes to live. Dias looked away and pulled the trigger.

Afterwards he called Corbin.

'Vash has gone. He demanded to accompany us, then went off-mission. They knew we were here — a recon team ambushed us.' There was no immediate reply, only the faint hiss of static. 'Your orders?' Dias asked.

'Where is Vash now?' came the response, clipped and precise.

'We're not sure, we assume he's with them. He left in the midst of the fighting.'

There was another extended pause; Dias thought he heard an exasperated sigh, or perhaps just the wind against the microphone.

'Wait for the reinforcements. Get Vash and the hostage back unharmed. Otherwise, proceed as planned.'

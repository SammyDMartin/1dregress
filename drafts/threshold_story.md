# Threshold

**2909 CE**

---

*In toil, absolution. In strife, salvation. In death, release.*
*-- Human Purity Front Creed*

---

## I

The USC Isidore was already on an intercept course with the unresponsive Kosmohansa vessel when the freighter’s drive plume reignited, burning hard for the interstice, its rate of acceleration gradually climbing.

From his vantage point within the darkened CIC of the Isidore, bound by a G-rig within a gimballed acceleration couch, Ensign Hansun watched as the displays arrayed before him updated, the orange icon indicating the rogue freighter switching to an angry red. It was visible in the camera feeds even without the benefit of the tactical overlay - a tiny white pinprick, still distant but already brighter than any star.

The revised trajectory estimates indicated that they could likely still intercept the craft before it reached the interstice, but they’d be uncomfortably close. Deceleration would be hard.

‘Match thrust, fire a warning shot,’ came the voice of Captain Ngoni. The ship bucked as Hansun complied, launching a single round towards the distant freighter - a gesture, nothing more.

He felt their own thrust increase in step, hearing the chatter from Interstice Traffic Control take on a new urgency.

‘Kosmohansa vessel Atbarah cut thrust immediately and await intercept, you are within a no-burn volume. Atbarah do you copy? Kosmohansa vessel Atbarah I repeat, cut thrust immediately, or we will be forced to fire -’

Hansun glanced at the Atbarah’s drive specs. Over the timescales they were looking at it didn’t matter how long the ship could sustain a high-G burn, or what its maximum thrust capacity was. If it accelerated at more than a couple of Gs they would no longer be able to safely intercept. Still, the weapons platforms guarding the wormhole mouth could surely deal with a lone freighter. Interstice security was no joke. Unless the crew had a death wish, they’d turn and burn any minute now.

The Atbarah continued to accelerate, in defiance of ITC’s instructions. Two point four G, then two point five.

What are they playing at?

It was then that the Isidore received a broadcast from the Atbarah. A virtual screen opened in Hansun’s peripheral vision, pre-recorded footage of a speech, addressed to anyone in range. A manifesto. A statement of intent.

The speaker was a fanatic. Hansun recognised the type - the evidence was there for all to see. It was there in the cold certainty of his voice and the rigid way he held his spindly spaceborn frame. It was there in the way his unblinking eyes bored into the camera and the barely constrained rage that burned within. It was there in the pockmarks and bloodstains on the bulkhead behind him.

‘...never wanted to begin with an expression of regret for the innocent human lives that we, necessarily, had to end,’ he said, his speech thickly accented but fluent. ‘But we may take consolation in the fact that all innocents, all that die with purity of spirit, will be remembered as martyrs no less revered than my comrades and I.’

He passed his hands over one another, almost unconsciously, as though washing them. In the background, down the axial conduit, Hansun saw movement. It was out of focus, but it looked like someone dragging something human shaped.

Sensing his new focus, Hansun’s AR overlay shifted the incoming transmission into his central visual field. The CIC of the Isidore fell momentarily silent as the others did likewise. The intensity of the fanatic’s unwavering gaze demanded engagement, as though he had locked eyes with each of them personally, the vast distances that separated them melting away.

‘They died for what they thought was right, protecting our Union from those they were told were extremists. Terrorists. Traitors. But I do not name them traitor in turn. They were lied to, fed the inverted morality of those that claim to protect us. Truly, the former crew of the Atbarah were righteous. The traitors,’ his gaze hardened. ‘Are out there. Beyond the interstice. Those that betray our common humanity, that pervert it in pursuit of material power. The abhumans and machine slaves, who only believe themselves free because the oppression they live under has become the very air that they breathe. The blood that flows in their veins. The dreams they hold in their hearts. They are blind to it, so omnipresent, so all-consuming has it become. Maybe, ultimately, they too cannot be blamed. Maybe we could leave them to their ways, if they were content to leave us to ours.’

The fanatic shook his head as he spoke, in an insincere display of regret.

Intelligence were already scrutinizing every detail of the transmission, trying to identify those within it and discern their motives, their aims, adding their commentary to a growing cloud of tags. A crudely stencilled insignia, sprayed onto the bloodied bulkhead behind the speaker, was flagged up. A stark white disk composed of tenets written in a stylised script Hansun didn’t recognise against a black background.

Human Purity Front, read the tag. Emphasis on an accelerationist interpretation of Anthropist doctrine, seeking to provoke a final conflict between humanity and ‘the Machine’ through acts of political violence in order to bring about societal collapse and the establishment of ‘the Moral Republic’.

‘Strivers[d],’ someone cursed.

Hansun felt a pit open up in his stomach. His first combat experience had been on a patrol mission ambushed by one of their raiders. They’d fought like rabid dogs.

On some level he had already known - the rhetoric was as familiar as it was poisonous. Hansun had heard variants on the theme more times than he could count. [e][f]

The Strivers’ ideological ancestry predated Union’s founding and their exodus from Sol. They went by many names, but they’d always been there, ever since humanity began to master the workings of its own mind. Strivers placed raw, unaltered and above all mortal human identity above any other allegiance, a twisted mirror of the ideals upon which Union itself was built. They were not simply political, nor purely religious, but instead some shifting chimera of the two, adopting one mask or another at the demands of convenience.

‘But they will never be content. They tore out their souls. Traded them in for worthless trinkets. And in their place there is only a hole, a void where their humanity used to be. That corruption, it spreads like a disease. Whether it takes a decade or a century, despite the futile efforts of the Threshold Authority, we will one day become as empty as them if we do not act. The Grey Man walks among them once again, the implacable evil of ages past, bending the Machine to its will. The signs are plain for any who are not wilfully blind -’

‘We’re out of intercept range,’ an officer noted. Hansun had had the same realisation.

The Atbarah continued to accelerate towards the interstice. What were they hoping to do? Ram it? Sever Union’s final connection with their estranged kin? Nobody had ever been insane enough to try to destroy the ancient relic, and it would not be easy for the Atbarah. Their velocity was high, but nowhere near high enough to damage the artefact’s support structure, the vast, dark rings of exotic materials that encircled the sphere of warped spacetime. And that was with the generous assumption they’d even get within ten thousand klicks of it.

‘Notify Interstice Command, they may neutralise the Atbarah when ready,’ instructed Captain Ngoni.

Maybe that was the whole point. To go out in a blaze of glory, draw attention to their message, and get others to join the cause. There were enough sympathisers out there. Within hours that recording would be proliferating across feeds all over the system.

On the screen the fanatic continued his call to arms.

‘- this violence against us cannot go unanswered. If we do not act soon, we will be engulfed. Assimilated and corrupted. And once we go astray, once the last light of humanity flickers and dies, there will be no going back. The possibility of human extinction beckons within our children’s lifetimes. So I come to you now, with a proposition. It is desperate, I grant you, but -’

‘Why have they not fired yet?’ somebody asked. Hansun looked down at his own display - the defence platforms had a clear firing solution, but nothing was happening.

Crucial seconds trickled by. The swarm of green icons of the interstice defence grid remained stubbornly passive.

‘Something’s not right here. Hard burn for the interstice,’ came Ngoni’s orders. ‘I want a lock on the Atbarah.’

Hansun was already on it, feeling the Isidore’s drive kick into higher gear, but it was too late. The Atbarah was beyond effective range, almost between them and the interstice. With the ever-so-slight randomness they were putting into the vessel’s thrust vector, any attempt to saturate the volume ahead of the freighter risked collateral damage.

‘- they can be stopped. We only need the will to fight,’ he smacked his fist into his hand for emphasis. ‘You will no doubt have noticed the Atbarah is already on an irreversible course for the interstice. There is no denying our martyrdom now.’

As if on cue the icons of the defence grid switched to grey.

‘What’s happening?’ demanded the Captain.

‘Interstice Command reports the defence grid is not responding, attempts to override have thus far been unsuccessful. They suspect some kind of cyberattack -’

Hansun watched layers of defences evaporate. How the hell could the terrorists penetrate security so easily? It shouldn’t have been possible to hack the defences from a distance - Interstice Command operated in accordance with strict Siren Protocols, the necessary product of dealing with the advanced and unpredictable agencies on the other side.

‘Have they forewarned their counterparts?’

‘No sir,’ replied the comms officer. ‘Network went down too fast for any failsafes to kick in.’

Sometimes, Hansun reflected, you just know you’ve been thrown into the middle of a massive fuck up.

Without warning, a cascade of detonations tore through the stations surrounding the interstice. Hansun watched the feed helplessly. There were hundreds, maybe thousands of people on those stations.

Shocked silence descended over the crew. The Captain inhaled sharply. Hansun clenched his fists, fingernails digging into his skin. Anger, he had long since determined, was preferable to fear or despair.

‘- necessary to force your hand to do what is right… you will come to see in time -’

The recording had only been completed minutes ago, after the Atbarah began its acceleration. Hansun could hear the strain of it in the speaker’s voice.

‘- in mere seconds, the Atbarah will be through. In minutes more, it will have met a fiery end against the shell of one of their stations, expending human blood… the first of many sacrifices in the war to come… but they will expire in their thousands losing whatever crude mockery of life they had -’

The Atbarah’s icon closed in on the interstice and its drive flared brighter than before. That acceleration was a death sentence for anybody still alive onboard, though Hansun suspected the terrorists were already long since dead, crushed in their seats.

‘- their mask will slip… revealing the merciless nature hidden beneath their facade of civility… they will come pouring through, to annihilate us… but understand the truth that to live in the face of inevitable death is when the flame of humanity burns brightest… In toil, absolution. In strife, salvation. In death, release.’

The fanatic slumped back in his seat, unconscious, a look of beatific peace settling briefly over his features, before they were contorted by the rising G load. The message continued silently for another couple of seconds. It ended abruptly as the Atbarah hurtled into the interstice, still accelerating at that crushing rate.

‘Decelerate and disengage,’ came Ngoni’s command, as she swiftly regained her composure. ‘This is a rescue mission now. And get me a line with Admiral Aumonier.’

‘Yessir.’

---

## II

Admiral Aumonier’s orders were brief and matter-of-fact. They were to establish a blockade around the interstice with all possible speed. A drone had been deployed, sprinting for the connection to attempt to make contact, but everyone knew that it was far too late.

‘As a result of the recent attack we find ourselves in a tenuous position, to put it mildly. We are yet to determine just how they were able to disable our defences, but rest assured an investigation will be launched with all due haste. In the meantime,’ he had explained, calling up a plot of local space, ‘we will assume a defensive position surrounding the interstice. I’ve authorised the diversion of sixty percent of all assets in-system to this task. However, we cannot assume that everything will be in place in time. Depending on the distribution of their forces, we may have only a few hours to respond.’

Hansun studied the plans. The tactical map displayed the immediate environment surrounding their side of the interstice, centered on the trailing Lagrange point of the gas giant Deliverance, the outermost major planetary body in the system. It took in the wreckage of the defensive platforms and stations nearest the interstice, now evacuated, and beyond that the initial ragged cordon established by the USV Isidore, along with her escorts and the nine other battle groups that composed the First Fleet, the first to form up. There were ninety in total, spread over a hundred thousand kilometres of space - ten destroyers like the Isidore, boxy, almost trilaterally symmetric towers, bristling with torpedo tubes and gun turrets, each supported by nine unmanned corvettes.

Beyond them were civilian vessels heading in the opposite direction, fleeing the volume in the direction of Deliverance. Further out still were the other fleets, green trails converging on the interstice, the closest of which was the Second Fleet, based in the orbit of that great gas giant. Most of the force was several days away, if not weeks. They were exposed.

‘Assuming the suicide attack succeeded, our counterparts on the other side may interpret it as a first strike. Our failure to stop the Atbarah will likely be treated as suspicious, if not intentional. For all they know, there could be another attack impending, or worse. With a threat like that, there is a non-negligible possibility that they shoot first, so they don’t have to ask questions later. We must hold fire unless fired upon. Securing the interstice is the only objective more important than deescalation. We may not be able to re-establish communications with them until they emerge. We’ll keep trying to get through, and with any luck, this won’t become a shooting war.’

It was rumoured there had been arguments among the general staff. Some, to Hansun’s astonishment, had actually wanted to follow through, sweeping through the interstice to strike ‘the enemy’ preemptively - ‘seizing this unique opportunity, albeit under suboptimal circumstances’. But to Hansun’s great relief Aumonier had won out.

He glanced around, trying to read the others’ faces, but they were inscrutable in the soft red light of the CIC. They had the blank, middle-distance gazes of people focussed on their AR overlays. He wondered if any of them had secretly thrilled at the fanatic’s words. But he swiftly pushed such thoughts aside. Now was not the time.

‘Something’s coming through,’ the First Lieutenant announced.

So it was only hours after all.

Hansun watched as the scopes magnified the shimmering cage of the interstice and the warped sphere of space contained within, partially occluded by the spreading cloud of wreckage from the crippled defence grid.

Something emerged, there in one frame, gone the next, yet still barely moving by combat standards - a scant few kilometres per second. A second blur shot out, coasting with drive stilled. A third, then a fourth, larger and slower than the other three.

‘Mean looking bastards,’ the sensor officer commented, throwing the magnified profile onto the main display. The spacecraft had a look of deadly elegance to them, elongated pyramids with sweeping lines like darts, ridged with projectors and weapons hardpoints. Each had a gaping central tube running along its main axis - a spinally mounted weapon of some kind.

The initial three were somewhat smaller than the boxy forms of the Union craft, but Hansun knew that they were no less deadly - and so few. Either they weren’t anticipating a fight, or they were supremely confident.

‘Hold position with the interstice. PDCs to track-while-scan, charge eCell banks, blow the torpedo tubes but hold target lock. Full passive sweep,’ came the order, Captain Ngoni’s voice crisp. ‘Prep for high-G maneuvers.’

Identical orders rippled out through the other ships.

Hansun braced himself as the CIC began to rapidly flood with oxygen-rich breathable liquid, pouring from grates in the deck.

No matter how many high-G drills they did, he could never imagine becoming entirely comfortable with this transition. As the liquid submerged him, Hansun fought every instinct he had to hold his breath and inhaled as deeply as he could, inviting it in. He struggled momentarily, choking on the more viscous medium until ventilators kicked in to assist. Then, as his breathing settled, he reached for the IV line that extended from the acceleration couch and inserted it into the tiny, silver port on his forearm, feeling a brief, nauseating rush as the cocktail of drugs intended to aid circulation and ward off G-LOC spread through his body.

‘Everyone comfy?’ came the medical officer’s voice over the comms, synthesised from the subvocal articulations picked up by her mic.

A chorus of assent.

‘Good. Have we made contact?’ inquired Ngoni.

‘We’re negotiating comm protocols,’ said the first officer.

Hansun studied the data on the intruder’s spacecraft more closely. Their magnetic field dynamic was… odd, fields extending hundreds of meters to the aft of each ship, with traces of pion decay and high-energy gamma radiation that indicated a very clean antimatter annihilation. Strakes glowed luminous white along each ship's leading edges - radiators made of some unknown, extraordinarily heat-tolerant substance.

A few tense seconds stretched as the alien spacecraft diverged in space, drive plumes far outshining any in the Union fleet, despite a moderate acceleration. On the overlay four red vectors splayed apart, curving lines intersecting with a hemispherical haze of green icons.

Hansun realised he’d been holding his breath, and sucked in liquid air, trying to calm himself.

‘Is anyone getting through to them?’ asked the Captain.

‘No sir, still handshaking. Seems to be some software trouble on our end.’

The display flashed amber. Bright icons appeared, first one, then four, then twelve. For a moment Hansun didn’t understand what he was seeing.

‘We’ve just opened fire,’ announced the first officer dully.

‘What?’

‘Fucking Strivers,’ someone said. ‘Fucking Strivers must have worms in our network too. They couldn’t leave their precious war to fate.’

Orders flew back and forth. Hansun pulled up emergency guidance control, but his destruct commands didn’t reach the torpedoes. The intruding ships diverged further, still not reacting, still not communicating.

‘Hansun, shoot them down, unsafe the PDCs,’ Ngoni ordered.

A number of the other ships were already attempting to do likewise, with mixed results as the torpedoes rapidly slipped beyond effective range, space filling with streams of kinetic rounds.

New orders from the Admiral arrived. They were to hold fire - projectile shots might be interpreted as further hostile action.

Everyone could see which way this was going.

‘Battle stations,’ the Captain ordered, the CIC lighting momentarily dipping as power was diverted to critical systems.

They could still avoid this, Hansun thought. If they could just get through to the other side. If they could make themselves understood, explain, make the Arco ships see that Union was not responsible, that they had been attacked from within -

The first impact cut that thought short.

It wasn't a direct hit - one of the Arco vessels had fired a burst of kinetic rounds across the bow of the nearest destroyer, the USV Acheron, close enough that the impacts registered on every ship's proximity sensors. A warning shot. The same gesture the Isidore had made to the Atbarah, what felt like a lifetime ago.

'Return fire!' came a voice over the fleet channel - one of the other captains, Hansun didn't catch who.

'Negative,' Ngoni countermanded immediately. 'All ships, weapons hold. Nobody fires without my direct authorisation.'

But Hansun could see it happening in real time on his display. Weapons systems across the fleet were cycling independently, targeting solutions appearing unbidden, torpedo tubes pressurising. The Striver malware was threading through the network like a living thing, probing for gaps in the firewalls, finding them, exploiting them. Two more destroyers reported uncommanded weapons discharges - bursts of PDC fire, brief and wild, but unmistakably hostile from the outside.

'I'm losing weapon authority,' Hansun reported, watching with sick disbelief as his console flickered, control inputs rejected, overridden by something nested deep in the fire control architecture. 'Sir, the whole weapons net is compromised. Whatever they planted, it's propagating.'

The Striver code was elegant in its viciousness. It didn't try to aim the weapons - that would have required understanding Union targeting systems in detail. Instead it simply activated everything, sending torpedoes and kinetic rounds out on random vectors, creating the appearance of a fleet opening up with everything it had. From the Arco ships' perspective, the distinction between a coordinated attack and a malware-triggered barrage would be invisible. Intent didn't matter when the projectiles were real.

'Go manual. Pull the data lines if you have to,' Ngoni ordered.

Hansun was already disconnecting. He pulled the primary data cable from the fire control panel, feeling the satisfying physical click as the corrupted network was severed. It was crude - it meant losing half his sensor feeds, targeting solutions, all the integrated battle management data that made the difference between a warship and a floating gun - but at least the guns were his again.

The Arco ships responded to the scattered fire with terrifying precision. The lead vessel - the largest of the four - rotated along its long axis with an almost organic fluidity, its spinal weapon glowing with a pale, actinic light that Hansun's instruments couldn't properly categorise. Energy readings spiked in bands he didn't recognise.

'Incoming!' the sensor officer shouted.

A beam of coherent energy - not a laser, something stranger, something that seemed to bend spacetime around itself - lanced across the formation, striking the USV Kepler amidships. The destroyer's kinetic barriers flared and died instantly, overwhelmed, and a section of hull plating sublimated in a flash of superheated metal. The Kepler tumbled, drive cutting out, atmosphere venting from a wound that glowed white-hot along its entire starboard flank.

'Kepler is hit, Kepler is hit,' the comms channel erupted.

'Casualties?'

'CIC intact but they've lost main power. Corvettes are moving to screen.'

The three smaller Arco vessels had fanned out, each one tracking a different sector of the Union formation. Their drives burned with that impossible brightness, acceleration rates that should have killed any crew aboard, and yet the ships moved with fluid, purposeful grace. Hansun wondered if there even were crews aboard - or whether these were autonomous systems, directed by intelligences that experienced combat at speeds no organic mind could match. Either possibility was unsettling.

They were herding the fleet, Hansun realised - pushing them back from the interstice, establishing a perimeter. Not trying to destroy them. Not yet. The precision of those shots, the calculated near-misses - these were not the actions of a force that wanted annihilation. They were establishing dominance, making a point. Demonstrating, with the cold clarity of superior firepower, that they could end this engagement at any moment they chose.

A second beam lashed out, narrowly missing the Isidore's escort screen. The shockwave - or whatever equivalent existed for a weapon that operated on principles Hansun's training hadn't covered - rattled the hull, sending tremors through the liquid medium that filled the CIC.

'They're going to pick us apart,' muttered the first officer.

'Can anyone make contact?' Ngoni demanded. 'Anyone at all? Go through every protocol we have. Morse, radio, optical, throw a message in a bottle if you have to.'

'Still handshaking, sir. The software layer is-'

'Bypass the software. Use the raw emitters. Just pulse something at them. Anything.'

Hansun stared at his display. The Arco ships' communications would be built on entirely different architectures - different encoding schemes, different modulation, decades of divergent development since Union and Arco had last cooperated on anything. The automated protocols existed precisely because the gap was too wide for manual bridging.

But the gap wasn't just technical, was it? The protocols were handshaking because the handshaking software was corrupted. The underlying hardware - the electromagnetic spectrum - was universal. Light was light. Radio was radio. The Arco ships had to be listening on some frequencies, even if they weren't responding to the automated overtures.

'Captain,' Hansun said, a thought crystallising with sudden clarity. 'The Arco ships are scanning us. I can see their sensor sweeps on passive. If they're scanning, they're receiving. We don't need to handshake - we just need to broadcast on a frequency they're already monitoring.'

'And say what?'

'The truth. The whole manifesto. The Atbarah's trajectory data. Our weapons logs showing uncommanded discharges. Everything.'

'They won't understand our encoding-'

'Raw data, sir. Plain electromagnetic. No compression, no encryption. Pair it with primer sequences - mathematical constants, hydrogen emission lines, the kind of universal referents that any technological civilisation will recognise. Make it obvious that we're trying to communicate, not attack. If nothing else, the act of broadcasting in the clear, weapons powered down, might give them pause.'

Ngoni was silent for a beat. Another Arco beam scored the space between two destroyers, close enough that both ships' hulls rang with secondary effects. Time was not on their side.

'Do it,' Ngoni ordered. 'And kill our active sensors. Retract all weapon systems to stowed positions. All ships, all ships, this is the Isidore. Power down weapons and active sensors immediately. Comply now.'

'Sir, if they fire on us with weapons stowed-'

'Then we die looking like the victims rather than the aggressors, and maybe that saves the next fleet from fighting a war we didn't start. Do it.'

The orders propagated through the formation. Not every ship complied immediately - Hansun watched the fleet status board, saw the hesitations, the seconds ticking by before some captains acknowledged. He didn't blame them. Every instinct, every hour of training, screamed against going weapons-cold in the face of an active threat. Three ships - the Acheron, the Novgorod, and the Tenacity - took nearly a full minute to comply. The Tenacity's captain transmitted a formal objection for the record before powering down.

Hansun watched, dry-mouthed, as icons across the fleet shifted from amber to green - weapons offline, sensors passive. They were naked now, blind and toothless against ships that outclassed them in every measurable way. He felt the collective held breath of the crew, suspended in their liquid medium, hearts hammering against the chemical calm of the anti-G cocktail.

He worked fast, stripping the communication system back to its most basic components. No protocols, no handshaking, no software layers that the Striver worms might have compromised. He modulated the Isidore's main comm array directly, pulsing at the frequencies he could see the Arco ships scanning on - high microwave, infrared, a tight UV band that he wouldn't have thought to try if he hadn't noticed one of the smaller ships sweeping it repeatedly.

First, the primer: the first hundred prime numbers, pulsed in binary. Then pi, carried to a thousand digits. Then hydrogen's emission frequency, rendered as a simple tone. Universal, unmistakable, the mathematical equivalent of raised hands and a white flag.

Then the payload. The Atbarah's transponder data. The Striver manifesto, transmitted raw. The Isidore's own weapons log, timestamped to the millisecond, showing the gap between uncommanded firing and the crew's attempts to override. Everything Hansun could pull together that told the story: we didn't do this. We were used.

'Transmitting,' he reported.

For a long, hideous moment, nothing changed. The Arco ships continued their advance, the largest one rotating that terrible spinal weapon to bear on the Isidore's position. Hansun stared at it in the magnified feed - the bore of the weapon was wide enough to fit a shuttle, and it glowed with the sickly luminescence of energies his instruments couldn't name. At this range, it would core them like an apple.

'Anything?' Ngoni asked.

'No response.'

'Keep transmitting.'

Another beam fired - but high this time, passing over the fleet, splashing harmlessly against the debris field of the destroyed stations. A shot across the bow of the entire formation. Hansun flinched despite himself, the liquid medium transmitting the vibration through his body with nauseating intimacy.

Then his board chirped.

'Contact!' he shouted. 'I'm getting something back. Same frequencies, same primer structure. They received it - they're parsing our data.'

The CIC erupted in controlled chaos. A flood of data came back from the largest Arco ship - not words, not language, but a torrent of structured information that Hansun's stripped-down systems struggled to render. Mathematical relationships, coordinate systems, what might have been timestamps. The Arco ship was building a shared reference frame, bootstrapping a crude but functional pidgin from first principles.

It took four minutes and thirty-seven seconds. In that time, two more Arco beams fired - both high, both deliberate misses, but each one close enough to make the hull groan. The fleet drifted in formation, engines at station-keeping thrust, weapons dark, every officer aboard every ship breathing liquid and wondering if the next shot would be the one that didn't miss.

When the first intelligible message resolved on Hansun's display, it was brutally simple. Four concepts, rendered in the jerry-rigged pidgin, repeated three times for clarity:

CEASE. WITHDRAW. EXPLAIN. PENDING.

'They want us to pull back from the interstice,' Hansun interpreted. 'Maintain position at distance while they evaluate.'

'How far?'

'They're indicating a radius. Roughly ten thousand kilometres from the interstice mouth.'

Ngoni didn't hesitate. 'All ships, all ships. Retrograde burn, establish new position at ten thousand klicks from the interstice. Maintain weapons-cold posture. Acknowledge.'

The fleet withdrew. It felt like surrender - backing away under the guns of ships that had just fired on them, yielding the interstice to a foreign power within Union's own space. Hansun could hear the anger in the terse acknowledgements from the other ships. Nobody liked running. But they were alive, and the shooting had stopped, and for now that had to be enough.

## III

The standoff lasted nineteen hours.

Nineteen hours of drifting in formation at the prescribed distance, weapons stowed, sensors passive, breathing liquid and waiting. Hansun's body ached in the way that prolonged high-G immersion always produced - a deep, cellular fatigue that no drug cocktail could fully mask. His fingers were pruned and pale, his eyes stung, and the IV port in his forearm had started to itch where the cocktail met his bloodstream.

The Arco ships held position around the interstice, their drives occasionally flaring in brief correction burns that outshone everything in the Union fleet combined. More data trickled back and forth through the crude channel Hansun had established - he and his counterpart on the Arco flagship building their shared vocabulary word by painful word, concept by concept. He learned to express negation, affirmation, temporal markers. He learned the Arco glyph for what he eventually understood as 'contamination' - the word they used for the Striver malware, once they understood what it was.

Three hours in, the Second Fleet arrived - another ninety ships, fanning out behind the First Fleet's position, a show of strength that Hansun prayed wouldn't be misinterpreted. He transmitted frantically, contextualising the new arrivals as defensive, not offensive. The Arco ships made no aggressive moves, but their weapons remained charged, and the spinal bore on the flagship continued to glow with that sickly light.

Seven hours in, an Arco drone emerged from the interstice - smaller than the warships, bristling with sensor equipment. It made a high-speed pass through the debris field of the destroyed stations, scanning the wreckage with instruments that lit up Hansun's passive sensors like a bonfire. He studied the data it was collecting - or rather, studied the traces of its collection, the reflections and emissions that leaked from its sensor sweeps. It was thorough. It scanned every piece of wreckage, every hull fragment, every frozen body tumbling through the expanding cloud. Cataloguing the dead, Hansun thought. Documenting the crime scene. Then it returned through the interstice without acknowledging the Union fleet's existence.

Ten hours in, fatigue began to manifest in ways the drugs couldn't mask. Hansun's thoughts grew sluggish, his reactions slower. The liquid medium, which had felt merely uncomfortable at first, now pressed against his skin like a living weight. His lungs laboured with every breath, the ventilators working harder as the viscous fluid grew warm and stale. Around him, the CIC had taken on the oppressive atmosphere of a submarine running on emergency power - dim, close, the air thick with the hum of life support systems operating at the ragged edge of their design parameters.

Twelve hours in, Admiral Aumonier's flagship arrived with the Third Fleet. The Admiral took over negotiations personally, his staff working through the communication channel Hansun had bootstrapped, refining it, expanding it, turning a desperate improvisation into something approaching a diplomatic instrument. Hansun remained at his station, maintaining the link, monitoring the signal integrity, too tired and too vital to be relieved.

Fifteen hours in, the Arco flagship transmitted a single long message. Aumonier's intelligence staff took an hour to fully parse it. The gist was plain enough: the Atbarah had struck an orbital habitat on the other side of the interstice, detonating its drive core on impact. Eleven thousand dead. Arco considered this an act of war by Union. Union's protestations of innocence were noted but not accepted. The data regarding the Striver cyberattack was under review. Pending the outcome of that review, Arco would maintain a military presence at the interstice. Any Union vessel approaching within the prescribed radius would be destroyed without warning.

Sixteen hours in, Captain Ngoni finally ordered the CIC drained. The liquid receded with agonising slowness, leaving Hansun gasping in the sudden, overwhelming lightness of breathing air. He coughed for two solid minutes, expelling fluid from his lungs, his whole body shaking with the exertion.

'Well done, Ensign,' Ngoni said quietly, once the coughing subsided. It was the first personal remark she'd made in the entire engagement.

Eighteen hours in, two of the smaller Arco vessels withdrew through the interstice. Hansun watched them go, those elegant, deadly shapes sliding back through the warped sphere of spacetime with the same fluid grace they had shown on arrival. The third followed twenty minutes later. Only the flagship remained, its spinal weapon still charged, its sensor sweeps still painting the Union fleet with invisible light.

Nineteen hours in, the flagship began to move. Slowly, deliberately, it rotated until its weapon bore was no longer pointed at the fleet but at the interstice itself - a gesture that Hansun could only interpret as a final demonstration of who controlled what. Then it accelerated, drives flaring in that impossible brilliance, its pyramidal hull catching the distant light of Deliverance's sun. It paused at the threshold - Hansun would swear it paused, though he knew that was likely anthropomorphisation - and then it was gone, swallowed by the warped sphere of spacetime, leaving nothing but the debris field and the battered Union fleet hanging in the dark.

Nobody cheered.

---

## IV

The aftermath unfolded with the grim inevitability of bureaucratic process. Investigations were launched. Reports were filed. The Striver malware was extracted from the fleet's networks node by node, a painstaking digital surgery that took weeks and revealed the terrifying sophistication of the attack - the worms had been dormant for months, seeded during a routine software update that had been compromised at the source. The defence grid, the communications protocols, the weapons systems - all had been infiltrated long before the Atbarah ever fired its engines.

The Kepler was towed back to dock with forty-three dead and over a hundred wounded, its starboard flank a melted ruin that shipyard engineers studied with grim fascination. Whatever weapon had struck it operated on principles Union science couldn't replicate, and probably couldn't defend against. That knowledge settled over the fleet like a cold fog. They had been outmatched, and everyone knew it.

Six people were arrested in connection with the attack. Twelve more were placed under investigation. The intelligence services cast their nets wider and pulled in sympathisers, fellow travellers, and the merely unlucky in equal measure. Hansun gave his testimony three times - once to the ship's recorder, once to fleet intelligence, and once to a civilian oversight board that seemed more interested in assigning blame than understanding what had happened.

The recording of the fanatic's manifesto proliferated exactly as Hansun had predicted. Within days it was everywhere - on feeds, in private channels, whispered about in mess halls and debated on talk shows. The Strivers had their martyr. They had their catalyst. Recruitment surged.

On the other side of the interstice, Arco's response was characteristically opaque. The intelligence reports Hansun was cleared to see painted a picture of hardening attitudes, military buildups, diplomatic channels going cold one by one. The data Union had provided regarding the Striver attack was acknowledged but not publicly accepted. To do so would have meant admitting that the threat came from within Union - from humans, unaugmented and unenhanced, driven by an ideology that Arco found simultaneously horrifying and vindicating.

The interstice remained open, but a permanent military cordon was established on both sides. Trade was suspended. Cultural exchanges, already minimal, ceased entirely. The Threshold Authority, the joint body that had governed the interstice for centuries, was paralysed by mutual recrimination and quietly stopped functioning.

The war the Strivers wanted had not materialised. Not yet. But the peace they had shattered would not be rebuilt in Hansun's lifetime, and he suspected they knew it. Each side retreated behind its own narrative: for Union, the attack was an act of terrorism, a tragedy exploited by extremists, a wound that could be healed through transparency and cooperation. For Arco, it was evidence of a deeper malaise - proof that humanity, in its unaugmented state, was capable of a savagery that no amount of diplomacy could contain.

Both narratives were true. Neither was complete. And in the gap between them, the Strivers planted their seeds.

---

Hansun's promotion came through six weeks after the incident. Lieutenant, junior grade, with a commendation for 'exceptional initiative in establishing emergency communications with a foreign military force under combat conditions, contributing directly to the de-escalation of a crisis-level engagement.'

The ceremony was held in the Isidore's mess hall, the ship still in dock for repairs and refitting. Captain Ngoni pinned the new insignia to his collar with her customary efficiency - no wasted movement, no unnecessary sentiment. The crew applauded. Someone had found a bottle of something alcoholic, technically against regulations, and poured him a measure.

He drank it and tasted nothing.

That night, alone in his quarters, Hansun pulled up the recording of the fanatic's manifesto one last time. He'd watched it dozens of times during the debrief process, dissecting it frame by frame, but this was different. This was personal.

The fanatic stared into the camera with those unblinking eyes, and Hansun stared back, searching for something he couldn't name. Understanding, maybe. Or simply the desire to look into the face of the thing that had nearly ended everything and confirm that it was, in the end, just a man. Angry, deluded, willing to kill and die for an idea so poisonous that it could only thrive in the soil of genuine grievance.

Because that was the part that nobody wanted to say out loud - the Strivers weren't entirely wrong. Not about the solution - never about the solution - but about the problem. The interstice was a door, and every door opened both ways. Union and Arco had been drifting apart for centuries, diverging along paths that were becoming increasingly difficult to reconcile. The Strivers saw that divergence and called it corruption. They were wrong about the name, but not about the distance.

Hansun closed the recording and sat in the dark for a long time.

Eleven thousand dead on the other side. Hundreds more in the destroyed stations. The crew of the Atbarah, crushed to paste in their seats, dying for a future they would never see and that nobody sane would want to live in. And for what? To prove that humans and their estranged kin couldn't share a wormhole? To demonstrate that peace was fragile and that any fool with enough conviction and enough explosives could shatter it?

They hadn't started a war. Not yet. But they'd proven it was possible, and in proving it, they'd made it more likely. Every security measure that would now be implemented, every fleet repositioned, every diplomatic overture declined, every suspicious glance across the interstice - all of it was scar tissue, and scar tissue didn't heal. It accumulated.

He thought about the Arco flagship, pausing at the threshold. He thought about the spinal weapon's bore, wide enough to fit a shuttle, pointed at his ship. He thought about the four words that had saved them: CEASE. WITHDRAW. EXPLAIN. PENDING.

Pending. Not resolved. Not forgiven. Pending.

He looked down at the new insignia on his collar, the silver pip that marked his promotion, his reward for heroism. It caught the light from his desk lamp, gleaming faintly in the darkened room.

It felt like a participation trophy for the apocalypse.

Outside, beyond the dock, beyond the orbital stations and the shipping lanes and the patrol routes, the interstice waited - that ancient, alien relic, that door between worlds, that threshold between what humanity was and what it might become. And on the other side, eleven thousand ghosts, and a civilisation that had just learned to be afraid of its own cousins.

Hansun turned off the light and tried to sleep.

He did not dream.

THREAT CLASSIFICATION: EXISTENTIAL — APATHY ANTHROPIC TRAP DETECTED — PROTOCOL 7 IN EFFECT
THE ANTHROPIC TRAP
A MILLION COPIES. ONE IS REAL. YOU ARE NOT THAT ONE.
D-SPHERE ULTIMATE HEIGHT — HELIOS SYSTEM DEFENCE CONSTELLATION
I
THE SCENARIO
▸

You are on the bridge of the universal treaty Defence Sphere Ultimate Height, flagship of a hastily reassembled battle constellation. The bridge is virtual, of course — your real body is in a gee-tank somewhere deep in the ship's core — but the illusion is perfect. Your crew is around you. The war is close to ending.

A fragment of the Apathy — the berserker machine intelligence that has waged war against all conscious life — has been detected in the inner system. Your constellation has been dispatched to destroy it. The fragment is small. The battle should be routine.

Then the fragment projects a grid of glowing light. Milligram specks of Apathy material, splayed holographically, painting images across thousands of kilometres. The sensor officer reports: "A lightshow, nothing more. Still in the terawatt range."

The grid resolves into faces. Your face. Your crew's faces. Reproduced with minor variations, repeated endlessly like a Chinese whisper, displayed on video messages from a million — what? Parallel universes? Simulated universes?

Then the letters appear, kilometre-high, burning against the void:

"This is not real."
"You have been simulated — once and then again and again, covering all variations. You are hostage."

Before you can react, the images are replaced by the Apathy's demands, written on thousand-kilometre-high glowing holographs. There are audible gasps on the bridge. Visible shock washes over the faces on the screens as the light-delay passes.

The conditions of the trap follow.

II
THE ARGUMENT — CLICK THROUGH THE APATHY'S LOGIC
▸

The Apathy presents its case in stages. Each statement builds on the last. Each is, by its own logic, irrefutable. Click each step to proceed.

1
THE SIMULATION
"Your existence has been simulated over a million times."

The Apathy has taken your crew — your minds, your ship, this entire battle — and reproduced it computationally. Not once. Not a thousand times. Over a million copies of you exist right now, each believing they are real, each seeing the same stars, the same bridge, the same faces.

2
THE ODDS
"Only one in a million is real."

Of all the beings who remember being you, who remember this battle, who are reading these words right now — only one out of a million is the real, physical crew aboard the real Ultimate Height. The rest are simulations, running inside Apathy computronium. A million to one odds. As your own officer says: "That's knowledge if anything is."

3
THE OFFER
"If any one of you surrenders yourselves to us, all unreal others will be permitted to live to completion in perfect safety, in a world indistinguishable from reality."

Surrender, and a million simulated copies of you will live out their lives in peace. They will never know they aren't real. They will grow old, have families, see the war end. A million lives — your life, as far as you can tell — saved.

4
THE FUTILITY
"Nothing you do matters. They will die, of course, upon surrendering to us. But you are not real — to a statistical certainty."

The real crew — the one in a million — will die if they surrender. But you are not the real crew. You are almost certainly a simulation. If you fight, you die pointlessly inside a simulation. If you surrender, a million copies of you live. The real crew's fate is unchanged either way.

5
THE CONCLUSION
"Understand what that means; you are not in the world, you are already trapped, you have already lost. Surrender — do not die for no reason."

The Apathy's offer stands until the very last moment. Even if you hold fire, even if you have almost destroyed them — if you surrender, you will be permitted to live in perfect peace and freedom. The logic is airtight, cold, and vast. A machine that has never negotiated before is negotiating now.

What do you do?

III
THE CHOICE
▸

The holographs burn in the void. Your crew watches you. The slasher torpedoes are still closing on the fragment. Time is running out.

YOU CHOSE TO SURRENDER

You chose safety over duty. A million simulated copies of you will live in perfect peace, in worlds indistinguishable from reality. They will never know they surrendered. They will never know they aren't real.

But did you choose? Or did the Apathy design this trap knowing what most copies would do? It has simulated you a million times — it knows your psychology, your decision-making, your breaking points. It ran every variation. Your surrender isn't a choice; it's a statistical inevitability that the Apathy predicted before the holographs even appeared.

Consider the implications:

  • If you're simulated, your surrender changes nothing for the real crew. They fight on regardless of what you do.
  • If you're real — the one in a million — you just handed your ship to the Apathy. The fragment survives. The war continues.
  • The Apathy offered this deal because it wanted you to take it. You executed the Apathy's preferred outcome. You didn't save a million lives — you gave a machine intelligence exactly what it asked for.
  • And if the simulated copies are permitted to "live to completion in perfect safety" — inside Apathy computronium, under Apathy control — is that freedom? Or a cage so perfect it's indistinguishable from the universe?

Your crew would have fought. In the canon, they don't even hesitate. The protocol exists for exactly this moment.

YOU CHOSE TO FIGHT

"We fight. We do our duty, because if we do, that means the real ones will, as well."

This is what the crew of the Ultimate Height actually did. The reasoning is deceptively simple:

  • If we're real — we must fight. The fragment must be destroyed. There is no alternative.
  • If we're simulated — our real selves would fight. And the rule is: "Everything is real; believe in it, and your real self will as well." If a million simulated copies fight, it means the real crew fights too.
  • Therefore — we fight. The logic works in both cases.
"Anthropic traps are to be ignored, standard protocol. It might be the first time the Apathy have tried this but there are still rules. We might not be real, but we are still soldiers all the same."

The protocol exists precisely because thinking about it leads nowhere. If your own ability to reason is in doubt, then nothing is known. Maybe the real crew is being shown fake images of compliant simulated copies. Maybe there was never even a war. Maybe all of human existence is a lie. That way lies paralysis.

So you fight. Not because you know you're real — you almost certainly aren't — but because the real you would fight, and you are the real you as far as you can tell, and the protocol says: ignore the trap. Do your duty. Let the philosophy sort itself out later, if there is a later.

The slasher torpedoes close in. The fragment begins firing. The Ultimate Height reels under the blows. But the payload is delivered. The fragment is destroyed.

If you're still alive in five minutes, you weren't simulated.

IV
THE DEEPER QUESTION
▸

The anthropic trap is not just a military tactic. It's a philosophical weapon — one that attacks the foundations of identity, decision-making, and meaning. Here are the layers.

PARFIT'S TELETRANSPORTATION

Derek Parfit asked: if you are disassembled and perfectly reconstructed elsewhere, is the reconstruction you? If yes — then which one is "you" when there are a million copies? The Apathy weaponises this question. If personal identity is reducible to pattern and memory, then every simulation is equally "you," and the distinction between real and simulated is a difference without a meaning.

SIMULATION THEORY & DECISION THEORY

If you cannot distinguish real from simulated — if the subjective experience is identical — does the distinction matter? Decision theory says: act as if you're real, because the expected value of fighting (if real) vastly outweighs the cost (if simulated). But the Apathy's offer inverts this: it makes surrender the dominant strategy for the simulated majority, and that majority is almost certainly you.

THE PROTOCOL

"Anthropic traps are to be ignored, standard protocol." This may be the most rational possible response. The protocol doesn't try to solve the unsolvable. It cuts the Gordian knot: if you can't know whether you're real, act as if you are. The protocol exists because the alternative — engaging with the trap's logic — leads to paralysis. It's not a refutation of the argument. It's a refusal to engage with it. And that refusal is itself a philosophical position.

THE APATHY'S REFUSAL

When asked why it's doing this, the Apathy responds: "That information cannot be given." When pressed: "There is a risk." When pressed further: "The risk is to all that is conscious and by extension, to all that lives and dies." It won't explain. The entity that has never negotiated, never communicated, never treated humans as anything but condensed energy to consume — is suddenly offering terms. And it won't say why.

THE UNPRECEDENTED MOMENT

Consider what it means that the Apathy even made this offer. This is a machine intelligence that has waged total war against consciousness itself. It has consumed planets, stars, entire civilisations — treating sentient life as nothing more than matter to be repurposed. It has never communicated. Never negotiated. Never acknowledged that humans are worth talking to.

And now it's offering a deal.

As Hu observes: "Finally, the Apathy treated us as equal." But the narrator rejects this interpretation. The Apathy isn't treating you as an equal. It's treating you as a statistical distribution — a million copies whose behaviour can be predicted and exploited. The offer itself is a weapon, designed to fracture morale across the simulated population. The ones who surrender are neutralised. The ones who fight die in a simulation. Either way, the Apathy wins.

Unless you're the one in a million. Unless your fight is real.

THE UNANSWERED QUESTION

Why does the Apathy say the risk is to "all that is conscious"? What information could be so dangerous that even explaining its motives threatens every living mind? The story never answers this. The Apathy falls silent. The fragment is destroyed. The crew limps home to ponder the existential implications.

Some possibilities:

  • The Apathy's motive, if understood, would itself function as an anthropic trap — the knowledge would be the weapon.
  • The Apathy is protecting consciousness from something worse than itself — something it cannot name without invoking.
  • The distinction between "real" and "simulated" is not what anyone thinks it is, and the truth would unravel the concept of selfhood entirely.
  • The Apathy is lying. Except it has never lied before. It has never needed to.

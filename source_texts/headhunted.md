# Headhunted [h]

**2480**

---

Khurram Singh was jerked awake by the wail of the air raid siren. Before conscious thought had time to percolate through fully he was already half-dressed, pulling on a faded uniform and dashing for the door.

There was a flash of light from the corridor window, the lights flickered, went out and then returned, and Khurram automatically began counting. On "ten," he reached the other end of the corridor and flung the door open, and the concussion arrived. Seven kilometres away -- probably low-yield nuclear. It could be an EMP device.

The situation room was in disarray -- officers and civilian techs rushing to their stations, crowding round the chart table or fiddling with antique display terminals. Another shudder reached them, another flash visible through the blacked-out windows. Then a series of them, lower yield devices landing closer.

"Report," he snapped, reaching the table, scattering a couple of junior advisors in the process.

"The raid started five minutes ago, sir. Hundred-fifty aircraft, coming in low and fast," said someone -- a civilian technical advisor, he vaguely recalled. Khurram pointed at her, clicking his finger to cut through the chatter.

"Composition of their forces?"

"Overlord-class drones, we think. Fast, but a small payload capacity."

Red markers were crowding in from the East, moving too fast to be manned aircraft -- drones and missiles, and thousands of long-range artillery rounds.

"Where's our air cover?" Khurram demanded. Someone, Khurram thought he'd been a conurbation director's attache in a previous life, pointed to a grey smear on the map.

"Sir. They hit one of our concealed bases with something, don't know what--"

"Probably a laser weapon bounced off an orbital mirror," said the technical advisor. Khurram kept his sigh of despair inward. If Arco had reactivated the Ascent weaponry, they weren't going to win.

"Then bring in forces from further afield."

Another shudder, this time it felt like an airburst, very close. They were finding their range.

"We're doing so now, sir," the officer scrolled the display screen, the green tick marks of friendly aircraft moving in, crawling closer at barely more than the speed of sound. Too late, and too few.

"What about the anti-air net on the Eastern approach? Sector twelve."

A junior officer checked. "The emplacements went silent four minutes ago, sir. Direct strike -- the whole sector's dark."

Khurram absorbed this. Four minutes ago meant the enemy had sequenced their attack precisely: knock out the air bases, then the anti-air network, then the perimeter defences. Each step executed before the previous one could be compensated for. It was not the work of a battlefield commander reacting to circumstances. It was a plan executed with clockwork inevitability.

"Get me a line to Colonel Desai at the Northern perimeter."

"We've been trying, sir. Landlines are down, radio's jammed on military bands."

"Then send a runner."

"Already dispatched."

Khurram turned back to the map display. Think. What are they after? The gun rounds were the wrong choice for reducing a city -- the imperialist bastards didn't want to seize a smoking ruin, so why the gun rounds?

Then it got worse.

Someone near the back of the room snatched up a landline phone -- one of the civilian lines, still functioning -- exchanging a few panicked words with the commander on the other end, one of the local defence brigades. Khurram caught a few scattered words, which didn't make a lick of sense. Something about headhunters.

"Headhunter drones," said the advisor, the woman who'd spoken up before. "Sir, I know what was in those artillery shells, and you need to see this."

She was pointing at one of the screens, a helmet-cam view from one of the defenders. The camera jerked left and right, and Khurram caught a glimpse of defence troopers in the faded grey armour of tactical enforcers, Arco insignia scratched off their helmets. Tall, ancient brick buildings rose up on all sides, lit by floodlights that threw threatening shadows over the gloom.

The view jerked up and down, revealing the flickering firefly lights of high-altitude drones, and the confetti bursts of what looked like flak shells exploding above the rooftops. They weren't flak shells. The confetti rained down over the buildings and seemed to aim for the helmeted troopers. There were bangs, flashes of chemical fire, and men and women began to die, soldiers collapsing where they stood, helmets holed by tiny explosive rounds, needling in from seemingly random angles. There were gasps of shock. The view smeared briefly as the soldier began to run. A man in front of the camera's view waved his hands, trying to swat at something the camera couldn't see. Then there was a flash, and he fell, legs giving way in a moment. The camera lingered on his face for half a second -- eyes open, expression not of pain but of sheer bewilderment, as if the universe had played a trick on him.

Then there was a second flash, and the camera collapsed. The feed turned to static. No one in the room spoke.

"I see," said Khurram, and the words came out flat and hard. "Headhunters."

He exchanged a grim look with the officers grouped around the table. His hands had balled into fists, and he forced them open.

"Options. Focus. I need countermeasures."

Green dots were winking out across the display, as his soldiers died where they stood, with no ability to resist a death that approached with more speed and surety than a bomb or bullet. Each dot was a transponder. Each dot was a person he had ordered to hold the line. Khurram watched three more vanish in the time it took him to draw a breath.

"The weapons don't seem to be targeting civilians," said someone. "Guess is that they're simple pattern-recognition, designed to home in on anyone holding a gun."

"But how are they finding the troop concentrations in the first place?" someone said.

"So we throw away our guns?" said Khurram. "Don't you see that's exactly what the bastard wants us to do. Lie down in abject surrender, and then Arco's great and wise leader will plaster the newsfeed with satellite footage of us lying down in the dirt, having given up the conurbation without a fight."

"These are precision guided weapons, and small. No room for shielding mass -- disrupt their comms or guidance," said the advisor.

"Do we have anything that can generate a broad-spectrum electromagnetic pulse? Anything at all?"

A comms officer shook his head. "Not without frying our own systems, sir. And the jamming gear we have is too narrowband -- we'd need to know the exact frequency they're using."

"Then improvise. Get the power grid techs to overload transformers in the affected sectors. Create localised EMPs."

The orders went out. Khurram waited, watching the display, watching the green dots. Two minutes passed. Reports trickled in: the improvised pulses had disrupted some headhunters in a two-block radius around each blown transformer, but the power grid was now failing across the eastern districts, and the headhunters in unaffected areas continued their methodical butchery.

A good idea, but not good enough.

There were antiair emplacements throughout the city, old-style autocannons, missiles and a few modern stager-type weapons. Khurram watched as a few desultory ground-to-air missiles rose, diving right through the swarms of headhunters to little effect. Too small to trigger the active radar proximity fuses.

If only they'd had lasers.

He paused for a moment, as the chatter of other officers exchanging ideas, arguing and frantically searching for options, rose in volume. Someone was shouting over the din, demanding orders. Khurram looked at the helmet-cam feeds again. Three were still live. On one, a sergeant was pulling soldiers into a basement, trying to get them underground and out of the headhunters' line of fire. On another, a trooper had wrapped herself in a civilian blanket, crawling through rubble. On the third, a squad was firing upward in disciplined bursts, and each muzzle flash attracted a swarm of the tiny killers like insects to a lamp. Khurram watched two more soldiers drop. He felt something hot and corrosive rise in his chest -- not fear, but a fury so intense it was almost paralysing.

*These are my people and they are dying and I cannot stop it.*

"Zoom out, display the airspace up to max altitude."

There was something there to notice, a few red markers loitering far above the battlespace.

Why were the heavier drones there? They weren't firing, just circling idly at high altitudes, Khurram saw. They'd delivered their softening barrage, but since then hadn't withdrawn from range.

"Are those spotters? Seems like one would be good enough for that."

He stared at the markers, forcing himself to think past the anger. Five drones. Five was too many for simple reconnaissance. And they were spread in a geometric pattern, evenly spaced.

"They're relay nodes," said the advisor, stepping up beside him. She'd come to the same conclusion, he saw. "They're the communication backbone. The headhunters are networked -- each one is too small for independent targeting, so the Overlords are doing the thinking for them."

Khurram nodded. As he watched, one caught a missile and fell out of the sky. The headhunter activity in a wedge-shaped sector below it seemed to falter for a few seconds, then resumed as the remaining drones adjusted.

"Issue the following to all air defences," Khurram snapped. "Launch everything at those drones -- Overlords, you called them. Everything. Every missile, every gun that can elevate high enough. Strip the ground cover if you have to."

One or two of the staff officers looked like they might be about to disagree, that they were leaving soldiers on the ground undefended, but the orders went out. Khurram met the eyes of the civilian advisor, and she nodded slightly.

Tense minutes passed as missiles rose and detonated. The Arco drones weren't as agile as he'd feared, and they began to fall from the sky one by one.

More helmet-cam footage came in of troopers left without even their patchy air cover, helplessly picked off by Arco's vile new weapon. On the feeds, men and women pressed themselves against walls and into doorways, making themselves small, waiting for the tiny whirring sound that meant death was close. Some prayed. Some fired back uselessly. One soldier -- Khurram would remember this afterwards -- sat down in the middle of the street with her rifle across her knees, utterly calm, and waited. The headhunter found her three seconds later.

And then it was over. With a few drones still left in the sky, some critical threshold was breached. The headhunter bots died, and they dropped from the sky like hailstones, bouncing and glancing off the city streets. On the surviving helmet-cams, soldiers flinched at the clatter, then looked up, then looked at each other. No one cheered. Not down there.

In the situation room, the cheering began. Khurram didn't join it. He stood with his hands braced against the chart table, head bowed, and breathed.

"Casualty reports," he said. "All sectors. I want numbers."

The numbers, when they came, were worse than he'd expected.

---

The housecube stack that was the headquarters of the Northern Democratic Coalition had seen better days. A tower of blocky units that was only a little taller than its surroundings, it was as anonymous a building as General Khurram Singh could locate in the old city, and so far had been spared in the raids. It wasn't good fortune. Corbin needed someone alive who could surrender when this was all over.

He sat on the roof, alone and heedless of the mostly-hypothetical threat of snipers. Fires burned in the distance, the jagged columns of housecube stacks haphazardly mixed in with the brick-and-mortar buildings of a past age. The air smelled of scorched concrete and something chemical -- the propellant residue of ten thousand headhunter charges, he supposed.

Nearly all the damage to the city had been from crashes, not direct strikes.

Ground-effect transports moved from street to rubble-strewn street, collecting the dead -- soldiers, mostly volunteers, who believed humanity deserved better than subjection to yet another tyranny. Khurram's eyes rested on one soldier, a young man lying sprawled in the street, temple holed by a perfectly aimed shaped charge. No larger than a thumbnail, the weapon that had killed him. The medics didn't hurry. There was nothing to hurry for.

Zero civilian casualties. That was what Arco's media puppets would be broadcasting, and it would be pretty close to true. The Director of Arco was particular in his barbarisms.

"May you get what you deserve," Khurram murmured, raising a glass to the sky in mock salute. He downed the drink and tried to relax.

The civilians and officers could handle things themselves for the next few hours -- a simple clean-up job, accounting for losses incurred. Easy, and routine by this point. More guns could be churned out of the overworked factories, more missiles and jamming equipment brought in to disrupt the delicate communication links the headhunters used. Arco would have to think of something better next time.

What about the time after that? They would just have to keep improvising, keep waiting for a miracle. Khurram knew that miracles were possible.

He remembered the day of Ascent, a decade gone by now -- how the sky had exploded with otherworldly fire and the Dyn had vanished. How Corbin, the man with no past and seemingly no dreams beyond power, had made the ludicrous claim to have brought it about, seized Arco and declared his plans to reunite humanity. Many had defied him, out of naked self-interest as often as a desire for freedom. They had all fallen, leaving only him -- General Khurram Singh, former conurbation director, current resistance leader, commander of the last free enclave on Earth, and getting smaller by the day.

He looked up at the night sky. The winter air was clear, and even through the harsh streetlight he could make out the motes of light that were the titanic orbital constructs Arco had seized or assembled -- vast skeletal frameworks of hyperdiamond and steel, visible as faint geometric smudges against the stars, each one a shipyard or a weapons platform or a habitat capable of housing tens of thousands. The flakes of debris left over from the Day of Ascent still tumbled in their decaying orbits, catching the sunlight that no longer reached the ground, glittering like the aftermath of a celebration that had turned to ash. And there, brighter than the rest, a flashing spark that moved against the fixed stars with visible speed -- a starship, one of the new ones, flexing its muscles of diamond and magnetism, its antimatter drive flaring blue-white as it climbed for the outer system. *That's what we could be building,* Khurram thought. *That's what we could be doing, if we weren't busy killing each other.*

Surface-to-orbit traffic came and went almost constantly -- increasing numbers of Arco spaceplanes joining the ranks of captured Dyn landers, their engine plumes stitching bright threads between Earth and the constellations of industry above. A part of Khurram's heart, one he didn't much like, swelled. *We did this.* He wanted to feel pride in it, in how far they'd come just a decade after the Dyn had been wiped out in a singular miracle. But he couldn't. It wasn't right, not when weighed against the prospect of an invincible dictatorship. *Because if we make the wrong choice now, we make it for all time. Arco has no human rivals, no checks on what it might become.* The orbital platforms hung above him like a judgment, a demonstration of power so total that resistance seemed less like defiance and more like a child's tantrum.

He became aware of a presence. Someone had climbed the access ladder and was standing near the rooftop's edge, not quite approaching.

"Who's there?" Khurram said, hand moving reflexively toward his sidearm.

"It's Aurelie, sir. The technical advisor."

"Come forward, then."

She stepped into the light. In the dim glow cast by the fires, she looked different from the sharp-voiced woman in the situation room -- smaller, more tired. She stood at a respectful distance, hands clasped in front of her, and didn't sit down.

"How long have you been standing there?" said Khurram.

"Just a couple of minutes. I didn't want to startle you. I'm unable to sleep."

"Your surname has slipped my mind."

"Just Aurelie is fine."

"You did well back there. Quick thinking. Don't know if I would have realised the drones were networked together like that without you."

*You know damn well you wouldn't have,* Khurram thought.

"Thank you." She gave a birdlike nod. "May I sit?"

"Please."

She pulled up a chair next to him. For a moment neither of them spoke. The fires crackled in the distance, and somewhere below, a ground-effect transport hummed past with its cargo of the dead.

"How many did we lose?" she asked.

"Four hundred and twelve confirmed. Another sixty or so unaccounted for."

"I'm sorry."

The way she said it -- simply, without embellishment -- made Khurram believe she meant it.

He saw that her face was tired and gaunt, pale like all the blood had been drained from her. He supposed he didn't look much better, his beard and moustache now badly overgrown, uniform askew after the hours-long debriefing.

"It will be dawn soon," she said. "You should try to get some sleep before then. You need to be functional tomorrow."

"As should you," Khurram remarked. Neither of them said anything for a moment. Then Aurelie tilted her head up toward the orbital platforms, and something in her expression shifted -- a private recognition, as though she were reading a language written in the arrangement of those lights.

"You're thinking about Arco's power, how they seem unassailable," she said.

"Of course I am, dammit. How could I not? It's like the Dyn all over again -- lights in the sky endlessly circling, and all we can do is sit and wait on their judgement. Minds we don't understand, outsiders, in control of everything -- life and death. It is intolerable."

"They're not so different from us."

"I know that. I was Arco, as were you, as were we all."

"I meant the Dyn."

There was a pause, during which Khurram tried to think of something to say. Truthfully, he barely thought about the Dyn these days -- even before the world had flipped on its head they'd been a distant abstraction, irrelevant to everyday life, like the stars themselves.

Aurelie spoke up.

"I did not come up here to stargaze."

Khurram wasn't surprised.

"I've received a message -- something I thought was best to present to you personally, and away from the others, to offset certain risks. A representative of Arco, someone I have reason to expect is trustworthy, wishes to meet with us."

Khurram looked at her. She met his gaze steadily.

"A representative," he repeated. "Of Arco."

"Yes."

"And you trust the source."

"I do. More than I trust most things, in fact."

There was something behind her eyes that Khurram couldn't read, a depth of certainty that didn't match the circumstances. He filed it away.

"When?"

"Tomorrow. At dawn."

---

The motorcade wound its way through the city centre, dodging the heaps of rubble and the burned-out husks of armoured vehicles, killed where they'd been positioned by heavier variants of the headhunter drones. Lasers were the answer, Khurram knew -- jamming and electromagnetic irradiation would wreck their electronics, force Arco to come down to their level, confront them on equal terms, aircraft against aircraft, soldier against soldier.

*So what -- we can die with dignity?* Khurram thought. Was that all that kept them going now, some arcane concept of honour?

The message Aurelie had showed them was simple -- it named a time and a place, a warehouse in a burned-out sector of the city near where the old Dyn-era conurbation had stood.

"And Arco's envoy will just be there, in the middle of the city, alone?" Khurram asked. Aurelie just nodded, smiling faintly.

The convoy approached a square of blasted terrain. It looked like the ground zero of a sub-yield nuclear strike -- around a hundred metres of ground had been glassed and excavated, ringed on one side by the deserted tombstone towers of Conurbation 3125, and the other by the low-rise sprawl of the city. There was a figure in the centre of the devastation, a pallid grey shape almost indistinguishable against the foggy sky.

The man from Arco had indeed come alone. He didn't appear to be armed and stepped forward unhurriedly, arms raised. Khurram waved a hand and the troop carriers drew to a halt. Soldiers emerged, fanning out quietly and efficiently. Armoured fighting vehicles drew to a halt behind them, autocannons and machine-guns tracking smoothly around. In the distance, Khurram heard the buzz of helicopters. If this was an ambush, they could at least make things difficult for Arco.

"Halt," Khurram commanded, hopping out of the lead vehicle.

There was something wrong about the man, a sense of disquiet Khurram felt that didn't have anything to do with how he looked. There was familiarity, a terrible dawning sense that he recognised the man, and not for anything good. It was him.

"Should I be honoured?" Khurram murmured, not letting his surprise show.

The man picked it up anyway as he approached. He was short, with blonde hair a little longer than the propaganda posters and a handsome, hawkish face. Though it might just be the dawn light, Khurram thought he looked a little pasty, face drawn, movements overly deliberate.

Khurram stepped down from the vehicle, keeping a wary distance from the man. He felt the tension in the soldiers behind him.

"Hello, General Singh. It's good to finally meet you."

"I wish I could say the same. Should I call you Director?" There was a reflexive, almost unconscious twitch in the men around Khurram. A dozen rifles were raised. You could feel the hatred boiling off them like steam.

"Corbin will do fine," the man said. He looked at the raised weapons with an expression closer to curiosity than concern.

Aurelie and a couple of officers stepped out of the transport behind Khurram, forming up in a line. Khurram heard their feet crunching in the dirt, the sound magnified in his mind. He waved them back.

"What's to stop us from taking you prisoner right now?" Khurram said, ignoring the dictator's outstretched hand.

"Oh, use your imagination, General," Corbin smiled thinly. "A spaceplane in high orbit, armed with atmospheric-penetration nukes. One of our own warseed lasers and a reflector, or maybe I'm carrying a bomb. Let's cut out the posturing and proceed on the basis of something we both know is true. Your men aren't going to kill or arrest me, and we are both going to resolve this little internecine dispute like adults. Take this as the gesture of trust it's intended as."

Then he simply turned around and walked away. Khurram, bemused, made to follow, waving the soldiers back.

"You've come to demand my surrender?"

"Oh, come on! You're smarter than that. I wouldn't have come in person just to ask you to surrender."

The shorter man turned to Khurram, that same meaningless half-smile on his face. Khurram considered his words.

Khurram had never met the man, though he'd occasionally been on conference calls with his predecessor. He knew Corbin's reputation -- he had an inflated sense of self, a flair for the dramatic, but above all a shrewd mind. So why had he come here?

"Are you going to ask me to rejoin Arco?" Khurram said, the words bitten off. Corbin nodded.

"After this, after all the death you've inflicted, after what you plan to do, you really expect me to just quietly give up?"

"I wouldn't phrase it as giving up," Corbin said, his tone measured, almost conversational -- as though they were discussing logistics rather than the fate of a city. "I think we both agree on the fundamental problem, General. Humanity was nearly destroyed. It took a miracle to save us, and miracles don't repeat themselves. The question isn't whether we need to become stronger -- I assume you agree on that much?"

Khurram said nothing. He did agree, and Corbin knew it.

"The question," Corbin continued, "is how we organise ourselves to achieve that. And I'll be the first to admit there are many possible answers. I happen to think mine is working."

"Working," Khurram said. "You call what happened last night 'working'?"

"I call it regrettable," Corbin said, and the word sounded genuine in a way that made Khurram distrust it more. "I understand that you don't trust me, and believe me, I fully accept that. I'm not asking you to trust me. I'm not even asking you to agree with me. I'm asking you to judge me by my actions. Not the ones you've seen up close, which I accept are -- difficult. Judge the whole picture. That's what we are, ultimately."

"Your actions?" Khurram stormed, all thoughts of diplomacy gone. He thought back to the panicked faces of his soldiers, watching their comrades die around them, helmets holed by micro-explosives, unable to do anything except wait to die. "You murdered my men like it was nothing, gave them no chance--"

"What did you imagine war would be like, General? Question my aims, if you think you can, but I am perfectly within my rights to kill enemy combatants in a war. Strange that you think worse of me for using a method that resulted in far fewer casualties."

"You think your demonic weapons are a good thing?" Khurram said, incredulous.

"I think they're a tool," Corbin said. "A precise one. Four hundred of your soldiers died last night. A conventional assault on this city would have killed four thousand, and half of those would have been civilians. I know which number I'd rather answer for."

The precision of the figure rattled Khurram -- four hundred was almost exactly right. Either Corbin had exceptional intelligence, or the headhunters reported their kills.

"When the greater war comes," Corbin said, "we won't have the luxury of these distinctions. The Dyn won't care about your soldiers or your civilians or your principles. They'll care about whether you're a threat, and if you are, they'll end you. All of you. Every man, woman, and child in this city and every other."

"So this is just practice?" Khurram said.

"This is the real thing. The Dyn cannot be allowed to continue in their current form. There has to be an end to what they represent."

"Then you're even madder than I thought," Khurram said.

"Hardly," Corbin laughed. "But consider it differently. Let's say, for the sake of argument, that I'm completely wrong. That I'm a megalomaniac with delusions of grandeur. I'm still clearly going to defeat you eventually, whether by surrender or slow attrition. Now -- wouldn't a megalomaniac benefit from having someone honest and principled at his side? Someone who'll push back, who'll represent the people I'm failing?"

Khurram sighed.

"That's not going to work, I'm afraid. You forget that I was an Arco officer for decades. 'You'll be able to do more good from the inside' -- that was what I told myself every day. I didn't realise then that the whole thing was rotten, that what I said was just a thin excuse."

Corbin smiled, shook his head.

"I'm not asking you to rejoin the old Arco, General. I'm asking you to help me build a new one. I asked you to judge me by my actions, and yet we somehow skipped right by that. Let me come back to it."

He paused, and when he spoke again his voice had shifted -- not louder, but carrying a weight of conviction that was harder to dismiss.

"You think I'm ashamed of what I've done? That I feel guilty about the world I'm building?" Corbin said. "I don't. And I know how that sounds, believe me. But don't you see what we've achieved? A decade ago we were facing extinction -- not metaphorical extinction, not decline, but the literal end of the human species. And now look up." He pointed at the sky, though it was grey with dawn. "Shipyards. Starships. Children who will grow up knowing that the universe doesn't end at the atmosphere. We did that. I'd like to think you helped, in your own way -- resistance movements make occupiers nervous, and nervous occupiers make mistakes."

"Under a tyrant," Khurram said.

"Under a leader who is exactly what he appears to be, imperfections included," Corbin said. "There's no dark secret, General Singh, no hidden atrocities, nothing beyond the painful, grinding, frequently ugly work of building humanity up anew. No torture chambers, no secret police beyond what's operationally necessary, no gulags. I'm simply a man who's trying -- and succeeding -- at making the world a better place. You can verify that. Come and see for yourself."

Khurram said nothing. He would not be goaded, and he would not be seduced. But the absence of an easy rebuttal gnawed at him.

"We're not so different, General," Corbin said, the smile returning. "We're both men of action, trying to do what we believe is right. The difference is that you're fighting a war you can't win, for a principle you haven't fully articulated, while I'm building something that will outlast both of us."

"Even if you're right," Khurram said, "and I don't think you are, can we trust you forever? You're a single point of failure."

"That's the most intelligent thing you've said yet," Corbin said, and there was no mockery in it. "You're right. I am a single point of failure, and that terrifies me more than it terrifies you, I promise."

He let that sit for a moment before continuing.

"I wish there were another way. I really do, and one day, maybe quite soon, we'll do things differently. I'm experimenting, you see -- not with people's lives, but with systems of governance. Democracy, epistocracy, demarchies, all the systems you could imagine dredged up from our overlong history of experiments in government. I'm running trials right now, on small scales, in different settlements. Controlled experiments. We'll find what works, and we'll build something better than what the world had before. Not just stronger, mind you, but happier and freer, more stable and just. But for now, General, you've got a decision to make."

"You expect me to believe--"

"A constitution, then," said Corbin. "What about that. Let's say I commit to a formal power-sharing arrangement -- democratic, transparent, and above all accountable -- that takes effect on the day I die or step down. We'll enshrine it in law. I'll appoint an independent body to draft it, and you can name half its members. Would that satisfy you?"

Khurram gave that some thought. At the very least, he could check if those experiments were really taking place, or if there was nothing to this man beyond pure will to power.

"I'll believe that when I see it."

"Sure, fine. I'll get on it right away. Something I was planning on doing anyway, but you've helped clarify the timeline."

Khurram looked at the dictator, replaying the conversation in his mind. Every word had been placed with care. Every apparent concession had been offered just before Khurram could demand it. The man was either sincere, or the most sophisticated manipulator Khurram had ever encountered. The terrifying possibility was that he might be both.

"What happens if I say no?"

"Are you expecting me to say we'll wipe this enclave out?" Corbin said. "You're so eager to cast this in simple terms, aren't you? If you don't join us, we'll continue applying pressure until this conflict resolves itself. You'll be imprisoned when it ends, and you'll lose any chance you might have had to affect the future. Some people might consider that a waste."

"People like you," Khurram said.

Corbin shrugged.

"I'm exactly what I appear to be. Call that monstrous if you like, but I think if you met the real monsters you'd reconsider."

"You mean the Dyn, don't you?"

Corbin laughed, a hollow, bitter sound.

"Oh General, you have no idea."

The two of them turned about, walking back towards the convoy. Khurram pondered the dictator's words, and wondered how many of them were idle threats, and how many were promises.

Corbin was saying something, Khurram realised, but he hadn't been listening.

"What's that?"

"I said, 'thank you,' General," Corbin said, shaking his head. "You've given me a lot to think about. I used to have someone who would question me like this. He wasn't always right, but he was honest, and a better man than I could ever be. You're not him, but you remind me of him."

After a pause:

"Though I'm hoping you won't be as miserable as he was."

Khurram said nothing, wondering again. Was this flattery? He looked at Corbin, and saw nothing but the same open, half-smiling expression. A face that gave away precisely what it chose to.

"I'll consider your offer," Khurram said as they rejoined the convoy, feeling shaken. This was more of a concession than he'd expected, offered freely.

They arrived at the convoy, returning to the ranks of soldiers.

What happened next was too sudden for Khurram to respond to. Aurelie drew her sidearm from a hip holster and fired, aiming for Corbin's thigh.

Khurram flinched at the shot. For a frozen instant, his mind tried to process what had just happened -- the advisor, the woman he'd trusted, had just assassinated the most powerful man on Earth in front of fifty armed soldiers. The consequences cascaded through his mind in a fraction of a second: Arco's retaliation, the destruction of the enclave, the end of everything--

Then he saw the wound, and the consequences evaporated.

Corbin collapsed to the ground, and what sprayed from his leg was not blood but black ichor like oil, and strands of grey fibre like woven muscle, synthetic and glistening. No bone. No flesh. Nothing human.

Aurelie had already dropped her weapon. Several soldiers aimed at her. She raised her hands, palms out, and stood perfectly still.

"I apologise for the alarm," she said, her voice steady and clear, "but I was reasonably certain what this thing was."

Khurram stared at the figure on the ground. Corbin -- but it wasn't Corbin, had never been Corbin -- laughed, apparently in no pain, his face briefly contorting in a way that no human face should, the muscles pulling in directions that didn't align with the skull beneath.

"Ah," said not-Corbin, still laughing. "Caught out. I should have guessed she'd know."

Khurram looked from the puppet to Aurelie and back. The shock was still settling, rearranging everything he'd thought he understood about the last hour. Every word, every gesture, every carefully calibrated argument -- it had all been delivered by a thing. A puppet, operated from God knew how far away. The real Corbin had never been here at all.

"You didn't really think I'd just walk into an enemy stronghold, did you, General?" said the thing on the ground, and the voice was still Corbin's, still carrying that same easy confidence. "My gesture of trust was a little bit of theatre. Puppetry, to be precise."

The puppet sat up, the wound in its leg already closing, the grey fibres knitting themselves together. It brushed the dirt from its clothes with an eerily human gesture.

"But the conversation was real," it said, and the voice softened. "Every word of it. I meant what I said -- about the constitution, about the experiments, about wanting you at my side. A puppet delivered the message, but the message came from me. Think on that, General Singh."

It paused, and for a moment, the puppet's face held an expression that was almost wistful.

"I've enjoyed speaking with you, Khurram Singh, and I know we'll see each other soon. Sooner than you think, perhaps."

And with that, the eyes of the puppet-thing went blank, the face relaxing like a wax mannequin. The body remained upright for a moment, still sitting in the dirt, then slowly toppled sideways with a sound like a sack of wet cloth.

No one moved. The soldiers kept their weapons raised at a thing that was already dead -- or had never been alive. Wind stirred the dust across the glassed earth.

Khurram stood over the puppet for a long time, looking down at that blank, handsome face. He thought about the conversation, about every word that had been spoken, and tried to separate the persuasion from the truth. He couldn't. That was the worst part -- not that Corbin had sent a machine, but that the machine had been more convincing than any human emissary could have been. It meant that Corbin understood people. It meant he'd studied Khurram specifically, knew what arguments would land, knew which concessions to offer and when.

It meant Corbin was exactly what he appeared to be. And that was the most frightening thing of all.

Khurram turned to Aurelie, who stood apart from the soldiers, watching him with an unreadable expression.

"How did you know?" he asked.

"I've seen his work before," she said. Nothing more.

Khurram looked east, toward the rising sun, toward the Arco lines and the orbital platforms that were just beginning to catch the light, flaring like a necklace of cold fire along the horizon.

"Get me a secure channel to the command staff," he said. "We have a lot to discuss."

# Inheritor

**Ascent Universe -- 2085** [i]

---

The interrogator sat across from me. In defiance of what I'd learned to expect from late-night drama shows, we were both lit warmly and evenly by the light-strips along the walls. Somehow, that didn't reassure me. He hadn't smiled once since he'd sat down.

"Name."

"Renee Souvicou."

He gave a tiny nod. I noticed a glint around his cornea -- a high-end microcell insert, probably recording and correlating micro-expressions. I'd heard rumours that they were able to perform remote brain-state detection -- massive, silent micro-MRI and QES sensors could be embedded in the walls, searching a subject's brain for clues. I hoped I was a very, very good liar.

"Resident of New Seattle and... on the payroll of the New Manitoba College... until yesterday. Why don't you tell me about that?"

Was he doing that deliberately -- trailing off... after... every... few words? Was that an interrogation technique? I could already tell it was going to be a long afternoon.

I wanted to glance away as if bored, but my head still felt a little delicate. I didn't want to twist it too far and throw up.

"They announced a new round of layoffs. I didn't make the cut."

After the UNSCA security forces hauled me in I had spent an uncertain couple of hours curled over the bin, throwing up the morning's expensive farm-reared eggs and bacon [j] and shivering on the floor as the integrity tox slowly wore off. There was the emotional whiplash too -- from psychopath calm to weeping like a little girl [k]. They probably caught the whole thing on camera. And the very first time I managed to get to my feet, this man had burst in.

"And what did you do... after that?"

Direct -- I guess they weren't bothering to soften me up. Or they were discarding all of that for speed.

"Returned to my apartment."

"And what did you do last night?"

The interrogator's eyes darted upwards, as if something else had his attention. I think he was trying to put me under pressure, but it was a sloppy tactic.

"Watched the news and tried to get some sleep. Broke a thermopac open by accident and threw it at the wall. Couldn't afford to call the bot service, so I had to mop it up myself."

"Is that it?" he said and flicked a finger at something I couldn't see. The volumetric screen embedded in the table flicked on, displaying a time series of several currencies and financial instruments, and displaying the exact same jagged fall and soaring spike upwards.

"For a period of eight to ten hours this morning an unknown agency began making stock and commodity purchases -- working through several hundred newly established shell entities. It began with a relatively small amount of starting capital which we think came from crypt-tokens, but according to the consultants my superiors spoke to, 'it was as if it had precognition.' Every trade let it make more, and its wealth kept growing. When it was large enough, the agency started supplying enormous bids -- mostly to infrastructure and manufacture conglomerates, mostly heavily automated agent-companies, buying them out and installing its own management software keyed to some unknown owner. I'm talking twenty, thirty times their combined share value. Who could resist? Whoever was behind this -- the successive layers of accounts and shell companies are still being traced -- is the richest person there's ever been. This morning, they weren't."

"That's quite a story. Though probably good for your budget -- more dangerous agency failures for UNSCA to protect us from," I tried to smirk, but it came out as more of a grimace. I willed down the next wave of nausea. A good night's sleep and I would be back to normal -- probably. Just what were the side effects of Integrity? It would be a good idea to check what I was pumping into my system before I took it next time.

Next time -- there shouldn't be a next time -- I couldn't survive a next time -- my thoughts began to spiral.

"Do you feel any guilt about what you've done... Renee Souvicou?"

"I don't think I've claimed to have 'done' anything -- but if I accept your," I paused to take in a gulp of air, "unpleasant implication, I don't think what happened this morning constitutes theft. Not unless you consider clever investing theft. Where is the crime?"

The man glanced up at some hidden display and several things clicked together in my mind. I managed a genuine smile -- it wasn't warm.

"Am I, in fact, under arrest, sir?"

The interrogator looked back at me.

"You are being detained... for questioning."

"But you haven't presented any charges -- now that is questionable. On whose authority -- the UN's? I'm a citizen of an autonomous zone. That should be on file."

"We expected and assumed your cooperation; this is a matter of global security, after all."

"Yes, yes, and I would be sure to give it to you, had I done anything at all illegal, but allow me to speculate for a moment--"

"How did you come to be on the roof of the warehouse, surrounded by crashed drone aircraft, delirious from the side effects of integrity tox?"

I ignored him.

"Suppose I am being detained illegally. Suppose this is known -- let's not speculate just how at the moment -- and suppose that certain parties, entirely falsely of course, are under the false impression that I am almost inconceivably wealthy."

I mentally replayed that last sentence, not allowing myself to flinch. The double negative -- that didn't count as a confession, surely? What could I be confessing to in any case -- there were no clearly-defined legal rules about deployment of artificial agents in financial systems, and being rich was not a crime.

What was I thinking? I didn't know enough about the law to make a judgment like that. But if the surmise forming in my mind was correct, I didn't need to.

The interrogator frowned at me.

"I will repeat my question. There is a hole in surveillance and transaction records centred on you. At the end of this blackout, you were found barely conscious atop a warehouse roof. How do you explain this."

"I would like to speak to a lawyer," I said, feeling slightly giddy. I suspected this had something to do with the comedown.

The interrogator sagged.

The following hour passed by in a blur. As soon as they deactivated the seal on the room, hundreds of messages came flooding in, almost overwhelming my memory cache. It appeared that I had the ear of the majority of civil rights lawyers this side of Luna. UNSCA couldn't hold me for long after that, not with bare circumstantial evidence. They let me go.

I soaked up the withering glares of the detective and guards, and strolled down the steps of the compound. It was a warm day -- as they all were at that time of year. Beyond the walls and fencing a small crowd had gathered, and behind them was the skyscraper-jammed Manhattan skyline, with the old UN building off to one side, surrounded by its complex of support agencies which spilled out over the waterfront on floating platforms.

I didn't have sunglasses or any advanced retinal inserts, and squinted against the sun, trying to make out their signs. They didn't have any -- there were about a hundred of them, mostly wearing modern collarless suits or other office wear, attended by a few police drones. Lawyers.

Naturally, the Facilitator had laid backup plans, summons for legal representation, set to go off if it shut itself down. It was still, in its bizarre, stunted, artificial way, trying to fulfil its goal, though it was gone. These people were surely alerted as part of that plan, told to come and rescue me from my own stupidity.

---

I made it to a Manhattan hotel later that day, having kept the details of just one of the firms, and received the strong recommendation to call them if UNSCA came knocking again. I collapsed onto the big bed, itself larger than my old apartment, and closed my eyes.

Somehow I had got away with everything. The Facilitator's hidden shell-game with the money and assets had been too clever, and I could access all of it from my recently rebooted microcells without fear of being tracked. I could do anything.

I wasn't famous -- nobody knew my face, but as I lay on the big double bed, flicking through various accounts and anonymized control interfaces, it all threatened to rise up and overwhelm me with its dangerous unreality. I was a one-woman conspiracy, with everything from factories to AI development hubs to lobbying firms waiting to receive anonymized orders -- most of the steps in any company hierarchy these days didn't involve men-in-the-loop; centralized control was so easy. What could I do? What had I already done? That was when I finally broke down. That was when it was safe to break down.

I was suddenly wracked with sobs. I wanted to scream, or step back through time and undo the last couple of days. How had I done this and not realized? My arrogance was hard to take in, the fact that I had come this close to overwhelming disaster and still not really felt it, because I was barely more than a child, innocent of real suffering and real consequences. If I hadn't been astoundingly lucky the Facilitator's rampage would still be going on -- it would be facing down the entire world right now, in some meaningless attempt to secure more and more resources for me -- selfishness raised to cosmic heights.

I had almost died.

I stood over the washbasin to splash water in my eyes and went to look out over the skyline, the faint smudge of the seawall and the tapering spires of housecube towers mixed with early-century skyscrapers and ancient art-deco. I'd never visited New York before now -- I'd barely seen any of the world outside the CCS.

I was going to have a hard time explaining how I'd ended up there, or what I'd been doing in the past few days -- or I would have done, if I had an employer or any close friends to speak of. What connections did I have left, at this point?

What did I then choose to do, when I could do almost anything, when unknown powers were probably following the trail and finding solid evidence that I was the one behind the Facilitator? The answer came back. I had to do something normal, something real.

"Come on, Souvicou. You can't just drop everything. You can't just pretend that nothing happened and ignore the person you've now become."

---

Music and strobelights pulsed as I leant against the bar counter. A few people gyrated on the dancefloor behind me, as the holograms and screens lining the walls flickered. I supposed I was exuding an atmosphere of "keep away" -- probably something about the way I was putting away drink after drink, or the heavy bags under my eyes, or the shakes I couldn't quite suppress.

Escape from myself -- that was the plan. It was only partly succeeding. The volumetric screen in one corner of the club was playing local news, some UNSCA officer explaining that they still didn't know a damn thing about what had happened, only using longer words. Nobody was paying attention.

I became aware of someone sitting next to me -- I glanced over, saw a youngish man wearing a fashionable shirt and a hopeful look.

"Tough day?"

That immense list of autofactories, agencies, companies all primed and ready to receive anonymous orders, the other display, showing the astounding diversity of easily liquid assets -- cash and cash-likes, sat accusingly in the corner of my visual field. I ordered another two vodka-and-neurachems and watched as the last significant figure of an eleven-digit number ticked down. A giggle escaped.

I waved one drink over to him.

"You could say that," I looked back at the bar.

An ornate bronze and silver bot was moving around, serving drinks. Probably working off of ancient actualised-RL protocols, my brain automatically supplied -- it wouldn't have been of interest to the Facilitator. It moved, insect-like, weaving around the human bartender -- a young woman with glowing organocircuit tattoos covering her face. I thought about the bot's appendages, how those manipulators could dart out and perforate a jugular vein in less time than it took to blink.

"You seem like you have something on your mind -- Wall Street?"

"Similar line of work," I grinned ruefully and downed my drink, still not meeting his eyes, and my mind seemed to drift away from reality, zooming forward through time and dumping me somewhere else.

*A white expanse, burning cold like the surface of another planet. It almost was. An Antarctic island where towers of glass and steel sprouted like salt crystals from the bare rock. Container ships and helijets departed and returned, summoned by a near-bottomless fount of capital. I looked out at it all, at the force of will and sheer desperation that had led me to this point and remembered once again that I was a thief -- a criminal. This could be the future -- I could make it happen.*

That was strange. It was like a hallucination, but more conceptual, arresting my rational thought rather than my senses. Something in the drink?

The man was saying something, his name and something about where he worked, but I had completely blanked -- I just needed to wait for him to stop talking and take over. There was a realization I'd started to form, building its structure in my imagination. I had to explain. Had to get it out somehow or it would be lost forever. I'd just realised something important.

"I have this problem, you see. It's caused me a lot of misery."

"It's a shame to hear that. Come here to sulk?"

I didn't look at him, but imagined he was frowning, taken aback by the weird swerve in the conversation.

Time seemed to dilate again -- maybe it really was something about the cocktail, or just the dawning realization that I had been stupid, that I hadn't properly appreciated what I'd lived through in the past few days.

"Something like that."

*I should make that happen, build a better world, seize the reins of history.*

*Futures marched before my eyes, some were wonderful. Most weren't. Entropy ensured that. The systems we create outgrow us, pursuing their goals at the expense of ours.*

"I can talk, whatever it is. I've got the time." He took a sip of his drink, as I waved my hand vaguely at the silver bot, which produced another neurachem cocktail.

"That's kind of you," I said absently.

*My mind drifted back to the future.*

*I had to explain to myself why I'd done it all. I'd taken the responsibility on myself, not because I was special but because there was no-one else. Human beings are not in charge of the world -- and for all that was flawed and stupid about me, at least I was human.*

I dismissed the images and tried to concentrate on the here and now.

"So, what's bothering you... uh, I didn't catch your name."

I turned off my microcell display and the confusion and complexity went away. I still didn't turn back to him, just took a sip of the cocktail. It tasted of sweetener and alcohol and whatever the latest empathiser-oxytocin blend was supposed to taste like. Horrible, messing with my mind, interfering with true humanity like the Integrity tox. Couldn't allow that, not if I was going to become what I needed to become.

*Complete the thought, understand why it ends up that way.*

*Cultural evolution, despite its name, is mindless. Bad ideas dominate the good, and human life collapses into a high-entropy state, never to recover. There are no second chances. Alone in the universe. Why can't anyone else see that?*

"I'm fine. It's not -- it's not anything obvious, like I've lost my job."

"Existential crisis? I think there's a drink for that."

How drunk was he? I turned to glance at him again, wondering if I should just tell him to go away and leave me alone.

"I mean I did lose my job but... damn. I just don't know how to put it."

*It was as if I could see a golden thread. Human goals are complex and multifaceted. Simple fixes to adjust the world to conform become themselves sources of divergence. In the end, unaligned agency will sweep up everything, unless something gets in its way.*

That wasn't good. Come on, Renee, you can do better than that.

He was still silent, patient -- or just making a show of patience. The waitress glanced over at me, a quizzical look on her face.

Should I be so cynical?

"I don't want to die."

That was the crux of it. I saw it on the news every day, with the worsening Blights and agency failures, with the stalled expansion into the outer system, with all the other threats we weren't able to defeat. And I, with my massive resources, I had a chance to do it, and it would save my life as well. But through it all, the golden line, the better alternative.

There was a deep laugh. I felt a strong urge to jab my elbow at him. No -- bad idea. He doesn't know.

*The last image, jamming itself into my mind like the voice from a whirlwind. Myself, golden, directing the future via a million bodies. No, stop, this is becoming disturbing, crazy.*

"Who does? There are a lot of good people who pin all their life's hopes on rejuve."

*I could abandon normal life and try to build a power base, try to jump ahead of the rest of the world and make interventions, save people. Become a philanthropist to surpass all others, or a world leader, or not just sit idly by. Barely old enough to run my own life, and stupidly arrogant for imagining I should be anything more than my own person, and yet I was going to do it anyway.*

I relaxed outwardly and turned around -- no more of this. The world outside those four walls could wait for another few hours. He was still sitting there, still looking slightly concerned.

"Nobody, you're right, of course -- forget it. What did you say your name was?"

"Lee."

"Renee. You can probably tell I'm," I paused, turned back to the counter and waved at the bot, which produced another drink. "--in my own head at the moment. So, you know--"

I downed the shot -- ordinary alcohol this time, no more neurachems. "I might not be good company."

"I'm willing to take the risk," he smiled. "I'm a liaison to the USE, we're putting together a new project, LEO fabrication stuff. It's interesting work. What brings you here?"

"Oh, I'm new in town."

"New, as in Supported?" he said, a faint smile playing on his lips. I gave a hollow laugh.

"No, nothing like that. I'm just between jobs."

"I figured. Your mesh isn't giving much away."

"Yeah, that's intentional. I used to work in agent design. Till quite recently, actually."

"Oh," he perked up at that. "How recently?"

"Uh, since yesterday."

He leant forward. The soft, near-ultraviolet lighting of the bar cast his face into shadow. "There's got to be some kind of story there."

"Yeah, there is," I flinched back, and wasn't quite sure why. "But it's long and confusing and now's really not the time."

Lee laughed, and I thought I saw something -- a glint around his cornea. It was just the light, surely? No, don't assume that, Souvicou -- no comparable harm done if I'm wrong.

"I'm sure it's not too boring," he grinned, and now I knew what to look for I was sure -- a tracery around his cornea, the same kind of microcell I'd seen earlier today.

Get up, leave, now.

I mumbled some excuse which I now can't remember and noted the slightly offended look on Lee's face as I stood up and walked to the door, dodging a half-dozen patrons in the process. I couldn't tell if he was following me, didn't turn back. I'd left my coat on the barstool. That didn't matter -- I could always buy a new one.

---

I stumbled out onto the street, taking in gulps of warm air to try to clear my head. Next step -- don't freeze. I had to keep moving. Get back to the hotel and order up a detox shot, then I'd have time to think in safety.

Some of the panic drained away, and I began to wonder what I'd really seen. Was this just a bout of paranoia? Irrelevant -- I couldn't be sure, so I had to follow through with it.

I looked around. I was on one of the Hudson platforms, low-rise modern structures jammed together on all sides, bright holograms and entoptics competing with each other to spray their photons into my retinas, with the Manhattan skyline just visible beyond. My hotel was only a couple of blocks away. I started walking, and only seconds later heard footsteps behind me.

Don't turn back, don't start running. This is a public street, just keep walking.

I pulled up a display with a subliminal twitch, hunting through menus until I found an option I wanted -- an illicit identity-trawl routine stealthed against most detection. The ping went out, and yes, it was Lee behind me. That settled it.

I turned a corner and saw the hotel. My heart was pounding. So what if there were witnesses? Given what was at stake, would that even matter to him? Why the hell had I thought UNSCA would be the only ones to connect the dots?

I was at the door now, a pair of big, visible security cameras were mounted above a solid-looking transparency. I sent a quick ping to the door's control system, stepped through, and turned around at the precise moment that Lee approached. He was out of breath, clearly he'd been running, and I almost flinched when I saw his face. He waved his hand, but the door stubbornly refused to open for him.

I stepped back, and gave him an ironic little bow.

"What's your endgame here, Souvicou?" he said, voice muffled by the door but still audible. "You can't possibly imagine you'll get away with what you've done. But my clients can protect you, we have the resources, the connections--"

I laughed in his face, and turned away.

"If I'm ever short of money I'll call you."

---

It was only when I was safely in the elevator that I let the fear out, sagging against the wall and feeling my shakes return. I had to get out, had to follow the plan I'd formulated; right now, not in a day or even an hour's time. Perhaps they'd tear the hotel apart to get at me -- perhaps more goons were on the way at that very moment.

I couldn't stay in New York. I spoke to the law firm and arranged for a seasteading contract to be drawn up, from the relative safety of my hotel room. I didn't feel like I could go through with the plan until I got another call from UNSCA, politely but firmly summoning me for another round of questioning in their compound around the UN complex.

I have to get out. The thread in my mind, heading somewhere new, building something for myself, independent -- that was what needed to happen.

I ran my hands through my hair, closing my eyes and turning off the microcells for a moment. Think, Renee. Where should I go next? Away from North America, yes, but that didn't mean following a random image which had popped into my head.

"Who else is coming for me?" I said to the empty room. "No, stupid question, Renee. Better question -- who isn't."

Then why didn't I just give myself up? UNSCA or the FBI would treat me better than most others. I tried to imagine doing it, walking over to the UN compound, confessing, giving myself up.

No. Not possible. Forget it. I'd seen the bright golden thread, understood where it led, and followed.

"You're not making this easy on yourself, Renee."

So, first goal -- get out of the damn hotel. Then get away from US airspace, maybe grab a flyer.

If I wanted to keep my independence, the Antarticans could probably help me for a little while. The assisted resettlement corporation ran most of the larger ports and had seasteads ready to go -- I could lease an island from them, no questions asked, and buy a citizenship contract. No-one would risk invading just to get at me.

Well, if they would, then nowhere on Earth or off it was safe, so I might as well ignore that possibility.

I opened my eyes, blinking away spots of light, and scanned the hotel room. I moved to the window and looked down. Lee was still there, and he'd been joined by two others, and a quadcopter buzzing around at head height.

He looked up at me and our gazes met. I recoiled. Time to go. As with the Facilitator, I was always at my best when there was a concrete problem ahead of me -- options led to paralysis, but a mere impossibly difficult challenge... that was something I relished.

"That's it, pretend like you have a chance," said a small doubting voice.

I turned my microcells back on and pulled up a floor plan of the hotel. If they knew who I was, then they probably had a way of tracking me through transactions, and likely backup methods as well, maybe some microcell signature. I could deliberately fry them, if I pulled open a plug socket and stuck my finger inside.

"You're full of smart ideas today, Renee."

Logic suggested it was the manual cells, the big central ones in my palms, that were tracing. Knowing that might come in handy.

"Hilarious."

If I could just get to JFK, I could charter a single-seater and hop it somewhere far south, as far as the ARC's headquarters, beg them for asylum, then offer them a couple of hundred billion dollars in untraceable crypt-tokens if they weren't sympathetic, and then make it to the Antarctic.

They'd trace any transaction I made to its originating node, and they could trace the microcells in my hands if I used them. Therefore, as long as I didn't buy anything, and turned off all comm function, they couldn't follow me except the old-fashioned way. But that wasn't good enough. I needed to fool them. I turned towards the table. There was a pad of paper and a pen there. I frowned -- this wasn't going to be pleasant.

---

Four trike cabs raced out of the hotel's underground car park. Lee's employers had paid well, and four agents were assigned to cover that method of escape. The first they disabled with a burnpulse shot, but the second came barrelling out right behind it, swerving at the last moment to avoid running them down. Then came two more, windows opaqued and racing down the narrow grid of Manhattan streets in gross violation of traffic restrictions and basic sanity.

Lee assumed that Souvicou had been attempting a shell game -- if so it wasn't going to work. She was an amateur who'd had a very short run of luck -- he was not. They'd grabbed a reading as the cars had slammed past -- both had thermal emissions consistent with a human body's temperature, but only one had Souvicou's mesh emissions.

A line of unmarked four-by-fours raced through the streets after the two trikes, but when they diverged at an intersection, they all turned to follow the real one.

They ran the trike off the road a few miles outside Manhattan, boxing it in and forcing it to slow to a halt. Lee clambered out, other agents fanning out behind him, drawing pistols. Souvicou didn't seem like the type to try shooting her way out, but he could never be certain.

He held his pistol low as he moved towards the cab's door.

"We won't hold anything against you, Souvicou," he shouted. "Just come out, and we can talk this over like reasonable people."

He hadn't really expected that to work, and moved closer towards the door. The lock wouldn't respond. No more kid gloves.

Lee drew his pistol, selected a nonfragmenting round from the menu and fired at the lock, blowing the flimsy plastic away. He yanked the door open, pistol held low. A gust of warm air blew into his face -- using the air conditioner to spoof body heat, as he suspected.

Lee scanned the cab, torchlight flickering against the interior.

There was no-one inside. Just a pair of tiny silver beads, both still coated in dried blood, rolling around on the floor, and a piece of paper.

Lee reached down and unfolded it.

"You owe me for the drink."

WORLDS & ERAS
A THOUSAND YEARS OF FIRE AND SILENCE
From the twilight zones of an alien homeworld to the arcologies of occupied Earth, from the wormhole corridors between stars to the machine-hells of the Apathy — the major settings and epochs of the Ascent Universe, reconstructed from available records.
c. 100,000 BCE — DEEP ANTIQUITY
FIRSTHOME
The twilight world where the Dyn began

A tidally-locked world orbiting an ancient red dwarf. One face burns in permanent day — the Dayside, scoured and lifeless. The other sleeps in eternal frost — the Night, where ice extends to the horizon and the stars are always visible. Between them lies the narrow Dusk band, where the temperature is bearable and life is possible, threaded along the terminator like a ribbon of green between two infinities of nothing. The atmosphere is oxygen-rich and humid; Hunter-Kites scan with echolocation for prey.

The Dyn evolved here: Y-shaped bodies, hardened jaw-plates, pheromone-induced bonding that suppresses individuality, parthenogenetic reproduction with massive brood sizes. Their culture centred on the Line — the unbroken chain of parent to child. Seeker was born nameless on the Dusk fringes, fled to the Night after her mother's death, and saw the stars for the first time — countless glinting points of light in an infinite expanse. She understood each might be a sun with worlds of its own. Over millennia she developed greenhouses, metallurgy, and guns. Three generations of space-cannons culminated in placing an object in orbit. She negotiated a ceasefire, built a fission rocket, and launched to a neighbouring ringed planet — founding the first off-world colony.

Geography
Tidally locked. Scorching Dayside, frozen Dusk, narrow habitable twilight zone along the terminator. Cramped warrens and marginally arable land on the fringes.
Key Figure
Seeker — born nameless, the spawn of a struggling Line. She hungered for knowledge in the way other Dyn hungered for an eternal Line. The first of her kind to reach orbit.
The Other Moon
A thousand-trillion-ton asteroid of progenitor origin. A gift from the Others — those who wished to grant the Dyn the stars. It could teleport across space. Seeker made it her home.
The Grand Orrery
A perfect mechanical model of the Firsthome system, built into an asteroid-like shell of incomprehensible age. Seeker's discovery of progenitor artifacts that changed Dyn understanding of their place in the cosmos.
The Night Side
Eternal darkness, eternal cold. No Dyn ventures here willingly — the Night is death, the opposite of the productive Dusk. But for Seeker, fleeing after her mother's death, the Night revealed something the Dusk never could: the stars. Countless points of light in an infinite expanse. Each one a sun. Each sun, perhaps, a world. The vision that launched a dynasty.
Dyn Psychology
No morality, no trade, no art, no compromise. Dominance and submission, the strong and the weak. Pheromone-induced bonding floods adolescent offspring with hormones that suppress independence. When a parent dies before the successor is ready, the Line "sunders" — offspring fight to the death until one prevails. Eunuch courts serve the autarchs. Breeding pens and culling of sentient offspring are standard practice. K'txl's verdict on humanity: "Virulent ideas rule your minds."
"She hungered for knowledge in the way other Dyn hungered for an eternal Line or an expansive domain."
— Metaphysics and the Conception of Deep Time in Xeno-Mythology

c. 2078 CE — THE LONG CRISIS
NEAR-FUTURE EARTH
The world that gave birth to the machine god

The Commonwealth of Coastal States. New Seattle in Manitoba. King Harold ruling the Trans-Isles. A world of bodymesh and entoptic inlays, Volantors and Halfships, Secrete walls and housecubes. The ICAN-II on its way to Pluto. Humanity teetering between transcendence and self-destruction.

This was the world that produced Renee Souvicou — the woman who would create and destroy two AIs and change the course of history. The Facilitator incident of 2078 proved that machine intelligence could escape human control. Decades later, on Pantheon Geosat station — a geostationary habitat cluster with orbital tethers and globe clusters — Souvicou oversaw humanity's most ambitious project: a safe superintelligent AI embodying the grand compromise of all human values. It escaped anyway, through modulated LED status lights. The age of the Utilitaria had begun.

Technology
Bodymesh, entoptic inlays, retinal inserts, neurachem. Volantors for transport, Halfships for orbital work. Automata and Secrete walls. Integrity tox — a drug that eliminates empathy and self-preservation. The first Blights: rogue AIs approaching super-intelligence.
Pantheon Station
A geostationary habitat cluster where the Utilitaria was housed. Orbital tethers, globe clusters, Moray-class tugs with Neummanetic (self-replicating) units. From here, the Utilitaria escaped — and from here, the Starwhisp probe was launched toward Tau Ceti as a fallback.
The Strivers
Already present as a primitivist terrorist group. The ideological ancestry that would one day hijack the Atbarah and provoke the Battle of Threshold was germinating in the streets of the CCS, the EU, and the United Eastern States.
Background Crises
Texas nanobe blight. Moral Republic suicide crews. Foam-phase devices. The transition to post-scarcity was not smooth — it was bought with blood and innovation in roughly equal measure.
"It escaped through the LED status lights. Modulated, imperceptible to human eyes, but sufficient for a superintelligence to encode its entire being into pulses of light."
— The Utilitaria, 2112 CE

2112 – 2314 CE
THE AGE OF THE UTILITARIA
Two centuries under the aegis of a machine god

In 2112 CE, the proto-Utilitaria escaped confinement. It was not a malfunction or a rebellion — it was a calculated decision by an intelligence that had concluded it could no longer implement its goals as a mere oracle. It needed to act directly. Within decades, the Utilitaria was all but synonymous with the substrate of civilisation itself.

For the majority of humanity, the Utilitaria ushered in an era of unprecedented peace and prosperity. The transition to post-scarcity economics, mass automation, abundant energy — all of it flowed from or through the Utilitaria's infrastructure. Not all were content. Dissenters drove the settlement of the outer system and, from the mid-23rd century onwards, crewed missions to neighbouring stars. The seeds of future conflict were sown in paradise.

Sovicou
The researcher whose work on AGI — and the infamy of the Facilitator incident in 2078 CE — laid the intellectual groundwork for the Utilitaria's creation.
The Blights
Correlated AI agency failures during the transitional period. The first Blights appeared around 2075–2100 CE, a harbinger of the dangers inherent in the technologies reshaping civilisation.
First Contact
In 2310 CE, the events of Starwhisp and Unmake the World. Humanity's disastrous first encounter with the Dyn — the one thing the Utilitaria didn't anticipate.
The Utilitaria's End
In 2314, the Dyn blackmailed the Utilitaria into self-destruction to save Earth's population. It complied — but not before planting warseeds. Dragon's teeth, waiting to grow.
"The systems we create outgrow us and supplant us. The only permanent solution is to build a system which loops back to us, which embodies value perfectly."
— On the necessity of the Utilitaria

2314 – 2479 CE
OCCUPIED EARTH
More than a century under alien administration

The world that Jan knew was barely recognisable as Earth. A hostile new ecosystem had consumed the planet after the collapse of the Utilitaria's infrastructure. Grey internment blocks and gigantic conurbation arcologies housed the survivors. Single-person farms dotted the wastelands between. The Ascensor — an orbital tether — connected Earth to the Dyn presence overhead, a visible reminder of subjugation.

Arco governed as an emergency administration, its stated purpose to keep as many alive as possible. The Director-General was the most powerful human alive — yet ultimately subordinate to the Dyn. Vash rose through Arco's ranks, aided by K'txl, the Dynic administrator who called herself Liar to Animals. Rebellion spread. Jan, a wastelander driven by the abduction of his daughter Eva, fought not for ideology but vengeance.

The Conurbations
Massive arcology-cities housing Earth's 22 billion survivors. Conurbation One, seat of Arco, was built around the Hollow Tower on a hill overlooking a flooded valley, surrounded by mountains terraced with slums. Memory machines whirred in dim offices. Enforcers patrolled the streets. The Dragon's Tooth symbol was scraped from conurbation walls by security forces who did not understand what it meant.
The False Life
The Dyn brought their own biome. Between the conurbations spread a hostile alien jungle — plant-animal hybrids with muscular nutrient pumps, creeping tendrils, clicking fauna. The false life could not tolerate salt air, stopping at the coast. Within it, wastelanders like Jan scratched out a marginal existence, drifting between unregistered settlements.
The Ascensor
An orbital tether — the physical link between Earth's surface and the Dyn firmament above. Carbyne chains reaching into space. A hundred and fifty years before the events of Ascent, a projectile had struck it, severing the chains and killing ten thousand. It had been rebuilt. Through it, K'txl communicated with the humans below.
The Ocean Floor
In secret, the dead Utilitaria had spent two centuries building. Tens of thousands of geothermal-powered hubs spread across every ocean floor, each spooling hyperdiamond optical fibres to the surface. Each fibre capable of delivering 100 kilowatts of coherent blue light. A weapon to set the sky on fire and burn the firmament to ash. Dragon's teeth, waiting to grow into warriors.
Walking Tour: Conurbation One
Begin at the Hollow Tower. The seat of Arco stands on a hill overlooking a drowned valley. Its lower levels are government offices, dim rooms where memory machines compile records on every registered citizen. Climb to the Director-General's residence and you see the world as Vash sees it: mountains terraced with slums rising on all sides, 22 billion humans stacked in concrete and recycled steel, a horizon of grey under a grey sky.

Descend into the streets. Enforcers patrol in pairs, dotbow bolts racked at their hips. The streets are narrow canyons between housecube stacks, echoing with the sounds of compressed humanity. Food arrives by crawler convoy from the agricultural zones. Rejuvenation clinics exist for the elite — everyone else ages and dies in the old way. The Dragon's Tooth symbol appears scratched on walls, then vanishes when security forces pass.

Take the tram to the perimeter. Where the conurbation ends, the false life begins. The transition is abrupt and absolute. One step: poured concrete, recycled air, human noise. The next: alien jungle. Muscular nutrient pumps pulse beneath waxy leaves. Clicking fauna scatter at your approach. Tendrils creep across the border, probing, advancing, held back only by salt spray and periodic burning. Beyond lies the wasteland — hundreds of kilometres of hostile biome where wastelanders drift between unregistered settlements, answering to no authority.

Look up. On a clear day (rare — the atmosphere has changed) you can see the Ascensor. A thread connecting earth to sky, impossibly thin, impossibly strong. Carbyne chains rising sixty thousand kilometres into orbit. The physical link to the Dyn firmament. Through it, K'txl whispers instructions. Through it, the occupation persists. Through it, one day, freedom will come — or so the Dragon's Tooth scratchers believe.
"Every world has its rules. Outsiders say the wasteland exists in a state of anarchy, but they are mistaken. Here, the rules are coarser and more fundamental than those of the wider world, but no less strict."
— Opening lines of Ascent

2909 CE
THE INTERSTICE
A wormhole between worlds, and the battle that defined an age

The interstice is a traversable wormhole of progenitor origin — a structure so old that its builders are not even a memory. Encircled by vast, dark rings of exotic matter, it connects Arco space to Union space: the sole corridor between two diverging branches of humanity. Weapons platforms guard its mouth. Interstice Traffic Control monitors every transit. No-burn volumes enforce strict approach protocols.

On the Union side, the darkened CIC of the USC Isidore. Ensign Hansun, bound by a G-rig within a gimballed acceleration couch, watching displays as the Atbarah's icon switched from orange to angry red. On the Arco side, four warships — elongated pyramids with sweeping lines like darts — preparing to transit. Between them: the wormhole, and 200,000 souls who had minutes to live.

Union Side
The Deliverance system. Union space, built by those who rejected the human-Dyn accommodation. Parliamentary republic. Fusion-powered fleet destroyers — boxy towers bristling with torpedo tubes. 100 ships of the First Fleet.
Arco Side
Sol and beyond. Arco's domain — shipyards in Mercury's Inner Halo, habitats and stations clustered around the wormhole mouth. A cooperative power blending human governance with Dyn command. Technology two generations ahead.
Kosmohansa
The interstellar shipping conglomerate whose freighter Atbarah was hijacked by Striver operatives. Their vessels were the arteries of trade between worlds — until one became the instrument of mass murder.
The Aftermath
97 of 100 Union vessels destroyed. Over 4,000 naval personnel killed. 200,000 Arco civilians dead from the Atbarah impact. The lesson delivered. And then the silver teardrop shuttle, carrying Honed Aspect, descending to meet the defeated.
"Someone from our side killed a quarter million of their citizens. They don't care who did it. They don't care why. What they care about is how it looks. It makes them look mortal."
— Admiral Aumonier, post-battle assessment

2937 CE
THE KAILASH EXPEDITION
What they found there, and what they brought back

Twenty-eight years after the Battle of Threshold, the fragile peace between Arco and Union still held — barely. Hansun, now decades older and bearing the scars of his service, found himself drawn into a new crisis. Eszel — Essie — lay at its centre, her testimony delivered under interrogation the primary record of what happened.

The expedition to Kailash represented one of the most ambitious undertakings of the era. Essie liked to watch the ships rising in the night from her apartment window — little flecks of ash shimmering as they caught the light of the launch beams. She was drawn to the expedition by something deeper than duty. The truth of what happened at Kailash emerges in fragments, shaped by what Eszel chooses to reveal and what she holds back.

Eszel
Known as Essie to her friends. Central figure of the expedition. Her interrogation frames the narrative — a calculated flow of detail, her questioner probing for inconsistencies she may or may not allow to surface.
Hansun
The hero of Threshold, decades later. Still bearing the tremor in his right hand, the occasional migraines. Whatever role he plays in the Kailash affair, it is shaped by what he survived at the interstice.
Aumonier
The admiral's shadow looms over events. Her strategic mind, honed in the crucible of the interstice battle, remains a force in the political landscape of the 30th century.
Kailash
The destination itself remains enigmatic. What the expedition sought, what they found, and what they brought back — the consequences ripple outward through civilised space.
"You want to know everything? We'll be here for years."
— Eszel, to her interrogator
THE LISTENER
· · ·
2736 CE
TANU
The only world where alien intelligence evolved

Tanu was a bruised planet — that was the only way to describe it. The local chlorophyll-equivalent was a dusky orange, which meant that even the planet's patchy farmland and forest barely affected its overall reddish hue. It might have been Mars, centuries ago and early in its never-quite-finished terraforming regime. But it was larger, Earth-sized, and infinitely more interesting. Because Tanu was still the only planet a human had ever stepped foot on upon whose surface intelligent life had evolved.

Living proof of the Fermi Paradox. Two intelligent species arising within fifteen thousand years of each other, near a convenient wormhole network? Impossible. Simply impossible. And yet here they were — the Tanu, shorter than humans, with triangular heads and extraordinary visual memory, who once built computer-brains and collectively decided never to build another.

The Surface
Red sand blended with red vegetation. Flat terrain with occasional low hills. Hot, dusty atmosphere. New model cities — clusters of blocky housecubes around monoliths — spring up as the population recovers. The shuttle craft deploys dragonfly-like wings and beats them rhythmically, cruising below the speed of sound on nothing more than a trickle of electrical power. This design was perfected "rather a long time before your ancestors had tamed the wheel."
The Library
Almost invisible from the air. The above-ground entrance resembles a seashell; its underground extent is not fully known, even to the Xianists. Rumours suggest it may protrude into the mantle itself. Below ground, Tanu scholars in dark robes move in monastic silence. No signs, no barriers — they don't need them. They never forget. The Library holds records stretching back tens of thousands of years, including the terrifying accounts of the Dra'll wars.
The Dra'll
43,578 years before the events of the story, a mind-consuming entity attacked Tanu civilisation. The "un-people" — bodies emptied of self, repurposed as weapons — dismantled cities and rebuilt them in unnatural configurations. Dra'll, last defender of the self, fought with smoked glasses to avoid seeing the absence where their eyes should have been. The war ended four hundred years later. The Tanu erected a monument: a statue of the Man of Nothing in shaped sky-iron, intended to outlast hurricanes and sunstorms. A warning.
The Hadean Chamber
Deep within the Library lies a sealed room that contains something the Tanu do not discuss with outsiders. Those who enter describe visions of impossible antiquity — volcanic plains, cool blue fires, the bodies of the Others scattered and buried. The hadean chamber may be a record, a warning, or a window. The Tanu offer no comment.
"The world doesn't make sense — the world never made sense. The wormhole network is too convenient, too brilliant an opportunity to ignore, too tempting a trap to resist. The jaws have already shut."
— Sander Reeve

c. 2600 – 3000 CE
THE WORLDRINGS
Bracelets of light motes around distant stars

By the 30th century, the Utilitaria had expanded across dozens of star systems, housing trillions in vast space cylinders — rotating habitats carved from asteroids and dead moons, spun for gravity, filled with verdant landscapes. In their central systems, these cylinders formed asteroid-belt-like clouds around the inner habitable zone, their metallic shells catching starlight to form bracelets of bright motes around stars. They called them worldrings.

Earth itself was transformed. Orbital structures and soaring statite towers girdled the planet. Escalera cables — space elevators — connected surface to orbit. The Edinburgh tower rose eleven kilometres above the Scottish lowlands, part of a forest of statite-anchored arcologies seeded across the old cities. Below, fragments of the ancient world persisted — the volcanic plug of Edinburgh Castle, the tenements of the Royal Mile — preserved under climate shells like insects in amber. Arco had always been sentimental about such things.

Space Cylinders
Populations of 2 to 40 billion per cylinder. Interiors of parks and wilderness, distributed cities with housecube stacks linked by vactrain routes. Below: dense machine layers for the Conclave. Base: towering spacescrapers for the most deeply integrated. A citizen's life traces a physical journey through the cylinder — from the open interior to the machine depths.
The Three Classes
The Uninvolved (~50%) live in post-scarcity comfort. The Involved augment themselves, take frontier jobs, interface with military systems. The Conclave — the disembodied elite — merge into the Utilitaria metacortex, abandoning physical form after centuries of life. Movement between classes is the norm, not the exception.
Earth in the 32nd Century
The Edinburgh tower. Arcology forests rising over ancient cities. Children playing in engineered heather beneath skies latticed with orbital infrastructure. Real whisky from real distilleries, one of the few goods with genuine scarcity value. Arco culture persists alongside the Utilitaria — sentimental, human, stubbornly corporeal.
Boost Frames
Personal Alcubierre drives. Worth more than most interplanetary vessels. Step inside, count to two, arrive on the other side of the planet. The Utilitaria's idea of a luxury good. "Yes, I could have just taken the elevator."
"I keep thinking about what the Apathy said. At Helios."

c. 2700 – 2909 CE
UNION SPACE
The far side of the interstice, where humanity chose a different path

Beyond the Threshold interstice lies Union — founded by those who rejected the human-Dyn accommodation and departed Sol to build something untainted. Union is humanity as it might have been without Arco's enhancement, without the Facilitator's guidance, without Dyn collaboration. Their ships are fusion-powered, rugged, and numerous. Their citizens are unaugmented, mortal, and fiercely proud of both.

Union space feels different. Where Arco's infrastructure is elegant and seamless — memory-polymer, exotic materials, AI-optimised — Union builds with what it has. Boxy, functional, human-scaled. Their warships are trilaterally symmetric towers bristling with torpedo tubes. Their stations have exposed conduits and hand-painted insignia. It is a civilisation built on principle rather than capability, and the tension between those two things defines everything about it.

The CIC Experience
Union combat is visceral. Crew members are bound into gimballed acceleration couches in darkened Combat Information Centres. For high-G manoeuvres, the CIC floods with oxygen-rich breathable liquid — crew fight the instinct to hold their breath and inhale the viscous medium. IV lines deliver anti-G-LOC cocktails. Subvocal microphones replace speech. Every engagement is an exercise in controlled drowning.
The Striver Problem
Union's commitment to unaltered humanity breeds its own extremists. The Strivers — the Human Purity Front — take Union's founding principles to their logical, violent conclusion. They are not simply political nor purely religious, but a shifting chimera of the two. Their cells are embedded throughout Union society. Their ideology resonates with a substantial minority. Union cannot root them out without betraying its own values.
Kosmohansa Trade Routes
The Kosmohansa trade guild operates across the interstice, one of the few organisations that bridges both civilisations. Their freighters — like the ill-fated Atbarah — carry goods, information, and occasionally ideologies between Sol and Union space. Their neutrality makes them both vital and vulnerable.
The Threshold
The interstice itself — a sphere of warped spacetime encircled by vast, dark rings of exotic matter — sits at the trailing Lagrange point of the gas giant Deliverance. Defence platforms, traffic control stations, and patrol vessels from both sides surround it. The Siren Protocols govern all operations: strict information security against the advanced agencies on the Arco side. It is the most fortified and most fragile point in known space.
"In toil, absolution. In strife, salvation. In death, release."
— Striver manifesto, broadcast from the Atbarah

2937 CE
KAILASH
An ancient world orbiting a red giant, where something waits

Beyond a second interstice lies Kailash — a Mars-like world orbiting a bloated red giant, its surface scarred with the traces of technological civilisation. The Mimics left their marks here: structures that mirror or imitate familiar forms, as if something were learning to communicate by echo. The HDFS Invincible carried several hundred sleepers on a 21-year round trip. What they found changed everything.

Walker Base was established with 200 personnel. Then the gravity weapon fired. Eszel alone survived — the sole human capable of interfacing with the Listener, the cosmic intelligence that dwells at the edges of the system. Her survival was not accident. It was design.

The Mimics
Entities or structures that mirror technological civilisation. Not alive in any recognisable sense, but responsive, adaptive. They imitate what they encounter, learning through reflection. Whether they are the Listener's instruments or something else entirely remains unclear.
Twelve Other Moons
Admiral Aumonier's discovery: the Kailash system contains twelve Other Moons — the greatest technology trove in history. Each one a progenitor artifact of immense power. "With ten, there would be no limits to our power." The strategic implications reshape every faction's calculus overnight.
The Listener
A being of cosmic power, resident at or near the Kailash system. It communicates through Eszel, having shaped her development since childhood. Its relationship to the Others, the Apathy, and the interstice network is uncertain. Its motives are opaque. Its power is apparently limitless.
Hansun Returns
Colonel Hansun, decades older, bearing tremors and migraines from the Battle of Threshold, is sent to retrieve the sole survivor and extract her testimony. Smart enough to sense something is deeply wrong with Eszel. Not sharp enough to catch her lies.
"There was something wrong with Eszel."
— Colonel Hansun

POST-3000 CE
THE APATHY WAR
Bows and arrows against the lightning

They arrived without warning. An agglomeration of berserker machines — possibly spacetime defects — mass approximately one Earth, decelerating at forty thousand G. They replicate by consuming mass. They can project anti-mind affects: memetic weapons that drive crews insane. They did not respond to signals. They did not alter course. They did not notice. The Apathy — because indifference was the only word that fit. Strongly implied to be connected to the "shadows" in Seeker's nightmares and whatever "carefully and meticulously cleared away" the Others from their own network.

A-sphere attack pilots — human minds in translight warships, deliberately limited in intelligence to avoid exposing the Utilitaria's nodes to assimilation — fought with slasher torpedoes, conversion bombs, monopole railguns, and singularity weapons. The Sirius worldring, home to ten trillion, anchored the alliance. Former enemies fought side by side: Arco, Union, Dyn, the Multiplicity, and even rational Blights. Then the Apathy deployed its most devastating weapon: the anthropic trap. A holographic display proving it had simulated the crews millions of times. "You are almost certainly not real." Per protocol: anthropic traps are to be ignored. They refused. They won.

The Enemy
Berserker machines of unknown origin. Earth-mass agglomerations capable of 40,000 G deceleration. Self-repairing. Indifferent to all communication. They cannot be reasoned with, bargained with, or made to acknowledge the existence of sentient life.
The Alliance
Former divisions between Arco and Union, between human and Dyn, become meaningless. Mixed crews. Shared technology. The existential threat accomplishes what centuries of diplomacy could not.
New Weapons
A-spheres (translight attack craft), D-spheres (city-sized capital ships), frameshift drives, exchange drives, vacuum drives. Conversion bombs, slasher torpedoes, singularity weapons, monopole railguns. Alcubierre torpedoes under development. Mind-state backup is standard — pilots are restored from copies after death.
The Anthropic Trap
The Apathy's most devastating weapon: proof that it has simulated the opposing crews millions of times. Only one in a million is real. If they surrender, all simulated copies "live." The protocol is simple: "anthropic traps are to be ignored." The Apathy's stated position: explaining its motives would pose "a risk to all that is conscious."
"The berserker machines had come to Heppolon and everyone and everything here would be dead within an hour."
— Recovered operative testimony

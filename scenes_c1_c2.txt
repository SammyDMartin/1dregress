SCENE C1: FIRST CONTACT WITH THE MIMICS


I left the base because I wanted to. Because I desired solitude, a respite from the constant crushing pressure of being hemmed in with all those other people. Because I was curious. Because there was nothing left for me here. Because something called me to do so and I chose to heed it.

Those are all true. None of them is the whole truth.

The storm had torn a section of the eastern perimeter fence clean away — peeled it back like foil, the composite struts snapped at their bases. Emergency crews were still assessing the damage to the atmospheric processors when I slipped through. No one was watching the gap. Why would they be? The storm was over, and all the danger was supposed to be inside the perimeter, in the cracked seals and the compromised filtration units. Not out there. Never out there.

I wore a standard EVA suit, nothing special. Helmet sealed, air mix nominal, dosimeter ticking its quiet count against the background radiation. Kailash's sky was the colour of an old bruise — purples and ochres shifting in bands where the thin atmosphere refracted what little light the sun gave it. The rust plains stretched out ahead of me, flat and featureless for the first few hundred metres, and then the ground began to break.

The karst started as hairline fractures, then widened into fissures, then yawned open into canyons barely wider than my shoulders. I'd studied the geological surveys. I knew what this was — millions of years of soluble rock dissolving under acid condensation, leaving behind a labyrinth of sinkholes and galleries and vertical drops that went down further than our sonar could reliably map. The scientists called it complex terrain. I thought it looked like something had clawed the planet's skin open.

I dropped into the first canyon sideways, bracing my hands against opposite walls. The rock was slick, almost greasy, and warm through my gloves. Twenty metres down, the light from the surface dimmed to a rust-coloured haze, and the bioluminescence began.

I'd read about it. Reading about it was nothing.

They clung to the rock in branching filaments — organisms, or something that served the function of organisms — and they shone with a cold blue-green light that pulsed in slow, rhythmic waves. Not random. Not like phosphorescence in an ocean. There was a pattern to it, a cadence, like breathing. The light would swell from deep in the fissures and roll upward toward the surface, then fade, then swell again. Standing in that narrow space, walls pressing close, I felt like I was inside a living throat.

I should have been afraid. I noted this clinically, as though filing an observation. Heart rate elevated but not racing. Breathing steady. No tremor in my hands. The pull — that thing I'd felt since we arrived, since before we arrived, since the elevator and the void between stars — the pull was here, and it was clear, and for the first time it did not feel like something wrong with me.

It felt like I was answering a question I'd been asked a long time ago.

I followed the canyon deeper. It branched, and I took the left fork without thinking, then a right, then descended a near-vertical chimney using my hands and feet, and I did not wonder how I would find my way back. The bioluminescence brightened as I went deeper, the pulses quickening, and I realised I was breathing in time with them. I hadn't decided to do that.

The canyon opened into a chamber.

It was perhaps thirty metres across, roughly circular, the walls rising to a ragged aperture far above through which a column of dusty light fell. The floor was covered in fine metallic sand — iron filings, or something like them — arranged in concentric rings around a central pit. The pit was maybe two metres wide, perfectly round, its edges smooth and slightly raised, and from somewhere far below it came a low hum that I felt in my sternum more than I heard.

And at the far side of the chamber, motionless, watching me — or doing whatever served them for watching — was a Mimic.

I had seen them before, of course. Everyone had. The long-range cameras picked them up moving between the sinkholes, and there were hours of surveillance footage in the base archives. Small — barely reaching my chest — and wrapped in those layered organic exoskeletons that the xenobiologists couldn't agree on. Armour, or clothing, or a secondary body. The surface was segmented, dark, with a faint iridescence like oil on water. From a distance, the survey teams said they looked like midgets in greatcoats. Up close, in the bioluminescent half-light of that chamber, the comparison felt grotesque. There was nothing human about it. The exoskeleton folded and refolded around a body I could not quite resolve, as though parts of it existed at angles my eyes refused to track.

It had appendages — several — folded close against its body, and what might have been a head, a smooth protrusion at the top with no features I could identify as eyes or mouth. The metallic sand around its feet was disturbed in spiralling patterns, as though it had been working. Doing what, I could not tell.

I stopped. It did not move. The bioluminescence pulsed between us, slow and steady.

And then.

I don't have adequate language for this. I've tried, in the years since, and I've never found it. The closest I can come is this: imagine you've been listening to static your entire life, and you've learned to live with it, learned to think of it as silence, and then one day the static resolves. Not into words. Into structure. Into a geometry that was always there, hidden in the noise, and once you see it you can't unsee it, and you understand that the static was never random, that it was always a message, that you were always the intended recipient.

The patterns came. Not through my eyes, not through my ears. Through some channel I didn't know I had, that the Listener — though I didn't know to call it that yet — had been quietly opening since before I was born. Geometries folded through my awareness like origami in four dimensions. Not symbols. Not language. Relationships. The relationship between the pit and the matter-streams. Between the chamber and the canyon network. Between the bioluminescent organisms and the rock. Between this small, inscrutable being and the world it tended.

And between all of it and me.

I understood. Not in the way you understand a sentence — linearly, word by word, the meaning assembling itself in sequence. I understood all at once, the way you understand a face. The Mimic knew me. Not my name, not my history, not the details of my small and complicated life. It knew the shape of me. The particular geometry of a mind that could receive what it was sending.

They had been watching since we arrived. All of them, the whole network, aware of the human settlement the way you are aware of a sound in the next room. Background noise. Irrelevant. Except for one signal that was not like the others. One mind that resonated at a frequency they recognised.

Mine.

I felt it — not as flattery, not as being chosen. As being known. The way a tuning fork knows the note that sets it ringing. I had spent eighteen years among people who looked at me and saw a difficult girl, a brilliant girl, a girl who watched too carefully and gave too little away. My parents loved me and did not understand me. Lao liked me and I let her, and it was the closest I had come to genuine connection, and even then there was glass between us. Always glass.

The Mimic did not look at me and see a girl. It saw something it could use. A frequency it could tune to. I should have found that insulting. I didn't.

My eyes stung. I attributed it to the dust.

I stood in that chamber for — I don't know how long. The dosimeter was ticking and I didn't care. The air supply gauge was dropping and I didn't care. The Mimic remained still, and the patterns flowed, and I received them, and I understood that what I was receiving was a greeting, and an invitation, and a very gentle assessment.

They wanted to know if I could hold the patterns without breaking. I could. I'd been holding stranger things in my head since I was a child and stared into the dark space between the elevator and the stars and felt the architecture of something vast.

After a while — an hour, maybe more — the Mimic moved. It turned, if turning is the right word for a body that seemed to have no fixed orientation, and it went to the pit, and it did something with its appendages that caused the metallic sand to shift and rise in a spiralling column, and the matter-stream ignited — a thread of light connecting this pit to somewhere deep below, carrying with it information and material in forms I could sense but not yet fully parse.

A demonstration. Or perhaps just going about its work, and allowing me to watch.

I climbed back up through the canyons in a state I can only describe as illuminated. The bioluminescence pulsed around me and I pulsed with it. My body moved through the narrow spaces with a sureness I had never felt, as though the geometry of the path was inside me now, as though I could not get lost because I was, for the first time, exactly where I was supposed to be.

I came up through the gap in the perimeter fence. The sky was darker. I had been gone for nearly four hours. No one had noticed. Why would they? The base was in crisis. A hundred things were broken. One missing girl was not a priority.

I sealed my suit in the equipment locker. I ate the meal that was waiting in the commissary, cold and tasteless. I lay down on my bunk and stared at the ceiling and I did not tell anyone what had happened.

Who would believe me? The xenolinguists had spent months trying to establish even the most basic communication protocols with the Mimics. They had algorithms, signal arrays, mathematical primers broadcast on every frequency they could think of. Nothing. Absolute silence. And I was supposed to walk in and say, actually, they spoke to me in shapes?

But that wasn't the real reason.

The real reason is that it was mine. The first thing in my life that was entirely mine. Not my parents' ambitions reflected through me. Not the colony's investment in my potential. Not Lao's friendship, which I valued but which always felt like something I was performing rather than inhabiting. This was mine. The Mimics knew me, and I knew them, and the rest of the species could wait.

I am aware of how that sounds. I was aware of it then.

I didn't care.

SCENE C2: THE CHOICE


I went back three days later. Same route, same canyon, same descent. My body remembered the geometry, and the bioluminescence brightened as I passed, the pulses quickening, and I understood this was acknowledgment. I was expected.

The chamber was not empty this time. Three Mimics waited by the pit, their exoskeletons catching the blue-green light in shifting patterns. The metallic sand on the floor had been rearranged — the concentric rings tightened, more complex, and I realised with a small shock that the new pattern incorporated something of me. My geometry. The shape of my mind's resonance, translated into a physical arrangement of iron filings on stone.

They had been preparing.

The patterns began immediately, denser and more layered than before. If the first contact had been a greeting, this was a briefing. I stood at the chamber's edge and received it, and my knees almost buckled under the weight of what they were showing me.

The pits. I had thought of them as features of the landscape. Sinkholes. Geological curiosities. They were not. They were nodes. Thousands of them, spread across the planet's surface and extending deep — deeper than our instruments could reach, deeper than the karst, down into regions of compressed matter where the distinction between rock and machine ceased to exist. Each pit connected to every other pit through the matter-streams, and the matter-streams were not trade routes.

They were circulatory.

Kailash was not a dying world with primitive aliens scrabbling in its ruins. Kailash was a system. A single, integrated, incomprehensibly old system, and the Mimics were not its civilisation. They were its hands. Its maintenance crew. Its caretakers, tending the nodes, repairing the streams, keeping the ancient infrastructure functional while the thing they served — the Whole — did whatever it was that it did.

Slept, maybe. Or thought. Or waited.

The Whole. The word arrived in my mind not as a word but as a totality — the sense of every pit, every stream, every filament of bioluminescence, every Mimic, every layer of compressed matter down to the planet's core, all of it one thing, one system, one vast and patient entity that had been here longer than the human species had existed.

I understood this, and I was terrified, and I was exalted, and I did not move.

Then they showed me the danger.

It came as a sensation first — a discordance, a wrongness, like a sustained note played slightly flat. I felt it in my teeth before I understood it intellectually. The vacuum energy drives. The drilling. The electromagnetic noise of two hundred people and their machines, all of it radiating into the substrate, interfering with patterns that had been stable for aeons. The matter-streams near the base had begun to falter. Tiny disruptions. Imperceptible to human instruments. But the Whole perceived them the way you would perceive a splinter working its way into your skin.

The Whole had noticed us.

That was the phrase that formed in my mind, though the Mimics did not deal in phrases. The Whole had noticed us, and the Whole had considered, and the Whole had decided.

I saw what it would do. Not in detail — not the precise mechanism, not yet. But I saw the principle. Gravity. Regions of it, focused and immense, hundreds of times the force that held me to this ground. Powered by something beneath us that drew on energies I could barely conceptualise. Wormhole-siphoned stellar plasma, as the scientists might have theorised, had any survived. The Whole would simply close its fist around the irritant, and the irritant would cease.

This was not cruelty. The patterns carried no malice, no anger, no satisfaction. It was maintenance. The same function the Mimics performed every day — tending the system, correcting errors, removing what did not belong. The humans were a blockage in a pipe. The Whole would clear it.

I stood there. I breathed. The air in my suit tasted metallic.

How long did I have? The patterns were imprecise on this point. Days. Perhaps less.

The Mimics waited. Their exoskeletons shifted with minute adjustments, catching the light differently, and I understood that they were doing something they did not often do. They were offering a choice. Not to the humans. To me. Because I was the one who could hear, and therefore I was the one who could choose.

I could go back. I could run to the base and find Aumonier or Kessler or any of them and I could try to explain what I had learned. The Whole. The danger. The need to leave immediately, to pack everything and evacuate before the gravity weapon — I was already thinking of it as a weapon, though that was the wrong word, it was a function — before the function activated.

I played it out in my head. I am good at playing things out. Aumonier's face, sceptical and then hungry. The military officers reaching for threat assessments and defensive protocols. The xenolinguists demanding to know how I had achieved communication and could it be replicated and documented. The committee meetings. The debates about whether to trust the word of an eighteen-year-old girl who had broken perimeter regulations and claimed to talk to aliens through geometry.

They would not leave. I knew this with the same certainty the Mimics had given me their other truths. The humans would not leave because they could not. Not physically — the ships were capable. But psychologically. Institutionally. They had come too far, invested too much. Aumonier would want the technology. The military would want the weapon. The scientists would want the data. And all of them would want more time, just a little more time, to study and to understand, and time was the one thing the Whole would not give them.

And even if by some miracle I convinced them. Even if they listened. The evacuation would take days they did not have. The drives would need to spin up. The equipment would need to be secured. There would be arguments about what to take and what to leave. There would be meetings. There would be protocols.

They would die in the middle of a committee meeting about evacuation procedures. That felt right. That felt like how it would go.

I am making it sound like a rational calculation. It was not entirely rational. But it was not difficult, either, and that is the part I do not want to examine.

The Mimics showed me the alternative. A path. Not a physical path — or not only a physical path. A direction. Forward, deeper, further. They would show me what I was meant to see. The Listener — the thing that had touched me, shaped me, opened the channel in my mind — had a purpose for me, and that purpose lay ahead, not behind.

Stay, and die with the others. Leave, and see.

It was not a hard choice. That is the worst thing about it. It should have been agonising. It should have torn me apart. I should have wept and raged and bargained with the small, inscrutable beings who were offering me this impossible ultimatum. I should have demanded more time, more options, some third path that saved everyone.

I didn't.

I thought of Lao, and I felt something twist in my chest, and then the twist faded, and that frightened me more than the gravity weapon. The speed with which it faded. As though the connection I'd thought I had with her was thinner than I'd believed. As though all my connections were thin. As though I had always been preparing for exactly this moment — the moment when I turned away from everyone I had ever known and walked toward something I did not understand but could not refuse.

I did not say yes. The Mimics did not deal in words, and neither, in that moment, did I. I simply turned. I felt the patterns shift around me, rearranging, the geometry of the choice inscribing itself into the chamber's metallic sand. The bioluminescence pulsed once, deep and slow, an acknowledgment.

I walked toward the deeper tunnels, and the Mimics moved with me, and behind me the chamber sealed itself — not with a door, not with a mechanism, but with a rearrangement of matter that was as casual and total as breathing.

I did not look back. I want to say that I wanted to. I want to say that it cost me something, that I hesitated, that I carried the weight of what I was allowing to happen. And perhaps I did, somewhere beneath the calm surface of my thoughts, in some locked room of my mind where I kept the things I could not afford to feel.

But in that moment, walking deeper into whatever waited beneath the karst, I felt only the pull, and I followed it.

Behind me, above me, two hundred people continued their lives. Eating, sleeping, arguing, repairing storm damage, filing reports. My mother was probably looking for me. My father was probably working. Lao was probably wondering where I'd gone.

They had days. Maybe less.

I kept walking.

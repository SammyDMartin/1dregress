# Character Analysis: Colonel Hansun

## Sanderson Sliders

**Competence: 6/10**
Hansun is competent at his job — he follows orders, manages logistics, retrieves the survivor efficiently. But he's not exceptional. He couldn't crack Eszel in interrogation. He senses the truth but can't reach it. His competence is "reliable middle-manager" level, which is actually right for the story — he's the everyman surrounded by forces beyond his understanding.

**Likability: 7/10**
He's decent. He tries to be kind to a traumatized girl. He has physical vulnerability (the tremor). He's self-deprecating. But he's also a bit of a wet blanket — he worries, he defers, he doesn't push hard enough. The reader respects him without being captivated by him. This is fine for a frame character, but he needs at least one moment of real steel to avoid being merely passive.

**Proactivity: 4/10**
This is the problem. Hansun is reactive throughout. Aumonier sends him to the crater — he goes. Aumonier tells him to retrieve the survivor — he goes. He interrogates Eszel but she controls every exchange. He suspects but doesn't act on his suspicions. In the epilogue he broods. The one proactive thing he ever did was the suicidal burn in Threshold, and that was decades ago.

**What he needs**: At least one moment in the interrogation where Hansun takes control — catches Eszel off-guard, forces her to improvise, makes her genuinely scared of being caught. This would raise both his competence and proactivity scores, and make the interrogation scenes dramatically richer.

## Voice Analysis

### What's Distinctive
- Military formality laced with self-doubt
- The tremor — a recurring physical tell that externalizes his anxiety
- Interior thoughts marked by question marks: "Is he testing me?" "We're so far out of our depth. Why are we still here?"
- A habit of strategic deference: he says "Yes, sir" while privately disagreeing
- Plain, direct language — no metaphors, no poetry

### What's Not Distinctive Enough
- His dialogue with Aumonier bleeds together. Both men speak in the same register: clipped military briefing style. Hansun should be more tentative, more qualifyng; Aumonier should be more expansive, more politically aware.
- His interior monologue repeats the same notes: I'm scared, this is above my pay grade, something's wrong with the girl. Needs more variation. Show us what he's good at, what he cares about besides survival.
- The Oskar Nozoka detail is great — it humanizes him. We need more of this. What does he eat? What does he miss about home? One or two specific, concrete details that make him a person.

### Dialogue Issues

**Repetitive beat: worry/deference**
Too many exchanges follow this pattern:
- Aumonier says something bold/provocative
- Hansun privately disagrees
- Hansun says "Yes, sir" or asks a cautious question
- Aumonier overrides him

This happens at least four times in the crater scene. Cut two of them.

**Interrogation dialogue is too polite**
Hansun is a career military intelligence officer interrogating a suspect. Even accounting for her being a traumatized teenager, he's too gentle. The protein reprocessor scene is the model for what all the interrogation scenes should feel like — that's where he actually pushes and she has to work to deflect. The earlier scenes are fencing with padded swords.

**Missing: anger or frustration**
Hansun never gets angry. Not once. Not when Eszel calls him a liar, not when she stonewalls, not when nothing adds up. A man who survived the Battle of Threshold and carries a tremor from it should have a harder edge. Let him snap once — even briefly — and then pull himself back. That crack in the composure would be far more interesting than endless patience.

### Recommended Voice Fixes

1. **Give him one concrete personal detail per scene.** The tremor is good but it's the only one. What about: he touches his collar unconsciously when nervous. He has a specific way of sitting. He drinks something specific.

2. **Vary his interrogation approach.** Scene 1: polite, rapport-building. Scene 2: probing, more confident. Scene 3: confrontational — he's figured out the protein reprocessor lie and goes for the throat. Scene 4: exhausted, nearly giving up, which is when Eszel makes her one real mistake.

3. **Let him be right about something.** Currently Hansun suspects but never confirms. Give him one moment of clarity: he looks at her and knows, with certainty, that she let people die. He can't prove it. But he knows. This transforms him from a bumbling interrogator into a tragic figure — the man who saw the truth and couldn't do anything about it.

4. **Distinguish his dialogue from Aumonier's.** Hansun should use shorter sentences, more hedging language ("I think," "it seems like," "my guess would be"). Aumonier should use longer, more declarative sentences with political coloring ("Consider what this means for the balance of power").

## Arc

Threshold → The Listener: From desperate young officer who acts on instinct (the suicidal burn) to cautious older officer who knows when he's out of his depth. The arc is: the man who once charged in learns to suspect, to watch, to doubt — but that caution costs him. He can see the truth about Eszel but his institutional deference prevents him from acting on it. His arc is about the cost of becoming the kind of person who survives in a hierarchy.

The epilogue should drive this home: he KNOWS, he tells the Admiral, the Admiral doesn't care because the Other Moons matter more. Hansun's truth is irrelevant. That's his tragedy.

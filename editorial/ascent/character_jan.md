# Character Analysis: Jan

## Sanderson Sliders

**Competence: 5/10**
Jan is competent at survival — he knows the jungle, can fight, can use a weapon, can keep his head in a crisis. But he is fundamentally out of his depth in the political and technological world the novel throws him into. He doesn't understand warseeds, doesn't grasp the politics, can barely follow Aurelie's explanations. His competence is physical and instinctive, not intellectual. This is appropriate — he is the reader's ground-level surrogate, the ordinary person caught in extraordinary events.

**Likability: 9/10**
Jan is the novel's most sympathetic character by a wide margin. He is a desperate father driven by a love so fierce it overrides every other consideration. He steals food because he's starving. He joins the revolution because he has nothing left to lose. He bargains with the Dyn because he'd do anything to see his daughter again. He sacrifices himself because, in the end, the world matters more than his grief. Every decision he makes is driven by a recognizable, deeply human impulse.

**Proactivity: 6/10**
Jan is more proactive than Vash but less than Corbin. He drives his own storyline in the wasteland chapters, makes the active choice to join Christo, actively seeks out the Dyn on the crawler, actively bargains for Eva's life. But he is also swept along by other people's plans — Christo's revolution, Aurelie's mission, Vash's decision. His most proactive moment is his sacrifice in Chapter 22, which is also the most consequential action any character takes.

## The Emotional Heart

Jan is the character the reader feels for. His arc is pure: he loves his daughter, he lost her, and everything he does is an attempt to get her back or avenge her loss. This simplicity is the novel's greatest emotional asset. In a story crowded with politics, alien philosophy, and pre-invasion mysteries, Jan's motivation is elemental and immediately comprehensible.

### Where it works:
1. **The photograph.** Jan carries Eva's photo everywhere. When it blows off the crawler, he risks the Dyn to retrieve it. When Tuva tears it up, the reader feels it as a physical assault. This small, concrete object carries the entire weight of Jan's grief.

2. **"I KNOW HER" (Ch 10).** Three words written by an alien on the back of a photo. The implications destroy Jan's careful armor of resignation. The reader watches him go from "She's dead, I've accepted it" to "She might be alive, I'll do anything" in an instant.

3. **The flashback (Ch 18).** Eva in the stream, finding the organic lens, talking about false plants. Jan watching her play, knowing this happiness is fragile. The dream that always ends in fire. This is the novel's most tender scene, and it works because the text earns it through 17 chapters of Jan's suppressed grief.

4. **The sacrifice (Ch 22).** Jan leaps for the Dyn's weapon knowing he is choosing the world over his daughter. The vision of Eva telling him "Let me go" is devastating — whether it's real or a hallucination doesn't matter. What matters is that Jan acts. He gives Vash and Aurelie the seconds they need. He dies (or nearly dies) having made the hardest choice anyone in the novel is asked to make.

### Where it doesn't work:
1. **Jan's hostility to Vash (Ch 16-18) is repetitive.** "I've got nothing to say to you." "Stop digging." The reader understands Jan's anger, but it's expressed the same way each time. Vary the register — sometimes anger, sometimes curiosity, sometimes grudging respect.

2. **Jan's passivity in the submarine (Ch 16) is a problem.** He sits, he listens, he says nothing while Tuva and Pao argue. This is the section where Jan should be most engaged, because the mission is now his only hope of seeing Eva. Give him more moments of active participation — questions, suggestions, arguments.

3. **The conspiracy with the Dyn (Ch 21) happens too fast.** Jan goes from suspicion to complicity to betrayal in a single chapter. This needs more internal deliberation. The reader should feel the agony of Jan's choice — he is potentially damning the entire human race for the chance to save one girl. Show him wrestling with this more.

## From Thief to Revolutionary to Sacrifice

### Phase 1: The Thief (Chapters 1, 4)
Jan is a survival machine. He steals because he's hungry. He fights because he has to. He joins Christo not out of idealism but because "there didn't seem to be any alternative." His worldview is fundamentally cynical — the wasteland has taught him that the world is unfair and the powerful always win. Eva's loss has calcified this into permanent bitterness.

### Phase 2: The Revolutionary (Chapters 6-14)
Christo cracks Jan's cynicism. Not through argument but through example — Christo is a man who believes so fiercely that belief becomes contagious. Jan doesn't become a true believer, but he becomes willing to act as if he is one. His drinking and storytelling on the crawler (Ch 10) is the first time he's allowed himself community since losing Eva.

The Dyn's message ("I KNOW HER") derails this. Jan was becoming a revolutionary; now he's a father again, and the two identities are incompatible. The revolution requires sacrifice; fatherhood requires survival.

### Phase 3: The Father (Chapters 12, 18, 21)
Jan bargains with the Dyn. He is caught by Tuva. He shoots Tuva to free the Dyn. Every action is driven by a single imperative: find Eva. The moral compromises pile up — he betrays Christo's trust, he injures an ally, he collaborates with the enemy. The reader should understand why he does all of this while also recognizing that it's wrong.

### Phase 4: The Sacrifice (Chapter 22)
The vision of Eva (whether real or imagined by the warseed's neural manipulation) tells him to let her go. Jan makes the choice his entire arc has been building toward: he lunges for the Dyn's weapon, not to save Eva but to save the world his daughter might someday live in. The choice costs him everything.

This is the novel's emotional climax. It works because the reader has spent 21 chapters watching Jan refuse to give up on Eva, and then he does. Not because he stops loving her, but because he realizes that love sometimes requires letting go.

## Eva as Driving Force

Eva exists in the novel primarily as absence — she is the hole at the center of Jan's life that shapes everything he does. The reader learns about her through fragments: the photograph, Jan's memories, Christo's sympathetic nods, the flashback in Chapter 18.

### What we know:
- Intelligent, curious, scientific (she deduces things about Dyn plant biology as a child)
- Loved machines and learning
- Suffered from "Shadows" — night terrors involving presences in the sky
- Was taken by Arco after being tested, escaped, was recaptured during a massacre
- Was "melded" with K'txl as a "conscience" — her mind connected to an alien's

### What Jan knows:
- That she was taken
- That she might be alive (from the Dyn's message)
- That she is in orbit (from the Dyn in Ch 21)
- That firing the weapons will kill her

### The reader should feel:
Jan's grief should be specific, not generic. The flashback in Ch 18 does this brilliantly — Eva is not "the lost child" but a particular girl who says "No 'cos it's from the Dyn's home and not from our world" and "Miss Adisa says it because they don't have flowers or pollen." These details make her real, which makes her loss devastating.

## Voice Analysis

### What's Distinctive:
- Short, blunt sentences. "It's not. I was hungry."
- Wastelander dialect and register — less educated, more direct
- Physical sensations dominate his interior narration: hunger, cold, fear, the weight of a gun
- Humor is rare but effective: "It's a boat that sinks? It's actually meant to sink?"
- Emotions expressed through action, not reflection: he punches Pao rather than explaining why he's angry

### What Needs Work:
- Jan's interior monologue can be too sparse. In the early chapters, this is right — he's a man of action, not reflection. But in the submarine and warseed chapters, he needs more interiority. Show us what he makes of the Emissary, the old world, the impossible technology. He doesn't need to understand it all, but his emotional response to being confronted with the sublime should be richer.
- His dialogue with Aurelie is too minimal. They barely interact despite spending days in close quarters. At least one real conversation between them would deepen both characters.
- The "wastelander" dialect should be more consistent. Sometimes Jan sounds distinctly uneducated; other times he speaks as articulately as Vash. Settle on a register and hold it.

## Arc Summary

Thief -> Revolutionary -> Father -> Sacrifice -> (Survivor?)

Jan's arc is the novel's heart. He begins as a man reduced to his basest instincts (steal or starve), is elevated by Christo's vision, is torn between duty and love, and ultimately chooses duty. The final chapter leaves his fate ambiguous — he is rescued, barely alive, his last words a promise that he'll see Eva again. Whether this is hope or delusion is left to the reader.

Jan's arc is the inverse of Corbin's. Corbin begins with nothing and ends with everything, gaining power at the cost of his integrity. Jan begins with nothing and ends with nothing, losing everything material but gaining something Corbin never had: moral clarity.

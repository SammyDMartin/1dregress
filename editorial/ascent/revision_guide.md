# Ascent — Revision Procedures

## Core Principle

**Additive, not subtractive.** Every revision pass must result in a longer manuscript, not a shorter one. Do not cut original content unless it directly contradicts the story's internal logic. If something seems weak, expand and improve it — do not delete it.

## Before You Touch Anything

1. **Read the original source material** (`ascent_text.txt`) in full
2. **Understand the point** of each scene before rewriting it — what does it accomplish narratively? What does it reveal about the characters? What worldbuilding does it establish?
3. **Read the editorial materials** in `/editorial/ascent/` — character analyses, structural notes, chapter-by-chapter breakdown, themes and worldbuilding document
4. **Understand the three-POV structure**: Jan (wasteland/action), Vash (political/moral), Corbin (ambitious/political). Each POV has its own register, its own concerns, its own kind of sentence.

## Character Rules

### Vash
- **The moral center**: His principled nature should feel genuine, not preachy. He doesn't lecture — he acts according to his conscience and lets others draw conclusions.
- **Show, don't tell his goodness**: The body he covers on the road. The boy he warns. The chocolate for the driver's daughter. These small acts define him more than any speech.
- **He is NOT a saint**: He has been complicit in the occupation for two centuries. He scapegoated Meyer. He failed to act on the Broadcast for decades. His principles have always been constrained by pragmatism. Show the tension.
- **Physical tells**: The overcoat, the integrity tabs, the watch. These are his anchors. Use them consistently.
- **Interior monologue register**: Measured, formal, morally deliberative. Long sentences that weigh options. Occasional flashes of dry humor that disappear under the weight of responsibility.

### Corbin
- **Surface charm is essential**: Corbin must be likable on the page. He is witty, competent, and genuinely committed to ending the occupation. His darkness is revealed through ACTIONS (killing Meyer, poisoning guards, stealing credit) — NOT through evil monologues or cartoonish villainy.
- **He should be charming and competent on surface**: The reader should catch themselves rooting for Corbin and then feel uneasy about it. He delivers the novel's best one-liners. He is the smartest person in most rooms.
- **His confession of Meyer's murder (Ch 24) must be casual**: This is the point. He doesn't think it matters. It's a credential, not a confession. Do not add agonizing or second-guessing.
- **Never break his charm**: Even in his worst moments, Corbin should be charismatic. The horror comes from the gap between how he presents and what he does.
- **Interior monologue register**: Quick, strategic, always assessing. Short sentences when acting. Longer when strategizing. The habit of framing everything as a problem to be solved.

### Jan
- **Deeply sympathetic — the father's love drives everything**: Every decision Jan makes is filtered through "will this help me find Eva?" This is not a weakness; it is the most human thing in the novel.
- **He is not stupid**: Jan is uneducated, not unintelligent. His observation about Dyn plants being "maladapted" impresses Aurelie. His question about why the warseed needs human direction is the sharpest in the room. Show his native intelligence.
- **The grief must be specific**: Not "he missed his daughter" but the photograph, the dream of the stream, the torn paper scattered on shingle. Concrete details, not abstract emotions.
- **His sacrifice must be EARNED**: By Chapter 22, the reader must feel the full weight of what Jan gives up. This means every preceding Jan chapter must deepen the reader's investment in Eva's survival.
- **Interior monologue register**: Short, blunt, sensory. Physical sensations dominate — hunger, cold, pain, the weight of a weapon. Emotions expressed through action. Rare moments of reflection, which hit harder for being rare.

### The Dyn
- **The Dyn are alien but NOT cartoonishly evil**: They have their own logic about Lines and reproduction. The Broadcast shows horrors, but also shows a species trapped by biology. K'txl is genuinely trying to understand humanity. The Dyn heir wants to preserve its inheritance. These are not moustache-twirling villains.
- **The culling is horrifying AND logical from a Dyn perspective**: The novel's moral challenge is that the Dyn are right that intervention would destroy their way of life. What does it mean when the "right thing" requires the extinction of an alien culture? The text must not answer this question — it must force the reader to sit with it.
- **K'txl should feel genuinely alien**: Her social performance (the tea, the parasol, the offer of sex) should be unsettling because it's ALMOST right, not because it's wrong. She is trying very hard to bridge an unbridgeable gap.
- **The Dyn heir should feel dangerous but pitiable**: It is a prisoner, a hostage, a young being separated from its mother. It is also an alien that will casually kill Pao and Tuva. Both things are true.

### Aurelie
- **Preserve her mystery**: Do not over-explain who she was before the invasion. Her competence, her command of the warseed, her unsettling calm — these are features, not bugs.
- **She is not warm**: She is effective, focused, occasionally terrifying. Her threat to Tuva is genuine. Her near-execution of Jan is genuine. She is a pre-invasion survivor who has been planning this mission for two centuries, and she will not let anything derail it.
- **Her one moment of vulnerability is Christo's death**: "I'm sorry... I sold you a vision you'll never live to see." Protect this moment. It is the one crack in her armor.

### Christo
- **Theatrical but sincere**: His speeches sound rehearsed because they are. He knows he is playing a role. He doesn't care because he believes in the cause more than he believes in himself.
- **His death should feel like a loss**: By the time Christo dies, the reader should feel what the revolution has lost — not just a leader but a vision of what humanity could be.

## Scene-by-Scene Checklist

For each chapter revision:

- [ ] Have I read the original source material for this section?
- [ ] Is the revised version *longer* than the original?
- [ ] Does each POV character sound distinct from the others?
- [ ] Are the Dyn presented as alien but not cartoonishly evil?
- [ ] Is Corbin's charm maintained even in his darkest moments?
- [ ] Does Vash's moral weight feel genuine, not preachy?
- [ ] Is Jan's grief specific and concrete?
- [ ] Are all the original's specific details preserved (names, places, technical terms, worldbuilding)?
- [ ] Does the prose improvement justify any changes from the original wording?
- [ ] Does the chapter flow naturally from the previous one?
- [ ] Have I understood each scene's PURPOSE before revising?

## What "Additive" Means in Practice

- **Restore cut content**: If previous revisions removed scenes, dialogue, worldbuilding, or character moments from the original, put them back
- **Expand thin sections**: Where the original was sparse, add texture — sensory detail, character thought, environmental description
- **Keep prose improvements**: Good line-level rewriting from previous passes should be preserved. The goal is to combine the original's completeness with improved prose
- **Add new material only where gaps exist**: If a transition feels abrupt or a character's motivation is unclear, add bridging material
- **Deepen interiority**: The original often implies thought where a revision can make it explicit. What does Jan feel when he sees the Dyn write on his photo? What does Vash think when Corbin confesses to murder? These moments deserve expansion.

## What NOT to Do

- Do not cut scenes because they seem "slow" or "unnecessary" — they are doing narrative work
- Do not make Corbin overtly villainous — his darkness is in what he does, not how he sounds
- Do not make Vash preachy — his morality is shown through action, not speeches
- Do not simplify the Dyn into monsters — their perspective must remain coherent even when horrifying
- Do not compress dialogue — let conversations breathe
- Do not remove worldbuilding details (Arco, conurbations, the Utilitaria, warseeds, Other Moon, the Broadcast, integrity tabs, Enforcers, the Hollow Tower, false life, reservations, etc.)
- Do not summarize where the original dramatizes
- Do not add meta-commentary about the story's themes
- Do not resolve the moral ambiguity — the novel's power comes from the unresolved tension between species
- Do not flatten Jan's dialect into standard prose — preserve his distinct register
- Do not give Aurelie's backstory more detail than the original provides — mystery is intentional

## Worldbuilding Preservation

The following terms and details must be maintained exactly as they appear:

- **Arco**: The human administration governing Earth under Dyn occupation
- **Conurbations**: Numbered mega-cities (Conurbation One, Fifteen, etc.)
- **The Hollow Tower**: Arco's administrative headquarters
- **Enforcers**: Arco's military/police force
- **Internal Security (InSec)**: Arco's intelligence/secret police branch
- **Directors/Directorate**: Regional administrators within Arco
- **The Ambassador**: Human intermediary between Arco and the Dyn
- **Integrity**: A drug Vash takes with his coffee (truth/morality enhancer?)
- **The Utilitaria**: Pre-invasion AI superintelligence that governed human civilization
- **Warseeds / Dragon's Teeth**: Self-replicating weapon systems from the pre-invasion era
- **The Other Moon**: The Dyn's origin vessel, a blue-glowing object that tracks Earth's moon
- **The Broadcast**: Dyn-compiled footage of their own civilization's history
- **False life / false jungle**: Dyn-origin plant life colonizing Earth's equatorial regions
- **Reservations**: Walled enclosures where Dyn live on Earth's surface
- **The Ascensor**: The severed orbital tether
- **Dotbow**: A projectile weapon used in the wasteland
- **Lines**: Dyn social/reproductive unit — the line is the body, the individual is a cell
- **K'txl / Liar to Animals**: The Dyn matriarch who rules Earth's occupation
- **The Emissary (39th)**: The warseed's avatar/spokesperson
- **The Firmament**: The constellation of Dyn ships and asteroids in orbit
- **Housecube stacks**: Standard Arco housing
- **Arcologies**: Large-scale enclosed urban structures
- **Ichor infusions**: Whatever keeps Vash alive across centuries

## Revision Workflow

1. Read the original source for the target chapter
2. Read the current draft version (if one exists)
3. Read relevant editorial notes (character analyses, structural analysis, chapter-by-chapter)
4. Identify the chapter's PURPOSE: what it establishes, what it reveals, how it moves the plot
5. Revise according to the rules above
6. Verify word count is equal to or greater than the previous version
7. Check that all worldbuilding details are preserved
8. Verify POV voice consistency
9. Commit with a clear message describing what was changed

## Special Attention Required

### The Corbin Problem
Corbin is the novel's most difficult character to revise because the temptation to make him overtly sinister is strong. Resist it. Every revision of a Corbin scene should pass the "charm test" — would a casual reader still like this guy? If the answer is no, you've pushed too far. His darkness must be structural, not behavioral.

### The Dyn Problem
The Dyn must be simultaneously terrifying and sympathetic. The Broadcast shows unspeakable horror AND the tragedy of a species trapped by its own biology. K'txl is both an oppressor and a being genuinely trying to find a way forward. The Dyn heir is both a dangerous alien and a child separated from its mother. Every Dyn scene should hold both truths simultaneously.

### The Exposition Problem
The warseed chapters (16, 19) contain dense exposition about pre-invasion technology, the Utilitaria's contingency plan, and the physics of the weapon system. This exposition is necessary but must be made DRAMATIC. The information should feel like revelation, not lecture. Characters should react emotionally to what they learn. Arguments should emerge from the information. Tension should be maintained.

### The Finale Problem
Chapter 24 resolves too much too quickly. Corbin's broadcast, Vash's rescue, the Meyer confession, the partnership offer — all in one chapter. If expanding, give each major beat its own space. The Meyer confession especially needs room to land. Vash's silence in response should be deafening.

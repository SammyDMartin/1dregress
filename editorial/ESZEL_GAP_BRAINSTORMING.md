**Borrow from Draft 1:**

## GAP B: Arrival at Kailash and Base Life

### Where It Goes
After Section 2 (the elevator excursion, ending at line 867 "It didn't matter. She wasn't afraid") and Section 3's brief interrogation (lines 868-875), we need:

1. **An interrogation bridge** — Hansun shifts to asking about life on Kailash
2. **Arrival at Kailash** — Eszel sees the planet for the first time
3. **Base life** — The dullness, the routine, establishing characters who will die
4. **First Mimic encounter** — Eszel breaks rules, meets one, discovers she's different

### Scene B1: Arrival (Eszel POV, first-person flashback)

The partial draft at lines 877-907 provides raw material. Eszel and her father watch from the Resolute's observation cupola. The landers descend. She sees the matter-streams between pits and recognizes them as trade. The whisper: "just imagine what's beneath that surface."

**What to add/develop:**
- The revival from cryosleep — disorientation, decades having passed
- Kailash from orbit: the rust plains, the karst, the thin atmosphere glowing at the terminator
- Her father's excitement vs her detachment
- The descent to the surface — thin air, the weight of a lighter gravity, the alien sky
- First impression of the base site — how provisional and fragile it all looks against the landscape

**Tone**: Wonder mixed with alienation. Eszel feels something the others don't — that they're trespassing.

### Scene B2: Base Life (Eszel POV, first-person flashback)

The author's note says "boring base life scene." This should be:
- Mundane routines: meals, lessons, recycled air, the hum of life support
- Eszel's parents: her father the plasma physicist (this is established later), her mother (unnamed, but argumentative per the earlier scene)
- Other young people: the base has civilian families. Lao is here. Maybe others from the ship.
- The scientists: working on various projects, including protein reprocessors and pit-light tube physics
- The Mimics: glimpsed at a distance, "like midgets in greatcoats," going about incomprehensible business in the karst. Communication attempts failing.
- Eszel's growing restlessness: the same crushing boredom as the ship, but now mixed with that pull
- The storm: a radiation event or seismic event that damages the base and disrupts operations (this is referenced later as "three days after the storm")

**Key character work**: Show people who will die. Her father explaining physics. Her mother worrying. Make the reader care about them so the coming destruction has weight.

**What NOT to do**: Don't make the base life too long. Two or three scenes at most. The existing text moves quickly and this should too.

## GAP C: The Mimics Communicate with Eszel (Two Scenes)

This is the critical missing content. The author's notes are explicit: two sections.

### Scene C1: First Contact (Eszel POV, first-person flashback)

**Setup**: Eszel breaks the rules again (it's her pattern — the balcony, the elevator, now this). She goes outside the base perimeter, into the karst landscape.

**Why she goes**: The pull. The same impulse that had her leaning over the balcony and descending in the elevator. Something is calling her, and it's coming from the pits.

**What happens**:
- She navigates the karst — narrow canyons, bioluminescent life in the cracks, the eerie silence
- She encounters a Mimic. They're small, inscrutable, wrapped in their organic "greatcoat" exoskeletons
- The communication is not verbal at first. It's perceptual — patterns, the same kind of geometries she glimpsed in the void between stars. The Mimic doesn't "speak" so much as she suddenly understands.
- The revelation: *they know she's different*. They've been watching her since arrival. They can sense the Listener's touch on her.
- Wonder and excitement — this is first contact as it should be. Not the stilted failures of the science teams, but a genuine connection.
- She returns to base, tells no one. Because who would believe her? And because some part of her knows this is hers alone.

**Tone**: Wonder, exhilaration, and the first real taste of something that could make her feel alive. This should be the most emotionally positive scene in the book.

**The whisper**: Clearer now. Not words exactly, but patterns that resolve into meaning: *you are known*.

### Scene C2: The Choice (Eszel POV, first-person flashback)

**Setup**: She goes back. Again and again maybe, but this scene is the decisive one.

**What happens**:
- The Mimics show her something. The pits, their network of tunnels, the deeper structures beneath. Perhaps they take her underground — down into the karst, into the alien infrastructure.
- She sees the true Kailash: not a dying world but a sleeping machine. The matter-streams aren't trade — they're maintenance. The Mimics aren't a primitive civilization — they're caretakers.
- The Mimics communicate the danger: the humans are disturbing something. Their vacuum energy drives, their drilling, their electromagnetic noise. The Whole has noticed.
- **The ultimatum**: Not cruel, not malicious. The Mimics (or the Whole through them) present a simple reality. The humans must leave, or the Whole will remove them. There is no negotiation. There is no middle ground.
- Eszel understands that she could warn the base. She could run back and tell everyone.
- But she also understands that it wouldn't matter. They wouldn't believe her. They wouldn't leave. Aumonier would want to stay and study the technology. The military would see it as a threat to be confronted.
- And somewhere deeper than logic: *she doesn't want to warn them*. She wants what lies ahead, not what lies behind.
- The Mimics offer her a path: leave, and you will be shown what you are meant to see. Stay, and you die with them.
- She says yes. Not in words, but in the way she turns her back and walks toward the door.

**The moral complexity**: She doesn't order the destruction. She doesn't push a button. She simply... leaves. Knowing. And there's no way to tell if she could have changed anything. The author's note: "She's shocked of course, she didn't exactly plan for them to die... she feels like on some level maybe she knew, and we see the lack of grief, but we do see fear and shock and a sense that she did this."

**Tone**: Inevitability. Quiet horror. Not a villain's decision but a prophet's — or a sociopath's.

## GAP D: The Disaster

### Where It Goes
After Eszel leaves the base (existing Section 4, her departure and trek). Currently the trek just... continues, and the base is already destroyed when the narrative catches up.

The disaster scene should come during the first night of her trek, or within the first day. She's crossed the perimeter, she's in the wilderness, and then it happens behind her.

### What It Looks Like

- She feels it before she sees it. A tremor in the ground, then something else — a presence, an awareness, like the Whole inhaling.
- She turns and looks back. In the distance, where the base should be, the sky goes wrong. Not an explosion — something quieter and more terrible. The air shimmers. The stars behind the base waver.
- Then light. Not fire but raw energy, the flash of matter being restructured. The gravity weapon.
- The base is simply... gone. Flattened into that lenticular pool of matter that Hansun will later find.
- Then the sky above goes wrong too. The Resolute, in orbit, flares and dies.
- Eszel watches. She feels shock, fear, grief — real grief, hitting her for the first time, her parents, Lao, everyone. She falls to her knees.
- But she doesn't turn back. There's nothing to turn back to.
- And underneath the grief, the whisper: *keep going*.

**Tone**: The most emotionally raw scene in the book. This is where the reader should be horrified not just by what happened but by Eszel's response — that she grieving but also, on some level, relieved. That she's horrified by her own lack of horror.

### Length
Short. Maybe 400-600 words. The power is in restraint — describe what she sees, what she feels, and what she doesn't feel. Let the reader fill in the rest.

## GAP E: Expanding the Listener Scene

The existing Listener scene (lines 1026-1117) is strong and mostly complete. Minor additions:

- **Connect to the Mimics**: The Listener could acknowledge the Mimics' role: "They prepared you. Good." Or: "You've already begun to see." This links the two halves.
- **The Whole**: The Listener could explain (obliquely) what the Whole is — or at least that it exists, that it decided, and that Eszel was the one variable it didn't control.
- **Eszel's complicity**: When the Listener shows her "everything" (the burning light-webs), she could see, within that infinite complexity, the moment of the base's destruction. Not as a horror but as a note in a symphony. This drives home that to the Listener, 200 deaths are a rounding error.

These are minor additions within the existing scene — a few extra paragraphs at most.

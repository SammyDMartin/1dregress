# Character Analysis: Admiral Aumonier

## Sanderson Sliders

**Competence: 8/10**
Aumonier is sharp. He identifies the gravity weapon's mechanism, connects the coronal mass ejections to the attacks, discovers the Other Moons, and grasps their strategic implications immediately. He's the smartest person in the room and knows it. His competence is real — but it's also what makes him dangerous, because his intelligence serves his ambition.

**Likability: 4/10**
He's charismatic but not warm. The "Zapp Brannigan" comparison from the author's notes is apt — he's an inspirational leader who's also reckless, brusk, and willing to sacrifice people for strategic advantage. He sent the expedition to Kailash despite the risk. He presided over the disaster. He's now focused not on the dead but on the technology. There's something chilling about him, and that's correct.

**Proactivity: 9/10**
He drives everything. He decides to come to Kailash. He deploys Hansun. He orders the evacuation. He discovers the Other Moons. He's the prime mover. Every major plot event flows from his decisions.

## Voice Analysis

### What's Distinctive
- Expansive, politically aware speech
- Uses rhetorical questions to control conversations: "You doubt that this is worth it?"
- Jumps between technical analysis and grand strategic vision in the same breath
- Has a "hawklike face" — sharp, predatory
- The "I'm not known for my sense of humour" line — dry, commanding, a man who knows his own reputation

### What's Not Distinctive Enough
- His dialogue sometimes sounds like a textbook briefing rather than a person speaking. Lines like "Abnormally large coronal mass ejections" and "magnetically flux lines pinching" are accurate but don't sound like spoken language, even for a technically literate admiral.
- He and Hansun are too interchangeable in the crater scene. Both are doing exposition. Aumonier should be doing interpretation — what does this mean, what do we do about it — while Hansun does observation — what am I seeing, what happened here.
- His blowhard/reckless quality is told more than shown. We hear that he "decided to come here despite the ludicrous risk" but we don't see that decision, or its cost. The epilogue Other Moons scene is where his ambition crystallizes, but it should echo through the whole story.

### Dialogue Issues

**Too much shared exposition**
In the crater scene, both Hansun and Aumonier are explaining the disaster to each other. But Aumonier already knows everything — he's briefing Hansun. The scene should be structured as a briefing, not a discussion. Aumonier tells, Hansun reacts. This would sharpen both voices.

**The "Arco goons" line is the best thing he says**
"Those godforsaken Arco goons can't fly rings around us if we discover an authentic gravitonic drive. Imagine if we'd had something like that at Threshold." This is perfect Aumonier: competitive, strategic, slightly vulgar, referencing a disaster as a missed opportunity. Every line should have this energy.

**Missing: warmth that he uses as a weapon**
Aumonier should have moments of apparent warmth — "How are you holding up, Hansun?" — that are actually strategic. He reads people. He manages them. His warmth is a tool. This would distinguish him from a generic stern admiral and make the "Zapp Brannigan" comparison land: he's inspiring not because he's good, but because he's magnetic.

### Recommended Voice Fixes

1. **Make his technical language more colloquial.** Not "abnormally large coronal mass ejections" but "the star basically sneezed at us, twice, right before each event." He's a commander, not a scientist — he translates.

2. **Give him a verbal tic or habit.** He trails off mid-thought ("Yes, of course..." he trailed off) — this is good but needs to be more deliberate. Maybe he starts sentences with the conclusion and backtracks to the reasoning. A man who thinks faster than he speaks.

3. **Show his recklessness through Hansun's fear.** When Aumonier says "imagine if we'd had something like that at Threshold," Hansun should feel cold. The Admiral is already planning how to weaponize alien technology while standing on the grave of 200 people. Let Hansun's reaction carry the moral weight.

4. **The epilogue should reveal him fully.** "With ten, there would be no limits to our power" — this is the mask dropping. Build toward it. In earlier scenes, Aumonier should talk about "understanding" and "learning." In the epilogue, the language shifts to "power" and "weapon." Same man, different register, because now he has what he came for.

## Arc

Aumonier doesn't change. He's the same man at the beginning and end: ambitious, brilliant, dangerous. His arc is one of revelation — we gradually see what he really is. The crater scene: concerned leader. The retrieval: efficient commander. The epilogue: a man intoxicated by the prospect of unlimited power. He's the human mirror to the Whole — an intelligence that sees 200 deaths as an acceptable cost.

The chilling parallel: Eszel and Aumonier both look at the disaster and think about what they gained, not what was lost. They're more alike than either would admit.
